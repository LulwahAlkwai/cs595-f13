<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt; text-indent: -18pt;">&nbsp;</span><span style="text-indent: -18pt;">Come up with an idea for a study. Don't sweat it. It's not as hard as it looks. All you need to do is take an idiomatic
expression and run with it.</span><span style="text-indent: -18pt;">&nbsp;Here we go: </span><i style="text-indent: -18pt;">the

<!--[if !supportLists]-->2.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp; &nbsp; </span>&nbsp;Create a theoretical background. Surely there is
some philosopher (preferably a Greek one) who has said something remotely
relevant about optimists and pessimists while staring at a wine glass. Include
him. For extra flavor you might want to add an anthropologist or a
sociologist into the mix; Google is your friend here. Top it off with a few
social psychology references. There, you have your theoretical framework. That
wasn&#8217;t so hard, was it?<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->3.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Think of a manipulation. Again, this is nothing to get
nervous about. All you need to do is take the expression literally. Imagine this scenario. The subject is in a room. In the glass-full
condition, a confederate comes in with an empty glass and a bottle of water.
She then pours the glass half full and leaves the room. In the glass-half-empty
condition, she comes in with a full glass and a bottle. She then pours half the
glass back into the bottle and leaves. <o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->4.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Think of a dependent measure. This is where the
fun begins. As you may know, the dependent measure of choice in social priming
research is candy. You simply cannot go wrong with candy! So let&#8217;s say the
subjects get to choose ten pieces of differently colored pieces of candy from
a container that has equal numbers of orange and brown M&amp;Ms. Your
prediction here is that people in the half-full condition will be more likely
to pick the cheery orange M&amp;Ms than those in the half-empty condition, who
will tend to prefer the gloomy brown ones.<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->5.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Get a sample. You don&#8217;t want to overdo it here. About 30 students from a nondescript university will do nicely. <i>Only 30 in a between-subjects design?,</i> you worry. Worry no more.
This is how we roll in social priming.<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->6.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Run Experiment 1. Don&#8217;t fuss about issues
like the age and gender of the subjects and details of the procedure; you won't
be reporting them anyway.<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->7.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Analyze the results. Normally, you&#8217;d worry that
you might not find an effect. But this is social priming remember? You are
guaranteed to find an effect. In fact, your effect size will be around .8.
That&#8217;s social priming for you!<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->8.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Now on to Experiment 2. Come up with a new
manipulation. <i>What&#8217;s wrong with the glass
and bottle from Experiment 1?</i>, you might wonder. Are you kidding? This is
social priming research. You need a new dependent measure. Just let your imagination
run wild. How about balloons? In the half-full condition, the confederate walks
in with an inflated balloon and lets half the air out in front of the subject.
In the half empty condition, she half-inflates a balloon. And bingo! You&#8217;re
done (careful with the word bingo, by the way; it makes people walk real slow).<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->9.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span><!--[endif]-->Think of a new dependent measure. Why not have
the subjects list their favorite TV shows? Your prediction here is that the
half-full condition will list more sitcoms like <i>Seinfeld</i> and <i>Big Bang Theory</i>
than the half-empty condition, which will list more crime shows like <i>CSI</i> and <i>Law &amp; Order </i>(or maybe one of those stupid vampire shows). You
could also include a second dependent measure. How about having subjects
indicate how much they identify with <i>Winnie
de Pooh</i> characters? Your prediction here is obvious: the half full
condition will identify with Tigger the most while the half empty condition
will prefer Eeyore by a landslide. <o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->10.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;"> </span><!--[endif]-->Repeat
steps 5-7.<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->11.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;"> </span><!--[endif]-->Now
you are ready to write your General Discussion. You want to discuss the
implications of your research. Don&#8217;t be shy here. Talk about the major
implications for business, health, education, and politics this research so
evidently has.<o:p></o:p></div>
<div class="MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle" style="mso-list: l0 level1 lfo1; text-indent: -18.0pt;">
<!--[if !supportLists]-->12.<span style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: 7pt;">&nbsp;</span><span style="text-indent: -18pt;">For garnish, add a quirky celebrity quote. Don&#8217;t
work yourself into a lather. Just go to </span><a href="http://www.goodreads.com/" style="text-indent: -18pt;">www.goodreads.com</a><span style="text-indent: -18pt;">
to find a quote. Here, I already did the work for you: </span><span style="background-color: white; background-position: initial initial; background-repeat: initial initial; color: #181818; text-indent: -18pt;">&#8220;Some people see the glass half full. Others see it half
empty.</span><span style="color: #181818; text-indent: -18pt;"> <span style="background: white;">I see a glass
that's twice as big as it needs to be.&#8221;<span class="apple-converted-space">&nbsp;</span>&#8213;<span class="apple-converted-space">&nbsp;</span></span></span><a href="http://www.goodreads.com/author/show/22782.George_Carlin" style="text-indent: -18pt;"><span style="background: white; color: #666600; mso-bidi-font-family: &quot;Times New Roman&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: &quot;Times New Roman&quot;; text-decoration: none; text-underline: none;">George
