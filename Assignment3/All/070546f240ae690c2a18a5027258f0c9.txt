<section class='article-content'>

<p>How do you book your online travel? On a site like <a href="http://mashable.com/category/travelocity/">Travelocity</a>, <a href="http://mashable.com/category/expedia/">Expedia</a> or <a href="http://mashable.com/category/orbitz/">Orbitz</a>? If you live in the United States, you most likely don't use Skyscanner, a global travel booking company with roots in Europe. Only now is the site expanding its footprint into the U.S.</p>
<p>The 300-person company recently acquired Fogg, a Barcelona-based hotel search company, poising it to make a bigger splash in the online travel space.</p>
<div class="see-also"><p>See also: <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/08/01/twitter-hotel/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Twitter Hotel Redefines the Social Media Vacation</a></p></div>
<p>“Our users are going to love hotel search as a result of this acquisition," said Skyscanner CEO and co-founder Gareth Williams in a press release. "We built the best flight search product anywhere in the world. We then expanded that offering into car rental. And today we’ve taken a big step towards completing the picture in the space of hotel search.”</p>
<p>I sat down with Williams just as his company announced the Fogg deal. Skyscanner, which boasts direct connections to 300 airlines and 400 online travel agencies, hopes to provide the same kind of direct connections in the hotel space. The goal here is growth — especially, it seems, in the U.S. </p>
<h2>How Big is the Pie?</h2>
<p>Travel is a hot space and a multi-billion-dollar industry. According to the research firm <a href="http://www.newmediatrendwatch.com/markets-by-country/17-usa/126-online-travel-market?start=1" target="_blank">eMarketer</a>, digital travel sales will top $136 billion by the end of this year and are projected to grow at least another 20% over the next five years.</p>
<p>Spotting this opportunity, major internet players like <a href="http://mashable.com/category/google/">Google</a> and <a href="http://mashable.com/category/microsoft/">Microsoft</a> have purchased search technology companies like <a href="http://mashable.com/2009/09/19/bing-extras/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Farecast</a> (Bing) and <a href="http://mashable.com/2010/07/01/google-ita/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">ITA</a> (Google) to shore up travel-related search results. Companies like <a href="http://mashable.com/category/priceline/">Priceline</a>, Orbitz, <a href="http://www.cheapoair.com/" target="_blank">Cheapoair</a> and Travelocity spend millions on television advertising and marketing.</p>
<p><span class="microcontent" data-fragment="skyscanner-runs-no-tv" data-description="Skyscanner runs no TV ads and is not a household name, but has found some success." data-micro="1">Skyscanner runs no TV ads and is not a household name, but has found some success.</span> According to Williams, Skyscanner has 24 million unique monthly visitors and 25 million installed apps. It generates roughly one-third of its traffic through mobile (not counting mobile web searches) and expects to post $100 million in revenues this year. It also has 50% repeat business, which is good news as long as the service grows month over month with new customers.</p>
<p><img src="http://rack.3.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDEzLzA5LzEzLzdjL3NreXNjYW5uZXJwLjg5YzQwLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/267d3966/bfc/skyscannerpage.jpg" alt="skyscannerpage" class="" data-width="1057" data-fragment="m!95c3" data-image="http://rack.3.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDEzLzA5LzEzLzdjL3NreXNjYW5uZXJwLjg5YzQwLmpwZwpwCXRodW1iCTEyMDB4OTYwMD4/267d3966/bfc/skyscannerpage.jpg" data-micro="1"></p>
<p>One reason Skyscanner execs believe they draw in so many repeat customers is price. Customers get the travel price set by the airlines, so they don’t have to pay an extra percentage to Skyscanner. Williams says his company makes money through referrals and, sometimes, a percentage of the booking fee (though that cost is not added on and charged to the customer).</p>
<p>When I noted that travel searches on Skyscanner did not result in significantly lower fares than my findings on comparable services like Kayak, Skyscanner representatives acknowledged that the greatest savings usually come from international travel. (Even there, though, the differences were not great.) A Skyscanner representative explained in an email, "It’s also about more than price — inspirational and functional travel both require a balance of price, convenience of time and preferred airline. The comprehensive coverage we offer is really important in that it gives customers greater choice in all of these areas."</p>
<p>For Williams, there is no magic to finding the best travel fares. The tips people offer — only book on Tuesdays, wait until midnight — are mostly myths, according to Williams.</p>
<p>“People think it’s like book search,” he said, referring to an index search using keywords and numbers. The reality is that travel search is hard, real-time, distributed, live-data-centric work. One query can yield 1,000 results. Travel startups have a tough time and a much higher failure rate than other startups because the data is so hard to reach, parse and deliver, Williams said. "Our job is to make it appear simple.”</p>
<h2>Plenty of Challenges</h2>
<p>Simplifying travel search is only part of Skyscanner's challenge as it tries to break into the lucrative U.S. travel market. Unlike some other mega travel sites, Skyscanner doesn't let you book directly. </p>
<p>According to Rafat Ali, CEO and founder of travel intelligence startup <a href="http://skift.com/" target="_blank">Skift</a>, that makes Skyscanner more akin to the better-known travel metasearch engine Kayak. Metasearch engines are defined as sites that do not handle transactions; those that do, like Priceline, are known as online travel agencies or OTAs. </p>
<p>Now, though, some crucial differences exist. </p>
<p>“Now that Kayak is owned by Priceline and many times bigger than Skyscanner, [Skyscanner] will have a tough time competing against the giants,” Ali wrote in an email. “Chances of global domination are tiny, but there are profitable niches in online travel booking, mostly regional … Skyscanner would be smart to focus on its traditional stronghold of Europe.”</p>
<p>Marcello Gasdia, consumer behavior analyst with the market research firm <a href="http://www.phocuswright.com/" target="_blank">PhoCusWright</a>, was also skeptical. </p>
<p>“There’s a reason why Skyscanner performs well in Europe and why they’ll have a really tough time in U.S.,” Gasdia told me. </p>
<p><span class="microcontent" data-fragment="metasearch-services-like-skyscanner" data-description="Metasearch services like Skyscanner are thriving right now in Europe’s challenging economic conditions" data-micro="1">Metasearch services like Skyscanner are thriving right now in Europe’s challenging economic conditions</span>, Gasdia explained.  Additionally, an incredibly fragmented online travel market like Europe’s demands a simple solution like Skyscanner. In the U.S., where the economy is bouncing back and the market is dominated by big travel companies and hotel chains, OTAs and metasearch engines like Kayak — which partner with or are owned by huge OTA parents — rule. Even the airline industry is seeing more consolidation (for example, take American Airlines and U.S. Airways).</p>
<p>Gasdia acknowledges that, at least for consumers, the line between OTAs and metasearch services is blurring. But he still thinks Skyscanner will have its work cut out in the U.S., where “Priceline and Expedia own a very large part of the online travel space.”</p>
<p>To win in the U.S., Skyscanner must deliver travel results as good (or better) than the competition and overcome the significant market hurdles outlined by Gasdia and Ali — or someone could simply buy them. </p>
<p>When I asked about the possibility of an acquisution-hungry company like <a href="http://mashable.com/category/yahoo/">Yahoo</a> snapping it up, Williams laughed. </p>
<p>"[Skyscanner has] at least a times-10 to achieve before that would happen,” he said, meaning he wants to scale the business by a factor of 10. Surprisingly, Williams says that at its current rate of growth, Skyscanner could achieve that goal in three years. The person responsible for helping Skyscanner make this goal a reality is Chief Marketing Officer Andrew Cocker, who worked at <a href="http://mashable.com/category/yahoo/">Yahoo</a> pre-<a href="http://mashable.com/category/marissa-mayer/">Marissa Mayer</a>.</p>
<p>During our meeting, Cocker noted that Skyscanner is particularly good at finding travel destinations during a certain period that fit within your budget.</p>
<p>Skyscanner is not on the market and Williams would rather run independently and serve as the "most trusted online travel company in the world." The Fogg acquisition is one step, but it sounds like the stairway to global (and more importantly) U.S.-based online travel success is quite high and steep.</p>
