			<img width="580" height="215" src="http://hbrblogs.files.wordpress.com/2012/09/2012102_41.jpg?w=580" class="pageFeatureImage wp-post-image" alt="2012102_41" />		<h1 id="articleTitle">How to Respond to&nbsp;Negativity</h1>
	<p class="byline">
		by Peter Bregman&nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;
		9:30 AM September 28, 2012	</p>
</div>
<!-- pageFeature end --><!-- shareWidgetTop -->
<div id="shareWidgetTop">
	<ul class="social-links">
		<li><a href="http://blogs.hbr.org/2012/09/how-to-respond-to-negativity/#disqus_thread" class="social-comments" data-disqus-identifier="http://blogs.hbr.org/bregman/2012/09/how-to-respond-to-negativity.html">Comments</a></li>
		<li class="gShare"><a href="javascript:window.print();" class="social-print">&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;</a></li>
		<li class="gShare"><div id="shareTopGigya"></div></li>
	</ul>
</div>
<!-- shareWidgetTop -->				<div id="articleBody">
					<p>&#8220;I&#8217;m getting to the end of my patience,&#8221; Dan,* the head of sales for a financial services firm, told me. &#8220;There is so much opportunity here &#8212; the business is growing, the work is interesting, and bonuses should be pretty good this year &#8212; but all I hear is complaining.&#8221;</p>
<p>When he passed his employees in the hall and asked how it was going, they would respond with a critical comment about a client or they would grumble about the amount of work they were juggling.</p>
<p>&#8220;How can I turn around the negativity that pervades my team?&#8221; he asked me.</p>
<p>I asked him what he was doing now. &#8220;At first, I told them how much opportunity we had in front of us, and I reiterated our mission statement,&#8221; he said. &#8220;I wanted to remind them what we&#8217;re all working towards. Now though?&#8221; he threw his hands up in the air, &#8220;I&#8217;m just pissed. I want to shake them out of their slump.&#8221;</p>
<p>Dan&#8217;s response is completely natural and intuitive. Unfortunately, it&#8217;s also completely ineffective.</p>
<p>Initially, he tried to counter the negativity with positivity. When that didn&#8217;t work, he became negative himself. Both responses reaped the same outcome: More negativity.</p>
<p>Here&#8217;s why: Countering someone&#8217;s negativity with your positivity doesn&#8217;t work because it&#8217;s <em>argumentative</em>. People don&#8217;t like to be emotionally contradicted and if you try to convince them that they shouldn&#8217;t feel something, they&#8217;ll only feel it more stubbornly. And if you&#8217;re a leader trying to be positive, it comes off even worse because you&#8217;ll appear out of touch and aloof to the reality that people are experiencing.</p>
<p>The other instinctive approach &#8212; confronting someone&#8217;s negativity with your own negativity &#8212; doesn&#8217;t work because it&#8217;s <em>additive</em>. Your negative reaction to their negative reaction simply adds fuel to the fire. Negativity breeds negativity.</p>
<p>So how can you turn around negativity?</p>
<p>I discovered the answer when I made Dan&#8217;s mistake with my wife Eleanor, when she was complaining about our kids fighting. At first I tried to convince her that all kids fight and ours weren&#8217;t so bad. Then I became frustrated with her complaining and told her as much.</p>
<p>She got angry. Who wouldn&#8217;t? But then she did something really helpful to me: She told me what she needed from me.</p>
<p>&#8220;I don&#8217;t want to feel that I&#8217;m alone in this,&#8221; she said, &#8220;I want to know you understand. I want you to tell me that we&#8217;re in this together. And if you share my frustrations, I want to know that too.&#8221;</p>
<p>In fact, I did share her frustrations, but I was trying not to be negative &#8211; which, of course, made the whole interaction more negative.</p>
<p>After my conversation with Eleanor, I had a surprising insight: You don&#8217;t need to change your response. You just need to redirect it.</p>
<p>What Dan had done with his employees is respond negatively <em>against</em> them (&#8220;I want to shake them out of their slump&#8221;) and positively <em>against</em> them (&#8220;I told them how much opportunity we had in front of us&#8221;).</p>
<p>But a much more productive response is to respond negatively <em>with</em> others and positively <em>with</em> them.</p>
<p>Here&#8217;s what I&#8217;m suggesting, translated into a three-step process for effectively turning around negative people:</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>1.  Understand how they feel and validate it. </strong>This might be hard because it could feel like you&#8217;re reinforcing their negative feelings. But you&#8217;re not. You&#8217;re not agreeing with them or justifying their negativity. You&#8217;re simply showing them that you understand how they feel.  </p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p><strong>2.  Find a place to agree with them. </strong>You don&#8217;t have to agree with everything they&#8217;ve said, but, if you can, agree with some of what they&#8217;re feeling. If you share some of their frustrations, let them know which.  </p></blockquote>
<p>During steps 1 and 2 you are responding negatively with others not against them. This relaxes and opens them. It helps them feel that they are not alone and you are not out of touch.</p>
<p>Instead of telling Eleanor she shouldn&#8217;t be so negative about our children, I told her that I shared her frustrations about their fighting. That I was also lost about how to deal with it much of the time and that it made me feel helpless &#8212; all of which was true. It&#8217;s not enough to simply say &#8220;I understand how you feel.&#8221; For this to work, you need to be specific.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>3.  Find out what they are positive about and reinforce it.</strong> This doesn&#8217;t mean trying to convince them to be positive. It means giving attention to whatever positive feelings they do show &#8212; and chances are they will have shown some because it&#8217;s unusual to find people who are purely negative.  If they <em>are</em> purely negative, then make sure they see you supporting others who have shown positivity. The idea is to give positive attention to positive feeling. And to offer concrete hope. It&#8217;s concrete because it&#8217;s based on actual positive feelings people already have, rather than harping on positive feelings you think they should have.
</p></blockquote>
<p>During step 3 you are responding positively <em>with</em> others, not <em>against</em> them. You are showing them that you support them. And you are showing them that they will be rewarded &#8212; with your support and attention &#8212; when they do and say things that are positive. During step 3 you are transforming the downward spiral into an upward one.</p>
<p>In my conversation with Eleanor, I asked her what <em>did</em> work to keep the kids playing nicely together. She talked about a previous morning when we directed their attention more proactively by doing an art project with them. It also worked well, she said, when she and I took each kid individually to do an errand or a project.</p>
<p>In less than five minutes, my conversation with Eleanor reversed its course from negative to positive.</p>
<p>These three steps are not easy to do because we have to fight our own highly emotional &#8212; and even reasonable &#8212; tendency to be negative about people who are complaining.</p>
<p>When I initially spoke with Dan, he was ready to fire some of his team. That would have, of course, simply exacerbated the negativity of those who stayed.</p>
<p>Instead, he started to listen and validate their negative feelings. What he found underneath the complaining was fear. The company had recently experienced lay-offs and the survivors were still shaken. Were their jobs at risk? (Step 1.)</p>
<p>Dan couldn&#8217;t say that they weren&#8217;t &#8212; especially since he was ready to fire some of the complainers. But what he did do was listen and tell them that he shared some of their anxiety &#8212; not about being fired, but about feeling unsettled with so much to accomplish and fewer people to get the job done. In other words, he was negative <em>with</em> them. (Step 2.)</p>
<p>Then he highlighted some positive things he noticed on his team &#8212; people taking smart risks, working together on complex sales, and partnering successfully with clients &#8212; that were helping to grow the company and secure people&#8217;s jobs. In other words, he was positive <em>with</em> them. (Step 3.)</p>
<p>Before, he never missed an opportunity to highlight &#8212; and criticize &#8212; a person&#8217;s negativity. Now he didn&#8217;t miss an opportunity to highlight &#8212; and praise &#8212; a person&#8217;s positivity.</p>
<p>And it worked. Eventually the mood in the sales group turned and they worked together to bring in the largest client the company had ever won.</p>
<p>As for me? The truth is, it&#8217;s often easier to teach this stuff than it is to do it. In the heat of the moment, I can still get frustrated with other people&#8217;s frustrations. But following these three steps has helped tremendously. And having a partner who reminds me of them?  That helps even more.</p>
<p> *Names Changed</p>
