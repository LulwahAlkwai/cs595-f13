Dissipative system

     *
     *
     * [14]Talk
     *

   This article includes a [15]list of references, related reading or
   [16]external links, but its sources remain unclear because it lacks
   [17]inline citations. Please [18]improve this article by introducing
   more precise citations. (April 2009)

   A dissipative system is a thermodynamically [19]open system which is
   operating out of, and often far from, [20]thermodynamic equilibrium in
   an environment with which it exchanges [21]energy and [22]matter.

   A dissipative structure is a dissipative system that has a dynamical
   régime that is in some sense in a reproducible steady state. This
   reproducible steady state may be reached by natural evolution of the
   system, by artifice, or by a combination of these two.

Overview[23]Edit

   A [24]dissipative structure is characterized by the spontaneous
   appearance of symmetry breaking ([25]anisotropy) and the formation of
   complex, sometimes [26]chaotic, structures where interacting particles
   exhibit long range correlations. The term dissipative structure was
   coined by Russian-Belgian physical chemist [27]Ilya Prigogine, who was
   awarded the [28]Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1977 for his pioneering
   work on these structures. The dissipative structures considered by
   Prigogine have dynamical régimes that can be regarded as
   thermodynamically steady states, and sometimes at least can be
   described by suitable [29]extremal principles in non-equilibrium
   thermodynamics.

   Examples in every day life include [30]convection, [31]cyclones,
   [32]hurricanes and [33]living organisms. Less common examples include
   [34]lasers, [35]Bénard cells, and the [36]Belousov-Zhabotinsky
   reaction.^[[37]citation needed]

   One way of mathematically modeling a dissipative system is given in the
   article on [38]wandering sets: it involves the action of a [39]group on
   a [40]measurable set.

In control theory[41]Edit

   In systems and [42]control theory, dissipative systems are dynamical
   systems with a state x(t) , inputs u(t) and outputs y(t) , which
   satisfy the so-called "dissipation inequality".

   Given a function w on U \times Y , with finite integral of its modulus
   for any input function u and initial state x(0) over any finite time t
   , called the "supply rate", a system is said to be dissipative if there
   exist a continuous nonnegative function V(x) , with x(0) = 0 , called
   the storage function, such that for any input u and initial state x(0)
   , the following inequality, known as dissipation inequality, always
   holds:

          V(x(t)) - V(x(0)) \le \int_{0}^{t} u(\tau) \cdot y(\tau) d \tau
          ,

   where \cdot denotes the scalar product,

   Dissipative systems satisfy the inequality:

          \frac{dV(x(t))}{dt} \le u(t) \cdot y(t)

   The physical interpretation is that V(x) is the energy in the system,
   whereas u \cdot y is the energy that is supplied to the system.

   This notion has a strong connection with [43]Lyapunov stability, where
   the storage functions may play, under certain conditions of
   controllability and observability of the dynamical system, the role of
   Lyapunov functions.

   Roughly speaking, dissipativity theory is useful for the design of
   feedback control laws for linear and nonlinear systems. Dissipative
   systems theory has been discussed by V.M. Popov, J.C. Willems, D.J.
   Hill and P. Moylan. In the case of linear invariant systems, this is
   known as positive real transfer functions, and a fundamental tool is
   the so-called [44]Kalman-Yakubovich-Popov lemma which relates the state
   space and the frequency domain properties of positive real systems.
   Dissipative systems are still an active field of research in systems
   and control, due to their important applications.

Quantum dissipative systems[45]Edit

   Main article: [46]Quantum dissipation

   As [47]quantum mechanics, and any classical [48]dynamical system,
   relies heavily on [49]Hamiltonian mechanics for which [50]time is
   reversible, these approximations are not intrinsically able to describe
   dissipative systems. It has been proposed that in principle, one can
   couple weakly the system - say, an oscillator - to a bath, i.e., an
   assembly of many oscillators in thermal equilibrium with a broad band
   spectrum, and trace (average) over the bath. This yields a [51]master
   equation which is a special case of a more general setting called the
   [52]Lindblad equation that is the quantum equivalent of the classical
   [53]Liouville equation. The well known form of this equation and its
   quantum counterpart takes time as a reversible variable over which to
   integrate, but the very foundations of dissipative structures imposes
   an [54]irreversible and constructive role for time.
