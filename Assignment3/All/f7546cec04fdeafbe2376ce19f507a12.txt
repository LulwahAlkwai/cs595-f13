			<h2 class="title">Are Wearables The Enterprise&nbsp;Future?</h2>

			<div class="post-info">
				<div>
					<span class="comments-meta"><a href="http://makemobilework.wordpress.com/2013/06/17/are-wearables-the-enterprise-future/#respond">Leave a Comment</a></span>
					Posted by <span class="author"><a href="http://makemobilework.wordpress.com/author/ctsilva/" title="Posts by C. Silva" rel="author">C. Silva</a></span> on <span class="time">June 17, 2013</span>
									</div>

			<div class="content">
				<h1>Glass Exploring, Badge Unlocked</h1>
<p>I was fortunate enough to be able to purchase my Google Glass Explorer Edition (XE) shortly before the Google I/O event this past month in San Francisco. No expense seemed to be spared in the on-boarding process. I arrived at San Francisco&#8217;s swank Intercontinental Hotel at the appointed time and was whisked to the presidential suite by of the Google&#8217;s Glass Guides &#8211; members of the Google team brought on for the express task of welcoming new Glass users and acquainting them with their new hardware.</p>
<div id="attachment_1267" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 235px"><a href="http://makemobilework.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/cs-wearing-glas-by-jowyang.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1267" alt="GLASS HEASDHOT" src="http://makemobilework.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/cs-wearing-glas-by-jowyang.jpg?w=225&#038;h=300" width="225" height="300" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Sporting The Most Unobtrusive Glass Color, Tangerine. Image captured by @JOwyang</p></div>
<p>The scene was nerd-vana, studded with Google execs, tech luminaries and Valley big shots. The pomp and circumstance aside, the 45 minute, 1:1 session I had with my Glass Guide was one of the most well-planned technology demos I&#8217;d ever taken part in, and I do these sorts of things quite often. That said, this sort of bespoke sales process won&#8217;t scale, and the question on many peoples&#8217; minds is, &#8220;who&#8217;s gonna buy this thing,&#8221; and beyond that, &#8220;is this the future of computing?&#8221;</p>
<h2>What It Is, My Man?</h2>
<p>After getting my Glass, I made an effort to wear them as often as possible and to expose as many people as possible to the experience when attending an event and speaking. Those who wore the device had comments that coalesced into four categories, here&#8217;s what they said:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>&#8220;I thought it did more.&#8221;</strong> The Glass interface is a series of cards, not unlike Google&#8217;s <a title="Google: Google Now" href="http://www.google.com/landing/now/" target="_blank">Now</a> service on Android smartphones and via the Google search app on iOS. While the device can gather data over an available WiFi connection you configure via your browser or the mobile app (forget about any network that requires a click through page or login, though) most of its functions need to be configured and customized via the same browser or a mobile application and things like GPS and calling are not native to the device, they come from your device &#8211; and do so better if it&#8217;s Android device. Glass, on its own, is a very complex and costly peripheral for your phone or tablet, relatively useless as a standalone device.</li>
<li><strong>&#8220;It costs how much?!&#8221;</strong> Yes, this is a beta device, yes Google had to underwrite the development of a hardware product from scratch and in doing both, it asked Explorers to pay a hefty sum ($1,500) to get on board early. When demoing the unit, I found zero users whose opinion of Glass didn&#8217;t change when they heard the price. Their enthusiasm almost always faded to, &#8220;well, it&#8217;s kinda cool,&#8221; once they&#8217;d digested the investment needed to get their own contrast with what they were able to &#8211; or not able to do with it. This is a device that will thrive at a $200-$300 price point, no more.</li>
<li><strong>Is there an X app for it?</strong> Everyone was most interested in what Glass <em>could</em> do, their wish list eclipsing its current capabilities. This isn&#8217;t surprising for a new product, imagination is almost always more enthralling than the real thing. However the first crop of &#8220;apps&#8221; that was released shortly after I picked up my XE left me wanting more, a lot more. The Twitter app was something I was really looking forward to. When it arrived, though, what I ended up with was a flurry of information &#8220;cards&#8221; for some of the more prolific Twitter accounts I follow and a confusing process to send a tweet. In my experience, users have to create a Twitter contact and use the &#8220;send a message&#8221; command  to share a tweet. Confusingly, you can&#8217;t use the &#8220;share&#8221; command which is reserved for Google+.</li>
<li><strong>Can I take a picture? Wait, how do I take a picture&#8230; OH!</strong> It&#8217;s hard to explain the intuitive nature of doing <em>some</em> things with Glass. One of them is taking pictures and capturing photos and video. It&#8217;s a bit confusing the first time, as we&#8217;re not used to using a wide-angle lens that captures what we&#8217;re already looking at. There&#8217;s no framing and no focusing something foreign to many of us who are used to thinking in viewfinder mode when shooting a picture. That causes a bit of dissonance for many users but once you get it you <strong>get</strong> it. The unobtrusiveness of Glass is its best asset and shows a lot of potential for how i might be used in the future, unfortunately this early in the game many parts of the Glass OS and commands get in the way of what should be a natural experience.</li>
</ul>
<div id="attachment_1269" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 310px"><a href="http://makemobilework.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/glass-from-stage-zoholics-13.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1269" alt="Stage IMG" src="http://makemobilework.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/glass-from-stage-zoholics-13.jpg?w=300&#038;h=221" width="300" height="221" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Taking pictures of your audience while onstage with Glass is&#8230; less creepy than with a smartphone. I think. Taken at Zoholics 2013</p></div>
<h2>Should A CIO Buy 1,000 Of These now? No. Buy 10</h2>
<p>I think it&#8217;s quite early to call this the dawning of the era of wearable tech. I firmly believe that we&#8217;ll see more, better (in the battery department, hopefully) and cheaper tools come out in the next two years. I also think that the biggest promise for this sort of technology is in industrial settings. I was asked by my Altimeter peers, &#8220;what does this mean for CIOs; should they buy 500? 1,000? 10,000?&#8221; My response, &#8220;they should wait until price comes down, function goes up and these get more rugged, then buy 10 and relegate their testing to process areas where a great deal of multi-tasking takes place.&#8221;</p>
<p>I can see huge implications for an ambient, unobtrusive data feed in medical applications, pick and pack workers using these for order and route information and first responders taking these into unknown locales to guide them amidst rapidly changing terrain. Do we <em>need </em>Glass? No. Fact is many of us carry smartphones but likely don&#8217;t <em>need</em> them, either. With the advent of Blackberrys and Treos, we gained email and  some basic apps in our pocket and our expectations of productivity shifted. We&#8217;d all functioned fine prior, but our expectations of what work got done where re-calibrated. Quantifying the benefit, well, that&#8217;s a bit more tricky and I&#8217;ve not seen too many widely applicable metrics. As more immersive devices like iOS and Android phones came to prominence, expectations shifted again. With app-rich devices and tablets, many of us work exclusively from a mobile device; it&#8217;s become a device that can do x <strong>and</strong> y, not a device that makes us as do I want x <strong>or</strong> y. So let&#8217;s give wearables a chance to come to prominence, the market will determine how much of a success these devices are for general users &#8211; I tend to think it&#8217;s limited and the real story is in industrial apps &#8211; and not rush to calculate the <em>need </em>for wearables; that argument is a bit of a slippery slope, after all.</p>
