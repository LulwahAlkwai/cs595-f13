					<h1><a href="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/2010/04/02/pigeons-outperform-humans-at-the-monty-hall-dilemma/">Pigeons outperform humans at the Monty Hall Dilemma</a></h1>
					<div class="post-author"><span class="by">by</span> Ed Yong </div>
				</header>
				<div class="article-text">
					<p><img class="alignright size-full wp-image-1147" src="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/files/2012/12/Monty_hall_dilemma1.jpg" alt="Monty_hall_dilemma" width="200" height="111" />Imagine that you’re in a game show and your host shows you three doors. Behind one of them is a shiny car and behind the others are far less lustrous goats. You pick one of the doors and get whatever lies within. After making your choice, your host opens one of the other two doors, which inevitably reveals a goat. He then asks you if you want to stick with your original pick, or swap to the other remaining door. What do you do?</p>
<p>Most people think that it doesn’t make a difference and they tend to stick with their first pick. With two doors left, you should have even odds of selecting the one with the car. If you agree with this reasoning, then you have just fallen foul of one of the most infamous of mathematical problems – <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monty_Hall_problem">the Monty Hall Dilemma</a>. In reality, you should actually swap every time – doing so means double the odds of getting the car. I will explain why shortly but if you’re currently confused, you are not alone. Over the years, the problem has ensnared countless people, including professional mathematicians. But not, it seems, pigeons.</p>
<p>Walter Hebranson and Julia Schroder showed that, after some training, the humble pigeon can learn the best tactic for the Monty Hall Problem, switching from their initial choice almost every time. Amazingly, humans who get similar extensive practice never develop the optimal strategies that the pigeons pick up. This doesn’t mean that pigeons are “smarter than humans” as some news stories have claimed, but it does mean that the two species approach probability problems in different ways. We suffer because we overthink the problem.</p>
<p><span id="more-1144"></span>The Monty Hall Dilemma takes its name from Monty Hall, the presenter of a show called <em>Let’s Make a Deal</em>, which involved similar choices. The dilemma became truly legendary when it featured in a column called <em>Ask Marilyn </em>in <em>Parade </em>magazine. When columnist Marilyn vos Savant, the then holder of the Guinness World Record for highest IQ, wrote the correct solution, she was inundated with complaints.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.marilynvossavant.com/articles/gameshow.html">Around 10,000 readers disagreed with her</a>, and wrote in to say as much (these were the days when trolls had to actually pay for postage; read the last letter in particular). Many of them had PhDs and many were mathematicians. Even <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Erd%C5%91s">Paul Erdos</a>, the most prolific mathematician in history, refused to believe this explanation until computer simulations proved beyond all doubt that always switching was the best strategy.</p>
<p>The problem is that most people assume that with two doors left, the odds of a car lying behind each one are 50/50. But that’s not the case – the actions of the host beforehand have shifted the odds, and engineered it so that the chosen door is half as likely to hide the car.</p>
<p>At the very start, the contestant has a one in three chance of picking the right door. If that’s the case, they should stick. They also have a two in three chance of picking a goat door. In these situations, the host, not wanting to reveal the car, <em>will always pick the other goat door</em>. The final door hides the car, so the contestant should swap. This means that there are two trials when the contestant should swap for every one trial when they should stick. The best strategy is to always swap – that way they have a two in three chance of driving off, happy and goatless.</p>
<p>All over the world, people are spectacularly bad at this. We almost always stick or, at best, show indifference. Hebranson and Schroder wanted to see if other species would be similarly vexed. They worked with six Silver King pigeons and altered the game show format to suit their beaks.</p>
<p>Each pigeon was faced with three lit keys, one of which could be pecked for food. At the first peck, all three keys switched off and after a second, two came back on including the bird’s first choice. The computer, playing the part of Monty Hall, had selected one of the unpecked keys to deactivate. If the pigeon pecked the right key of the remaining two, it earned some grain. On the first day of testing, the pigeons switched on just a third of the trials. But after a month, all six birds switched almost every time, earning virtually the maximum grainy reward.</p>
<p><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1148" src="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/files/2012/12/Monty_pigeon.jpg" alt="Monty_pigeon" width="500" height="290" />Every tasty reward would reinforce the pigeon’s behaviour, so if it got a meal twice as often when it switched, you’d expect it to soon learn to switch. Hebranson and Schroder demonstrated this with a cunning variant of the Monty Hall Dilemma, where the best strategy would be to <em>stick </em>every time. With these altered probabilities, the pigeons eventually learned the topsy-turvy tactic.</p>
<p>It may seem obvious that one should choose the strategy that would yield the most frequent rewards and even the dimmest pigeon should pick up the right tactic after a month of training. But try telling that to students. Hebranson and Schroder presented 13 students with a similar set-up to the pigeons. There were limited instructions and no framing storyline – just three lit keys and a goal to earn as many points as possible. They had to work out what was going on through trial and error and they had 200 goes at guessing the right key over the course of a month.</p>
<p>At first, they were equally likely to switch or stay. By the final trial, they were still only switching on two thirds of the trials. They had edged towards the right strategy but they were a long way from the ideal approach of the pigeons. And by the end of the study, they were showing no signs of further improvement.</p>
<p><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1149" src="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/files/2012/12/Monty_human.jpg" alt="Monty_human" width="500" height="287" />Why is the Monty Hall Dilemma so perplexing to humans, when mere pigeons seem to cope with it? Hebranson and Schroder think this is a case of our own vaunted intelligence working against us. When faced with a problem like this, we try to think it through, working out the best solution before we do anything. This would be fine, except we’re really quite bad at problems involving conditional probability (such as “if this happens, what are the odds of that happening?”). Despite our best attempts at reasoning, most of us arrive at the wrong answer.</p>
<p>Pigeons, on the other hand, rely on experience to work out probabilities. They have a go, and they choose the strategy that seems to be paying off best. They also seem immune to a quirk of ours called “probability matching”. If the odds of winning by switching are two in three, we’ll switch on two out of three occasions, even though that’s a worse strategy than always switching. This is, of course, exactly what the students in Hebranson and Schroder’s experiments did. The pigeons, on the other hand, always switched – no probability matching for them.</p>
<p>In short, pigeons succeed because they don’t over-think the problem. It’s telling that among humans, it’s the youngest students who do best at this puzzle. Eighth graders are actually more likely to work out the benefits of switching than older and supposedly wiser university students. Education, it seems, actually worsens our performance at the Monty Hall Dilemma.</p>

	<div class="comment-text">
<p><i>[...] computer simulations proved beyond all doubt that always switching was the best strategy.</i></p>
<p>Excuse me for nitpicking, but I&#8217;m not sure the term &#8220;proof&#8221; is appropriate here, since this a mathematical, not a science problem. You prove a mathematical statement by deriving the statement from known theorems (and ultimately from the basic axioms). Examples &#8211; because that&#8217;s what we&#8217;re really talking about here &#8211; may serve as a hint towards the truth value (and of course counterexamples can demolish any universal claim), but don&#8217;t constitute proof themselves. Here in particular a computer simulation necessarily runs only through a finite number of examples. You could modify the problem so that the best strategy is to switch only in x% of all cases (where x is sufficiently large), and computer simulations would likely mislead you.</p>
<p>Admittedly, the last scenario may seem disconnected from reality, but that&#8217;s the difference between science and math. <img src='http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/icon_wink.gif' alt=';-)' class='wp-smiley' /> </p>
<p>I&#8217;m curious as to how the students &#8220;overthought&#8221; their task. Did they keep track of which buttons they pressed, which lit up and so on? I don&#8217;t know how many iterations you&#8217;d need &#8211; now that&#8217;s a problem where computer simulations would come in real handy &#8211; but at some point I&#8217;d think just looking at your previous successes and failures you should be able to figure out that switching is the better strategy (although I don&#8217;t know if 200 runs are enough for that). Did they formulate a theory on how to best play the game? Basically I&#8217;m wondering if they actually approached the game from a thinking, analytical perspective or whether they relied on their intuition.</p>
<p>Also, I thought Monty Hall was a topic in school math. So, had the student never learned (or forgotten) about the Monty Hall problem? Or did they know it, but didn&#8217;t recognise it in the button pressing variant?</p>
<p>Did Hebranson and Schroder ask the students about any of this?</p>

	<div class="comment-text">
<p>Just imagine there are two candidates, A and B. A and B both choose the same door. After the moderator picked one door A always stays with his first choice, B always changes his choice to the remaining third door. Now imagine you run this experiment 999 times. What will happen? Because A always stays with his initial choice, he will win 333 cars. But where are the remaining 666 cars? Of course B won them!</p>
<p>Another illustration:<br />
Let us conduct the experiment with 100 doors! Now let&#8217;s say the candidate picks door 8. By rule of the game the moderator now has to open 98 of the remaining 99 doors behind which there is no car. Afterwards there is only one door left besides door 8 that the candidate has chosen. Obviously you would change your decision now! The same should be the case with only 3 doors!</p>

	<div class="comment-text">
<p>In regards to &#8216;overthinking&#8217;. The idea here is that the humans had come to the conclusion that the choice did not matter. They had convinced themselves that there was a 50/50 chance of getting the grain, and therefore stopped really looking at new data. Or, (in regards to probability matching) they had come up with a better feel, but were trying to game a random situation. That is, they maybe thought that if they choose the less optimal strategy 33% of the time, it would somehow make them more likely to succeed.</p>
<p>Lastly: You can prove monty hall using binary logic (Brute force all possible outcomes of the binary choices the contestant makes) and you can determine a strategy to win 2 in 3.  The reason why a computer model is used to &#8216;prove&#8217; this to mathematicians, is because they were no doubt a little leery of accepting the proof that contradicted their intuition so strongly.  But having a simulation repeat the test millions of times becomes increasingly hard to deny.</p>

Jerry<br />

April 2, 2010

	<div class="comment-text">
<p>Most decriptions of the Monty Hall Dilemma I have seen rely on an unstated assumption.  That assumption is that Monty either will always show you the goat, or at least Monty showing you the goat is random and not related to whether you selected the door with the car.  But what if that isn&#8217;t the case.  After all the original premise is pick the door you think has the car, not pick the door you think has the car, then I&#8217;ll show you one of the goats and give you chance to switch.</p>
<p>So, what if Monty is short on cash and wants to avoid giving away cars.  If you pick a goat, he declares the game  over, and you&#8217;ve got a goat, or maybe he&#8217;ll give you a chance to trade your door for a curtain that has a sheep behind it.  But if you pick a car, then Monty will show you a goat and try to get you to switch.  In this scenario, switching is a guaranteed loss, and not switching a guaranteed win.</p>
<p>In statistics terms the 2 variables, whether you picked the car, and whether you get to make a second choice are assumed to be independent, while that is never never stated in the scenario.  If they are not independent then the statistical reasoning behind it is incorrect.</p>

<p>There really is no problem here. There is nothing to doubt either. It&#8217;s logic. You don&#8217;t need to simulate this, it&#8217;s a self-contained truth. Your chance of picking the car first time is 1/3 but your chance of choosing a door with a goat behind it, at the beginning, is 2/3. Thus on average, 2/3 of times that you are playing this game you&#8217;ll pick a goat at first go. That also means that 2/3 of times that you are playing this game, and by definition pick a goat, the moderator will have to pick the only remaining goat. Because given the laws of the game the moderator knows where the car is and is only allowed to open a door with a goat in it. What does that mean? That on average, at first go, you pick a goat 2/3 of the time and hence the moderator is forced to pick the remaining goat 2/3 of the time. That means 2/3 of the time there is no goat left, only the car is left behind the remaining door. Therefore 2/3 of the time the remaining door has the car.</p>
