(CBS Seattle) &#8211; </strong>It sounds like something out of the movie “Johnny Mnemonic,” but scientists have successfully been able to “hack” a brain with a device that&#8217;s easily available on the open market.</p>
<p>Researchers from the University of California and University of Oxford in Geneva figured out a way to pluck sensitive information from a person’s head, such as PIN numbers and bank information.</p>
<p>The scientists took an off-the-shelf Emotiv brain-computer interface, a device that costs around $299, which allows users to interact with their computers by thought.</p>
<p>The scientists then sat their subjects in front of a computer screen and showed them images of banks, people, and PIN numbers. They then tracked the readings coming off of the brain, specifically the P300 signal.</p>
<p>The P300 signal is typically given off when a person recognizes something meaningful, such as someone or something they interact with on a regular basis.<div class="entry-injected-ad narrow"><script type="text/javascript">adsonar_placementId=1557648 ;adsonar_pid=2728767 ;adsonar_ps=-1;adsonar_zw=260;adsonar_zh=410;adsonar_jv="ads.adsonar.com";</script><script language="JavaScript" src="http://js.adsonar.com/js/adsonar.js"></script></div>
<p>Scientists that conducted the experiment found they could reduce the randomness of the images by 15 to 40 percent, giving them a better chance of guessing the correct answer.</p>
<p>Another interesting facet about the experiments is how the P300 signal could be read for lie detection.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity12/feasibility-side-channel-attacks-brain-computer-interfaces" target="_blank">In the paper that the scientists released</a>, they state that “the P300 can be used as a discriminative feature in detecting whether or not the relevant information is stored in the subject’s memory.</p>
<p>&#8220;For this reason, a GKT based on the P300 has a promising use within interrogation protocols that enable detection of potential criminal details held by the suspect,” the researchers said.</p>
<p>However, scientists say this way of lie detection is “vulnerable to specific countermeasures,” but not as many compared to a traditional lie detector.</p>
<p>This could only be the beginning of a new form of fraud. Scientists say that a person with their guard lowered could be “easily engaged into &#8216;mind games&#8217; that camouflage the interrogation of the user and make them more cooperative.”</p>
