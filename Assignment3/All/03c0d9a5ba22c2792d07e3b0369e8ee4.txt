<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
At Data Transparency 2013, Issa Confronts SEC's Stalled Progess on Open Data
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-692887020318086688' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on">
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Last Tuesday at <a href="http://datatransparency2013.com/">Data Transparency 2013</a>, Rep. Darrell
 Issa (R-CA), chairman of the House Committee on Oversight and 
Government Reform, announced a new investigation aimed at persuading the
 U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission to restart the <a href="http://www.complianceweek.com/sec-called-to-explain-use-of-interactive-data/article/311885/">stalled transformation</a> of its corporate disclosure <span style="font-size: small;">filings</span> from documents into 
open data.</span></span><br />

I<span style="font-size: small;">t's </span></span>also a l<span style="font-size: small;">esson in the<span style="font-size: small;"> <span style="font-size: small;">challenges of converting any compl<span style="font-size: small;">ex area of <span style="font-size: small;">government reporting <span style="font-size: small;">from <span style="font-size: small;">stat<span style="font-size: small;">ic </span>PDFs with <span style="font-size: small;">page breaks into open data<span style="font-size: small;">--standardized,</span> structured<span style="font-size: small;">, <span style="font-size: small;">and accessible to all</span>.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><br />
<div>
<div>
<div>
<div>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"><br /></span></span>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">In 2009 the SEC began phasing in a <a href="http://xbrl.sec.gov/">requirement</a> for public companies 
to submit their financial statements (and related footnotes) in the <a href="http://xbrl.us/">eXtensible Business Reporting Language</a> (XBRL) structured data format, 
alongside the old-fashioned plain-text <span style="font-size: small;">document versions of those <span style="font-size: small;">financial statements</span></span>. The SEC publishes the 
XBRL files on its <a href="http://www.sec.gov/edgar.shtml">EDGAR</a> website. The SEC's attempts at standardization and 
publication<span style="font-size: small;">--</span>the two fundamental steps of open data <span style="font-size: small;">--</span>should, by now, 
have brought benefits to the agency, investors, and public company 
filers.</span></span></div>
<ul style="text-align: left;">
<li><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Since structured data--with each element electronically 
identified--can be analyzed automatically, the XBRL requirement should
allow the SEC to review corporate financial statements faster and use 
Big Data analytics to find errors and fraud.</span></span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Since structured data can be downloaded read<span style="font-size: small;">il</span>y into databases and
 spreadsheets, the XBRL requirement should allow investors to make 
faster, better decisions.</span></span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Since structured data, as long as 
each element has an agreed definition, can be produced automatically by 
software, the XBRL requirement should allow filers' internal accounting 
software to create their financial statements automatically and submit 
them more easily.</span></span></li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">These three benefits exemplify the three promises of open data 
in government: better management, better public transparency, and 
automated compliance. Other federal agencies choosing to standardize and
 publish their reports--notably the <a href="http://thehill.com/blogs/congress-blog/technology/228873-halfway-home-to-reform-time-for-senate-to-move-on-data-act">Recovery Accountability and Transparency Board</a> in 20<span style="font-size: small;">09 </span>and the <a href="http://www.xbrl.org/us/us/FFIEC%20White%20Paper%2002Feb2006.pdf">FDIC</a> in 200<span style="font-size: small;">6</span>--have achieved all three.</span></span><br />
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"><br /></span></span>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">But so far the SEC has <span style="font-size: small;">missed all three opportunities</span>, to the detriment 
of its own mission, investors' interests, and the compliance burden of the companies it regulates. On 
Tuesday Rep. Issa announced he had sent a <a href="http://www.mondovisione.com/_assets/files/2013-09-10-DEI-to-White-re-Interactive-Data-Rule.pdf">letter</a> to SEC chairman Mary Jo White requesting
 answers and documents <span style="font-size: small;">in</span> each of these three<span style="font-size: small;"> <span style="font-size: small;">areas</span></span>. Here's <span style="font-size: small;">Iss<span style="font-size: small;">a's</span></span> letter:</span></span></div>
<div>
<div style="-x-system-font: none; display: block; font-family: Helvetica,Arial,Sans-serif; font-size-adjust: none; font-size: 14px; font-stretch: normal; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; line-height: normal; margin: 12px auto 6px auto;">
<a href="http://www.scribd.com/doc/168830597/Issa-to-White-re-SEC-open-data" style="text-decoration: underline;" title="View Issa to White re SEC open data on Scribd">Issa to White re SEC open data</a></div>
<iframe class="scribd_iframe_embed" data-aspect-ratio="undefined" data-auto-height="false" frameborder="0" height="600" id="doc_80595" scrolling="no" src="//www.scribd.com/embeds/168830597/content?start_page=1&amp;view_mode=scroll&amp;show_recommendations=true" width="100%"></iframe> <span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">&nbsp;</span></span><br />
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Rep. Issa is not alone in his criticisms. <a href="http://datacoalition.blogspot.com/2013/07/house-appropriations-committee-to-sec.html">Congressional <span style="font-size: small;">appropriators</span></a>, the SEC's own <a href="http://datacoalition.blogspot.com/2013/07/investors-ask-sec-to-commit-to-smart.html">Investor Advisory Committee</a>, the <a href="http://www.aicpa.org/Advocacy/Issues/Pages/XBRL.aspx">American Institute of CPAs</a>, <a href="http://datacoalition.blogspot.com/2013/01/connecting-big-business-regulation-to.html">Columbia Business School</a>, and our <a href="http://datacoalition.com/issues/financial-industry-transparency-act.html"><span style="font-size: small;">c</span>oalition</a> of tech 
companies have all made the same points. But his letter provides the 
most detailed explanation to date of exactly why the SEC's attempt at 
open data has so far <span style="font-size: small;">missed the benefits that</span> the Recovery Board, the FDIC, and 
others have <span style="font-size: small;">realized</span>.</span></span><br />
<br />
<table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: left; margin-right: 1em; text-align: left;"><tbody>
<tr><td style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://img3.allvoices.com/thumbs/image/609/480/100534641-president-obama.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"><img border="0" height="157" src="http://img3.allvoices.com/thumbs/image/609/480/100534641-president-obama.jpg" width="200" /></a></td></tr>
<tr><td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">Disagree with Issa? Ask the President.</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Moreover, on open data, the Republican Chairman of the House Oversight Committee is aligned with <span style="font-size: small;">the Democratic President of the United States. <span style="font-size: small;">On May 9, President Obama released his <span style="font-size: small;"><a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/sites/default/files/omb/memoranda/2013/m-13-13.pdf">Open Data Policy</a>, which requires agencies to <span style="font-size: small;">open</span> <i>all </i><span style="font-size: small;">data they collect and generat</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">e. The SEC's corporate disclosure regime, taken as a whole, flunks all seven parts of President Obama's seven-part definition (<a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/sites/default/files/omb/memoranda/2013/m-13-13.pdf">page 5</a>) of open data.&nbsp; </span><br />
<br /></div>
<div>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"><b>1. <span style="font-size: small;">The SEC Spent $<span style="font-size: small;">11 Million <span style="font-size: small;">Doubl<span style="font-size: small;">e-Checking Fi<span style="font-size: small;">nancial Statements <span style="font-size: small;">with Calculators.</span></span></span></span></span></span></b></span></span><b></b><br />
<b><br /></b></div>
<div>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">First, the SEC has missed its opportunity to use <span style="font-size: small;">structured</span> data to find and 
correct errors (primarily the responsibility of its Division of 
Corporation Finance, or "CorpFin") and accounting fraud (primary the 
Division of Enforcement's job) in the financial statements. Why? Because
 the agency has not tried to incorporate the electronic structure of 
XBRL into CorpFin's reviews of corporate disclosures<span style="font-size: small;">. <span style="font-size: small;">N</span></span>or has it (yet) 
deployed Big Data analytics to help Enforcement sift through financial 
statements for fraud.</span></span></div>
<div>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">I'm accustomed to writing our Coalition's blog in the 
third person, but I must shift to the first person to explain a relevant
 backstory. In 2009 I was an attorney in the SEC's <a href="http://www.sec.gov/spotlight/xbrl.shtml">office of interactive disclosure</a>. I interviewed lawyers and accountants working for CorpFin to
 learn how they review incoming corporate disclosures, particularly the 
financial statements, to find errors and confirm that accounting rules 
and the SEC's regulations are followed.</span></span><br />
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;"><br /></span></span>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">As might be expected, CorpFin<span style="font-size: small;">'s reviewers need to</span> verify that 
all the required items are present in the financial statements that <span style="font-size: small;">the SEC </span>receives. <span style="font-size: small;">They</span> must check the math of financial 
statements to see if any calculations are wrong. They also apply 
accounting rules. For instance, under U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting
 Principles (U.S. GAAP), no "miscellaneous" line item should be greater 
than ten percent of the larger category it's in. If, say, "Miscellaneous
 
Revenue" is more than ten percent of "Total Revenue," CorpFin contacts 
the company to request that it revise the financial statement 
and further break down "Miscellaneous Revenue."</span></span></div>
<div>
<span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">I was shocked to find that CorpFin's attorneys and accountants do all of 
this work manually<span style="font-size: small;">.</span></span></span><br />
<br />
