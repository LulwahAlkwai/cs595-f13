<h3 class='post-title entry-title'>
Governor signs junk-science habeas bill
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-3978642752905602207'>
Grits was particularly gratified to finally see Governor Perry has signed <a href="http://www.capitol.state.tx.us/BillLookup/History.aspx?LegSess=83R&amp;Bill=SB344">SB 344 by Whitmire/Turner</a> ensuring access to the courts for <a href="http://www.texastribune.org/2013/02/04/criminal-justice-advocates-renew-call-flawed-scien/">habeas corpus writs based on junk science</a> that's been debunked since the trial. This is a bill I worked on quite a bit on behalf of the Innocence Project of Texas, finally coming to&nbsp; terms with Justin Wood from the Harris County DA's office over the exact language of the bill. See a copy of <a href="https://docs.google.com/file/d/0B6HJLeMEu3hlQTVJZHFiMkE2S00/edit?usp=sharing">written testimony</a> your correspondent submitted on behalf of the IPOT in support of the legislation.<br />
<br />
Before this bill, the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals had been all over the map on this question - granting relief in some cases on a narrow 5-4 vote, denying it in others. But three of the five judges who've sometimes supported relief in junk science cases - Cathy Cochran, Paul Womack and Tom Price - are all up for re-election next year and it's widely speculated all three will retire. That made passing SB 344 especially critical this session and it's a tremendous relief that it passed. There's no telling who might replace those three judges or how their replacements might have ruled on these cases.<br />
<br />
Congrats to Chairmans John Whtimre and Sylvester Turner for getting this done. This was the <a href="http://gritsforbreakfast.blogspot.com/2013/05/lege-slowly-but-surely-plowing-through.html">5th of six legislative recommendations</a> authored by the Timothy Cole Advisory Panel on Wrongful Convictions.
