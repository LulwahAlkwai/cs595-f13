<div class="tb"><h1>Tomorrow&rsquo;s Food, Today: 3 Opportunities for Disruption of the Industrial Food System</h1>
        <ul class="navigation byline">
            <li><a class="nolink">Posted by </a><a href="/profile/BrittanySmith">Brittany Smith</a><a class="nolink"> on September 6, 2013 at 10:53am</a></li>
            <li><a class="xg_sprite xg_sprite-view" href="http://insights.wired.com/profiles/blog/list?user=06im82jn8fwc4">View Blog</a></li>
        </ul>

        <div class="postbody">
                    <div class="xg_user_generated">
                <p>It’s a waste of words to spend time writing about how broken our food system is. For anyone who cares to find out, it’s not difficult to uproot articles and research about polluted waterways caused by fertilizer runoff, “pink slime,” the obesity epidemic, food deserts, harmful growth hormones in our meat, the “carbon footprint” of food transportation, millions of pounds of food going to waste <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/bethhoffman/2013/02/07/what-food-do-we-throw-out-how-to-stop-food-waste-at-home/" target="_blank">every year</a>, and more.</p>
<p>These problems, further complicated by the political and social climate surrounding the food production system, have not gone unnoticed.</p>
<p>Entrepreneurs and venture capitalists have realized that an opportunity to change the way we produce, transport, process, and consume food exists. According to <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/04/29/business/venture-capitalists-are-making-bigger-bets-on-food-start-ups.html?pagewanted=1" target="_blank"><em>The New York Times</em></a>, venture funding to food tech startups is higher than ever before, to the tune of $350 million in the last year.</p>
<p>But food tech startups, especially at the consumer level, have yet to prove themselves profitable or permeating, despite the dire need for innovation in our food system and despite the gargantuan market potential for entrepreneurs (everyone eats, after all). There are several reasons that investment in food technology is currently going to waste.</p>
<p>First and foremost, the rhetoric surrounding the so-called “food movement” invokes a certain nostalgia for the days before technology entered our food system, before GPS-enabled tractors, before genetically engineered organisms, and before large scale processing techniques.</p>
<p>It’s difficult for companies with a good product and a solid business plan to scale when “processed” food is a bad word and “technology” has become the villain in this Neo-Luddism era of Slow Food. As the New York Times notes, “The movement[...] is decidedly low-tech.”</p>
<p>Secondly, the consumer-facing tech products that are coming out are geared at a more informed, more affluent audience, not the mainstream. They often require significant behavior change in order to achieve widespread adoption, and it's hard to get even the folks who can afford it to change their eating and buying habits in any radical way.</p>
<p>That’s why fundamental change in our food system has to happen in the actual machinery of the system — the harvesting, the processing, the transportation. Venture capital and entrepreneurial efforts are currently misguided and should be refocused on the system as a whole, where they will have the most impact on the food system (and, consequently, will see the greatest pay-off).</p>
<p>Three of the most promising technologies that are being developed in the here and now to reinvent the way we produce food include large-scale vertical farming coupled with aquaponics and/or hydroponics; algae production and cultivation for fertilizer, as well as for nutrient/protein consumption (for animals and humans); and finally, entomophagy.</p>
<p><strong>Food in the City: Growing Up</strong></p>
<p>Companies like <a rel=nofollow href="http://plantagon.com/" target="_blank">Plantagon</a> in Sweden are designing futuristic “skyscraper farms” meant to feed an urban population from within a city’s borders. <a rel=nofollow href="http://farmiculture.com/" target="_blank">Farmiculture</a>, based in Phoenix, is meanwhile turning commercial warehouses into vertical hydroponic greenhouses, maximizing space and resources and eliminating the need for tractors, intensive tilling, and expensive and wasteful irrigation systems.</p>
<p>When done well, both large-scale vertical farming and hydroponics has proven to be more productive and diverse, <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.scientificamerican.com/article.cfm?id=the-rise-of-vertical-farms" target="_blank">less wasteful</a>, and extremely energy and resource efficient. Even better, produce can be grown year-round and crops are more resistant to climate change and other weather-related factors that conventional farmers face.</p>
<p>Leafy greens, tomatoes, herbs, and peppers can be raised in close proximity to the retailers who will stock the vegetables in their grocery stores and restaurants, assuaging some of the cost of transportation, both to the farmer and to the environment. The plants in these systems grow much more quickly because they are not exposed to the stress caused by being under or over-watered or having to struggle in suboptimal conditions.</p>
<p>Vertical farms and hydroponics (vertical or otherwise) are a win-win-win solution for the farmers (who can cut costs on water and fertilizer), the consumers (who can buy affordable, fresh produce year-round) and the environment. Aquaponics systems use 70% to 90% less water than conventional irrigation techniques.</p>
<p><strong>The Algae Band-Aid: Biofertilizer</strong></p>
<p>Large-scale algae cultivation for biofuels and other high-value products already exists at scale, but the latest application for algae comes from the leftover biomass of the “de-oiling” process.</p>
<p>The leftover mass is nutrient- and energy-rich and makes for an organic and environmentally safe biofertilizer that’s able to compete (in terms of yield/acre) with the chemical fertilizers responsible for such environmental damage such as the “dead zone” in the Gulf of Mexico and the loss of biodiversity (and therefore, productivity) in the soil.</p>
<p>In a <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WEYqHZTupW0&amp;feature=youtu.be" target="_blank">study</a> performed at the University of Texas at Austin, algae-based fertilizer showed higher crop yields than traditional chemical fertilizers, another win for farmers who have to meet their bottom line.</p>
<p>Biofertilizers also reinforce soil nutrients and they encourage microbiological diversity in the soil. These are obvious boons to the environment, but they also have long-term economic benefits as well. Good soil is good for yield is good for business.</p>
<p>Right now, the upfront cost of growing algae and producing fertilizers is high as research and development are still going into commercial biofertilizers, but as the technology proves itself out, prices will fall as the short- and long-term benefits are realized.</p>
<p><strong>Eating Insects</strong></p>
<p>Grasshopper cookies and cricket protein bars could soon be making an appearance at your local grocery store or farmer's market. And you should buy them.</p>
<p>Bugs are plentiful, cheap to produce, inexpensive to feed, high in protein, iron, and calcium, low in saturated fat, and require very little water or space. And they’re already being produced at scale to feed the pet chameleons and bearded dragons of American households.</p>
<p>For all of those reasons, insects are an affordable source of protein (which 80% of the world already consumes) and far better for the environment, and our health, than red meat. </p>
<p>In fact, it’s not a new idea. Startups are already jumping (squirming, hopping?) at the market opportunity. The technology to raise and harvest crickets at scale already exists. Companies like <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.chirpfarms.com/" target="_blank">Chirp</a> and <a rel=nofollow href="http://chapul.com/" target="_blank">Chapul</a> are producing organic cricket flour for protein bars and baked goods. Restaurants like <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.lapopularnyc.com/" target="_blank">Antojeria Popular</a> in New York serve crickets disguised as tostadas and <a rel=nofollow href="http://www.donbugito.com/" target="_blank">Don Bugito</a>, a mobile street food vendor in San Francisco, cooks up Spicy Superworms.  </p>
<p>And we already eat weird things. Escargot, lobsters, fish roe -- are grasshoppers really that big of a leap? And yet, there’s still a major barrier to entry for entomophagy. Western psychology tells us that bugs are icky, dirty, and for squashing, not cooking.</p>
<p>Luckily, there’s already a solution to psychological barriers to new products -- good marketing. Insect startups should be funneling their investment funds into their marketing departments, partnering with innovative chefs, holding tastings, designing appealing packaging, and holding cooking classes and educational workshops.</p>
<p>If consumers are properly primed, by the time concerns about our food system and our health reach a tipping point in the next ten years (or sooner), grasshopper spring rolls and mealworm stir fry will be seen as a sustainable alternative to beef, poultry, and pork.</p>
<p><strong>Next Steps</strong></p>
<p>Before these technologies can take hold, we first have to change the way we think about food and food production.</p>
<p>On the most fundamental level, we have to be comfortable with redefining the very concept of food itself. If it was never actually living, is it still meat? Can it be produce if it wasn’t grown in the ground?</p>
<p>That’s the first step.</p>
<p>Then, we have to overcome the local vs. industrial rhetoric to realize that sustainable solutions will likely be a combination of the two.</p>
<p>Finally, we need to help producers overcome the high upfront capital costs of food technology. This will happen when universities, large cash-rich food corporations, and venture capitalists realize that investments in early stage food technology research will have a high pay off in the long-run.</p>
<p>The answer will lie in the win-win-win innovations -- good for the producer, good for the consumer, and good for the environment.</p>
<p><em>By day, Brittany Smith brings early-stage technologies to market at Jones-Dilworth, Inc. At night, she writes frequently about the rhetoric of local food systems and food technology, hoping to one day redefine the way we process, distribute and eat food. To continue the conversation, follow her on Twitter at @<a rel=nofollow href="http://www.twitter.com/brittanyrhea" target="_blank">brittanyrhea</a>. </em></p>            </div>
