              <h4><span style="font-weight:normal">12 Jun 2013:</span> 
Bird-Mimicking Mobile Apps<br /> Harmful to Species, UK Groups Say</h4>
Wildlife officials in the UK are urging people not to use mobile phone apps that mimic bird songs in nature reserves, warning that the devices <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-england-dorset-22863383" target="_blank">can harm some sensitive species</a>, particularly during breeding season. The increasingly popular apps, which use recordings of bird calls to lure the birds closer for <div class="imageleft"><img style="margin: 5px 0px 3 px 0px;" src="http://e360.yale.edu/images/digest/ichirp_bird_song_app.jpg" alt="Chirp Bird Song App" width="105" height="105" border="0" /><div style="width: 105px;" class="credit">iSpiny</div><div style="width: 105px;" class="caption">Icon for Chirp! app</div></div> photographs or better viewing, can distract birds from critical tasks, such as feeding their young, according to the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds. The Dorset Wildlife Trust (DWT), a conservation group that oversees 42 reserves, <a href="http://www.dorsetwildlifetrust.org.uk/bird-apps-warning" target="_blank">is discouraging the use of the mobile apps at its reserves</a>, calling it an intentional disturbance of sensitive species such as the Nightjar, a nocturnal bird that has experienced a recent recovery in the area. “I’m sure visitors would be devastated if they realized the possible disturbance they were causing to wildlife,” said Chris Thain, a manager at the DWT’s Brownsea Island reserve. Hilary Wilson, a biologist who helped develop the Chirp! app for the UK-based developer <a href="http://www.spinysoft.co.uk/ispiny.html" target="_blank">iSpiny</a>, told the BBC that the technology is meant to be an educational tool, but agreed that the sounds could have a powerful effect on birds. “Out of consideration for both the birds and fellow birdwatchers, just keep the volume low,” she said.<br /><br />
