	<h2>Hackbright: Women Can Code</h2>
	
	<div class="article-info">
	 <a href="http://blog.evernote.com/opportunitynotes/category/service/" title="View all posts in Service" rel="category tag">Service</a> <span class="separator">|</span> <time datetime="2013-04-23" pubdate>April 23, 2013 7:01 am</time>	
	</div>
	
	
	
	<div id="attachment_1413" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 330px"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1413" alt="Hackbright teaches coding to women." src="http://blog.evernote.com/opportunitynotes/files/2013/04/Screenshot_4_22_13_12_45_PM-320x219.png" width="320" height="219" /><p class="wp-caption-text">Hackbright teaches coding to women.</p></div>
<p>Some people are born hackers. They&#8217;re drawn to computers, take up coding as soon as they learn to read, and they never stop programming, learning, and developing their skills. I work with some of these people at Evernote. You probably do, too. Maybe you are one.</p>
<p>Geeks and natural coders make up the hacker elite, and create many of the apps and services that we use. But you don&#8217;t have to be born a geek to become a coder. That&#8217;s good, because there aren&#8217;t nearly enough developers now, judging by the job boards in Silicon Valley.</p>
<p>That also makes an opportunity. There is money in creating engineers.</p>
<p>There are several organizations that teach the people who neglected to be prodigies or CS majors programming. One of the most interesting is The <a href="http://www.hackbrightacademy.com/">Hackbright Academy</a>, which runs 10-week programming courses exclusively for women.</p>
<p>I met Hackbright&#8217;s <a href="https://twitter.com/thisgirlangie">Angie Chang</a> at a <a href="http://girlgeekdinners.com/">Girl Geek</a> dinner she organized that we hosted here at Evernote. So far, she told me, she&#8217;s graduated all 28 women that have entered her program, and 90% of them are now employed as software engineers.</p>
<p>&#8220;Our goal is to pivot peoples&#8217; careers,&#8221; Chang told me. But pivoting women into coding jobs is different from teaching men.</p>
<h3>It&#8217;s different for girls</h3>
<p>I talked with Sara Gottlieb, one of the Hackbright graduates and now a front-end engineer at <a href="http://www.surveymonkey.com/">Survey Monkey</a>. She told me that her psychology degree was &#8220;not helping&#8221; in the job market. And while she saw the opportunity for technical talent in the Bay Area, she feared, as many people do, that without a lifetime of experience as a geek, she&#8217;d never learn enough to get a job in tech.</p>
<p>Gottlieb told me that her college education reinforced the myth of the natural hacker. Even in Intro to Computer Science at her school, University of Vermont, she says, &#8220;Nobody was really starting from zero.&#8221;</p>
<p>But she found Hackbright, learned Python in the first five weeks, and then the remainder of the term on a personal project. Landing a job was easy, as Survey Monkey reps came to the demo day at the end of the session.</p>
<p>&#8220;There&#8217;s still a lot for me to learn,&#8221; she says. But she&#8217;s doing it now from inside a company.</p>
<p>Another Hackbright grad, Nicole Zuckerman, came from background that would appear to be the most nurturing possible for a budding geek. She told me she &#8220;grew up with the sound of a modem in my ears.&#8221; She took AP calculus and physics. Her parents encouraged her to pursue the sciences.</p>
<p>But, she says, &#8220;I don&#8217;t think they could conceptualize this [programming] as an option for me.&#8221;</p>
<p>She said the message to girls is different. They&#8217;re taught to focus on the intrinsics more than on the things they build. &#8220;Girls are raised not to screw up. If you don&#8217;t make it, it seems more destructive for girls than boys.&#8221;</p>
<p>Zuckerman ended up with a job in publishing, and was moving up the management ladder. But she wanted, she told me, &#8220;to feel like I was contributing.&#8221; She left her job for a Hackbright session.</p>
<p>She codes at Eventbrite now, which gives her the direct job satisfaction she was seeking. &#8220;Like, today, I made a thing happen,&#8221; she says.</p>
<p>Before Hackbright, she had only taken a few online tutorials in programming. It remained daunting, &#8220;I had heard programmers had to start as kids,&#8221; she told me. But Hackbright sounded promising, so she thought she&#8217;d give it a shot.</p>
<p>At the end of her session, she says, &#8220;There were 20-plus companies listening to 16 grads present.&#8221; Good odds for an entry-level developer.</p>
<p>We might be in a jobs bubble for developers. But it&#8217;s still a skill worth learning, since if you can&#8217;t get a job as a programmer, you can always go entrepreneurial and create your own. I&#8217;d like to see more Hackbrights, for groups that might learn differently from the way programming is traditionally taught: Women, inner-city kids, older people, journalists (ahem), you name it. There&#8217;s a lot of work to be done, and a lot of people who would like to do it.</p>
<p>-<a href="http://twitter.com/rafe">Rafe</a></p>
