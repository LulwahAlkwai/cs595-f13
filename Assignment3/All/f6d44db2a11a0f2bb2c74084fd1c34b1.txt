                  <h1 class="title">Military Report: America Has &#039;Misguided&#039; Fixation With Domestic Drilling</h1>            <div id="node-26482" class="node node-type-blog clear-block">
	
			
	<div class="meta">
		
		<h3>The report, released quietly this month, says climate change is a bigger national security threat than the country's dependence on foreign oil.</h3>
		<div id="authoredBy" style="width: 65%; float:left;">
			<a href="/author/john-h.-cushman-jr.">By John H. Cushman Jr., InsideClimate News</a>		</div>

		<div style="width: 35%; float:right; text-align: right;">
			Jun 24, 2013		</div>
		<div style="clear: both;"></div>
	</div>

<p>WASHINGTON—A <a href="http://www.cna.org/research/2013/climate-energy-nexus-summary">new report</a> from the <a href="http://www.cna.org/centers/cna" target="_blank">U.S. Center for Naval Analyses</a> and the London-based <a href="http://www.rusi.org/" target="_blank">Royal United Services Institute</a>, two of the NATO alliance's front-line strategy centers, recommends putting more effort into fighting global warming than securing reliable supplies of fossil fuels.</p>
<p class="Normal1">The authors call the habitual American fixation on winning energy independence through expanded North American production of oil and natural gas "misguided." They say the "only sustainable solution" to the problem of energy insecurity is not through more drilling, but through energy efficiency and renewable fuels, like biofuels to replace oil.</p>
<p class="Normal1">Despite the steady supplies provided by the current U.S. drilling boom, "the increased domestic production of oil and natural gas is not a panacea for the country's energy security dilemma," they say.</p>
<p class="Normal1">And in blunt language, they criticize American policymakers and legislators for refusing to accept the "robust" scientific evidence that emissions of carbon dioxide are already causing harmful global warming, and for refusing to take actions that, if taken swiftly, could ward off its worst effects.</p>
<p class="Normal1">"Political leaders, including many in the United States, refuse to accept short-term costs to address long-term dangers even though the future costs of responding to disasters after they occur will be far greater," said their report, published this month.</p>
<p class="Normal1">The report, in the works for a year, was released as President Obama prepared to ramp up the administration's efforts on climate change, and while the State Department was <a href="http://insideclimatenews.org/news/20130312/keystone-xl-pipeline-state-department-environmental-impact-statement-climate-change-global-warming-nepa-lawyers" target="_blank">immersed in its review of whether to approve the Keystone XL pipeline</a> to carry tar sands oil from Canada to refineries in the United States.</p>
<p class="Normal1">In a major policy speech on Tuesday, Obama is expected to renew his commitment to regulating emissions from coal-fired power plants, as well as other measures involving renewable energy and green technologies, but not to tip his hand on the Keystone decision. Many in Washington believe that he wants to offer strict controls on power plants, the nation’s leading source of greenhouse gases, as a quid pro quo for approving the controversial pipeline, which is seen by opponents as a contributor to the global warming problem.</p>
<p class="Normal1">Keystone's proponents have described the project as important for energy security.</p>
<p class="Normal1">For several years, the view that global warming caused by burning fossil fuels is an overwhelming national security threat has been taking firmer hold in national security circles. In 2007, a <a href="http://www.cna.org/reports/climate" target="_blank">report from CNA's military advisory board called climate change</a> a "threat multiplier." In 2008, a formal National Intelligence Assessment found that climate change poses a serious threat to national security and long-term global stability. <a href="http://www.defense.gov/qdr/images/QDR_as_of_12Feb10_1000.pdf" target="_blank">The Department of Defense's 2010 Quadrennial Defense Review</a>, a major planning document, warned that climate change may fuel conflict, put new strains on military forces operating in the field, and cause damage to military bases, especially ports exposed to rising seas and intense storms.</p>
<p class="Normal1">In an article published in <a class="Normal1" href="http://www.foreignaffairs.com/articles/139509/tom-donilon/energy-and-american-power?page=show">Foreign Affairs online in June</a>, Tom Donilon, the former National Security Adviser to President Obama, wrote: "The Obama administration's National Security Strategy recognizes the 'real, urgent, and severe' threat posed by climate change in no uncertain terms, stating, 'change wrought by a warming planet will lead to new conflicts over refugees and resources; new suffering from drought and famine; catastrophic natural disasters; and the degradation of land across the globe.'"</p>
<p class="Normal1">But the focus of Donilon's piece was on energy, not on climate change, and it spoke expansively of the importance of increased energy production to America's strength in the world. For example, it claimed that by helping to provide plentiful oil to satisfy world demand, the United States could more effectively squeeze Iran with an embargo, a strategy that otherwise would harm oil-deficient allies.</p>
<p class="Normal1">The new American-British report looks mostly at the other side of the coin, the risks presented by the burning of fossil fuels no matter where they come from.</p>
<p class="Normal1">It acknowledges the problems caused by Western reliance on imported oil, especially from unstable parts of the world. But the new report says that even more important is the compelling need to stop using fossil fuels in the first place, since the steady addition of carbon dioxide to the atmosphere is now posing imminent dangers to national security.</p>
<p class="Normal1">Even though the United States gets just 20 percent of its oil imports from the Middle East, the lowest share in four decades—and it could be headed toward being an oil exporter two decades from now—"the U.S. economy is highly sensitive to supply shocks and price fluctuations, regardless of the source of the oil," it says. "Even new, domestic sources of oil and gas do not free the United States from the risks of over-reliance, because the prices of these commodities will be determined by global markets," the report says.</p><div class="item-list"><ul class="pager"><li class="pager-current first">1</li>
