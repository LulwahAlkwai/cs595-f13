    </header><!-- .entry-header -->

<div class="entry-content">
    <script async src="http://platform.vine.co/static/scripts/embed.js" charset="utf-8"></script><p>I always wanted to attend the original <a href="http://makerfaire.com/">Maker Faire</a> in the Bay Area and the annual shows that followed, but they started soon after I moved to Oregon and they never overlapped with my travel down there. I recently noticed they were touring around <a href="https://www.omsi.edu/maker-faire-pdx">Mini Maker Faires</a> to different cities and I was delighted when my local science museum (OMSI) mentioned it was coming up this weekend. I bought tickets for my family and figured it&#8217;d be a lot like the parking lot at O&#8217;Reilly conferences I&#8217;ve attended: mostly nerds with their hobby gadgets, stuff like robots and rockets and a car converted to run on coffee grinds. My daughter is 8 now and we&#8217;re always trying to push her towards having the best STEM education she can get and I figured this might be good for a few science demos. I planned to go on Sunday and looked at random Twitter/Flickr photos from the opening day on Saturday and saw only somewhat interesting looking robots and rockets and figured it&#8217;d probably take an hour to see it all and honestly I just hoped my daughter wouldn&#8217;t be bored by the dry presentation of it.</p>
<p>I got it all hopelessly, completely wrong, and it kind of blew my mind a bit.</p>
<p>First off, my estimate of spending an hour with a bored child turned out to be showing up soon after it opened followed by forcing ourselves to leave about 90min before closing. It was incredible, they had a huge variety of demonstrations that covered not only all aspects of science, but also art, anthropology, cooking, and even some swords and archery. The demos were well-tailored to a young audience but were also fun for adults (I got to make my own pewter coin!). Another aspect I thought was handled well was the mixture of commerce and education. Many of the demos were done by local businesses and they sometimes offered stuff you could buy to do more of the same thing at home. This could have gone very wrong and seemed really crass if vendors only showed up in an effort to sell their junk disguised as a science demo, but it worked out well where if you really liked making a bit of fresh cheese in a booth, you could buy a cheesemaking kit for doing larger batches in your own kitchen.</p>
<p><a href="https://vine.co/v/hnDnYugIZe1"><img class="alignright size-full wp-image-3370" alt="Screen Shot 2013-09-16 at 2.23.04 PM" src="http://a.wholelottanothing.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/Screen-Shot-2013-09-16-at-2.23.04-PM.png" width="222" height="222" /></a>Lastly, the thing that really blew my mind was <a href="https://vine.co/v/hnDnYugIZe1">seeing my daughter learn how to solder and assemble basic electronics</a> by building what was a essentially <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LED_art#LED_throwies">a LED throwie</a>. The LED Throwie is a small battery, a magnet, and a few LED lights that experimental graffiti artists came up with at <a href="http://www.eyebeam.org/">Eyebeam</a> and plans on how to make them launched on the early <a href="http://www.instructables.com/">Instructables</a> website. I recall Throwies pissed off a lot of major cities as random blinking lights started appearing on their public metal sculptures. I remember watching Throwies blow up in popularity while <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonah_Peretti">Jonah Peretti</a> was at Eyebeam, who likely used those lessons to help push content viral when he started Buzzfeed and Huffington Post later on. Then I thought about <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_O'Reilly">Tim O&#8217;Reilly</a> always watching the alpha geeks at the edges, knowing those people are 3-5 years ahead of the curve and that we could look to what they were interested in as what the mainstream would someday be into, and how today&#8217;s Mini Maker Faire was a lot like the Emerging Technology conferences I attended 5+ years ago. I also thought about <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dale_Dougherty">Dale Dougherty</a> &amp; <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Frauenfelder">Mark Frauenfelder</a> launching Make Magazine that turned into this.</p>
<p>It was amazing to see what was once an experimental graffiti project loved and hated around the world morph into a simple teaching tool for kids that could expose them to science, art, and fun by letting them create a small blinking object they could pin on their clothes. This all grew from geek hobbyists in a parking lot much like the one I stood in today, and the magazine that launched from that. The Mini Maker Faire quite literally was helping pass along the wonder and knowledge I saw over the last decade to a new generation of Makers.</p>
<p>Needless to say, if you&#8217;re a parent and one of the Mini Maker Faires is in your town, by all means do everything you can to attend one; you&#8217;ll have a great time.</p>
