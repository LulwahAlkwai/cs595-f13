			<h1 class="post-title">Stars, and Starlets, of Science</h1>
		</header>
		<p><a href="http://hergenius.wordpress.com/2012/12/21/stars-and-starlets-of-science/photo-3/" rel="attachment wp-att-209"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-209" alt="photo" src="http://hergenius.files.wordpress.com/2012/12/photo2.jpg?w=545&#038;h=408" width="545" height="408" /></a>Christmas season in New York has what you would call &#8220;high production values.&#8221; The scale and fanfare of the lights and events in Manhattan this week remind me of the live finale of Stars of Science I attended last month in Doha.</p>
<p>I went reluctantly. Doha seemed too long a haul from New York for a one-night event. But “Stars of Science” Content Producer Leila Creber is persistent about the charms of the Arab-world reality television show, now entering its fifth season. The series has upwards of 1.5 million viewers by all estimates, spread throughout the GCC, North Africa and the Eastern Mediterranean, as well as 358,000 followers on Facebook and a growing number&#8211;nearly 4000&#8211;on Twitter. The four finalists of each season’s nine-week competition walk away with awards totaling $600,000 to develop their technological innovation—no strings attached. Previous years’ contestants now head their own companies, and manufacture products ranging from medical devices to educational media to home appliances.There’s nothing like this television show in the United States or the UK, two places that seem to mint a new reality series every week.</p>
<p>The live finale on an open-air stage at the Qatar Science and Technology Park was attended by about 500 people. We sat in over-sized, brown velour seats watching dancers scale graphic walls and thrust laser spears at one another while drummers with neon-dotted vests pounded out a soundtrack between earnest video summaries of the finalists projects: a desktop holographic tool, an automated home pharmacy, a programmable “smart cooker,” and a shared desktop projection system. Like most reality shows, it was whacky, even kitschy. Unlike most reality television, it was important.</p>
<p>Despite the fact that the ancient Arab world literally invented mathematics, the practice of medicine, astronomy, physics, and numerous gadgets for executing and recording those disciplines, most of the region’s science and technology has lagged in modern times compared with that of its western counterparts. The reasons are complex: in some places like Palestine, Lebanon, Iraq, Afghanistan, now Syria, wars have permanently distressed and displaced large bands of the population. Wars notwithstanding, generations of privileged children have also been sent abroad from the Arab region to study at prestigious western universities. Many forged new lives away from home.</p>
<p>In yet other places, long colonial rule by the French or the British stymied homegrown innovation. The discovery of massive oil and natural gas reserves later dampened the desire or the perceived need for a robust culture of invention. In some places, tourist economies were seeded and now thrive. In different ways, the region grew into a culture of haves and have nots. The ruling rich could buy whatever they needed, including the services of all classes of guest workers. They could import their food, their cars, their luxury goods, and even their science and technology. In large measure, they still do.</p>
<p>Arab thought leaders understand that the future is not set in oil and natural gas. Tourism is not guaranteed. They understand that before the petroleum dollars run out, new industries must be conceived and the Arab countries must compete and contribute on equal footing at a global scale.</p>
<p>Stars of Science was born of this progressivism. Funded by the Qatar Foundation, the education-focused philanthropy headed by the miraculous Sheikha Mozah (the second of the Emir&#8217;s three wives), the show inspires young Arabs to believe in themselves and their creative impulses while it exposes those young innovators (aged 18-30) to the practical details of turning a concept into a business. During their nine weeks on Stars of Science, the 16 contestants, whittled down from a pool of 200, gleaned after casting calls bringing in many thousands of hopefuls each year in a multi-country auditions (the season 5 casting is scheduled in nine countries) face four hurdles.</p>
<p>Proof of Principle, Engineering, Design and Business Plan&#8211;these four benchmarks determine which contestants will stay and which ones be eliminated over the nine-week taping of the show. Each contestant has a work space, mentors and a wealth of consulting experts to help develop their concept while in residence.</p>
<p>Naturally, I wanted to know how the women contestants fared. The final four last month were all men. But out of three female contestants who were part of this season&#8217;s show, one, Aynour Tatanaki, a 20-year-old of Egyptian and Libyan descent, received a special award at the finale to develop her Flexible LED Technology. She received a scholarship that will send her to Singapore to study with world experts in Flexible LED. There, she will develop her programmable walls and floors and other interior surfaces—&#8221;theme design&#8221; for homes, as she calls it, which has possibilities beyond the decorative. It could potentially aid the visually impaired. This &#8220;Stars of Science&#8221; award came after theoretical physicist and author Michio Kaku gave Tatanaki&#8217;s project an appreciative nod during development in a chance meeting when he came to Qatar.</p>
<div id="attachment_197" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 490px"><a href="http://hergenius.wordpress.com/2012/12/21/stars-and-starlets-of-science/photo/" rel="attachment wp-att-197"><img class="size-full wp-image-197" alt="Aynour Tatanaki, Nov. 8, Stars of Science Finale" src="http://hergenius.files.wordpress.com/2012/12/photo.jpg?w=545"   /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Aynour Tatanaki, Nov. 8, Stars of Science Finale</p></div>
<p>I&#8217;ll be following Aynour&#8217;s journey, even though she wasn&#8217;t season four&#8217;s winner, when she heads to Singapore in 2013.</p>
<p>Aynour&#8217;s story will be important. One of the difficulties of women&#8217;s careers in science and technology is that  mentorship&#8211;mentorship that tracks and supports the vicissitudes of women&#8217;s real lives&#8211;is hard to come by. We&#8217;ve known this for decades as various scholars of science, Donna Haraway in particular, have examined the material reality and gendered tensions of staying on track in these careers. One of the challenges of a reality television show about innovation is that the participants, especially those eliminated before the finale, have little to no ongoing guidance after the glare of the cameras fades. Stars of Science judges acknowledged as much in their press conference preceding the finale.</p>
<p>As we&#8217;ve seen with American shows such as Project Runway, for example, non-winning contestants&#8217; ideas may be good, or they may have a well of other pitches that did not get air time. With Stars of Science, some proposals may be good enough to shape the hoped-for technology sectors of the region with a bit more development and guidance.</p>
<p>Between the costumed dancers of the finale and the depth of creativity of its contestants, Stars of Science might just be more exciting than Santa Con.</p>
<p>Track the new season at the official <a href="http://starsofscience.com/sos/en/">Stars of Science website.</a></p>
<p>Read WIRED magazine&#8217;s marvelous <a href="http://www.wired.com/magazine/2012/01/mf_starsofscience/">report (though little on women contestants) of season three. </a></p>
