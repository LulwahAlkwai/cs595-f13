					<h1><a href="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/2013/09/09/the-second-draft-of-the-history-of-science/">The Second Draft of the History of Science</a></h1>
					<div class="post-author"><span class="by">by</span> Carl Zimmer </div>
				</header>
				<div class="article-text">
					<p>I&#8217;ve been hearing good things for a while now about <a href="http://retroreport.org/">Retro Report</a>, a journalism project that produces 10-to-20-minute-long videos about what happened to big headline stories from decades ago. I&#8217;m now gobbling up my Monday morning watching their backlist. It&#8217;s excellent stuff, and I&#8217;ve been trying to figure out why I like it. I think it&#8217;s because the programs get beyond the simple &#8220;Where Are They Now?&#8221; format. The journalists who make the pieces really report the stories&#8211;they go back and find people who were in the midst of the news to interview them, and then they discover the surprising course of the story after it fell away from the world&#8217;s attention.</p>
<p>What really surprised me about Retro Report is that most of their categories are related in one way or another science. I write about new scientific research, and so my job requires me to keep my eyes locked on the future, trying to figure out what some discovery or invention will mean to generations to come. And the longer you spend in this job, the more you start asking yourself, &#8220;Hey, what happened to&#8230;?&#8221; In many cases, things quietly take an unexpected&#8211;but revealing&#8211;turn. Retro Report shows that going back to a story about science can reveal important lessons about what&#8217;s going on today, but ones you may not have predicted.</p>
<p><a href="http://retroreport.org/test-tube-tomato/">Here</a>, for example, is a piece about a GMO tomato that turned up in supermarkets way back in 1994. People went hysterical over the Flavr Savr tomato, either as an evil plot or the salvation of our food supply. After the cameras were shut off and the reporters went away, the company that made the tomato struggled to make a business out of it and quietly sold their patent off to GMO giant Monsanto, which then quietly shut the project down&#8211;arguably because it was boldly labeled in stores as genetically modified. Since then, Monsanto has gone on to make big profits on GMO plants by making farmers their customers, not consumers.</p>
<p><iframe src="//player.vimeo.com/video/68929952" height="300" width="400" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0"></iframe></p>
<p>Here&#8217;s another piece, called <a href="http://retroreport.org/crack-babies-a-tale-from-the-drug-wars/">Crack Babies</a>. In the 1980s, people got frantically worried that crack-addicted women would give birth to a generation of brain-damaged infants. The idea&#8211;based on some preliminary research&#8211;turned out to be wrong. Yet it became a wildly successful meme, perhaps because it involved a then-new drug and perhaps because crack addicts were mostly poor blacks. Retro Report rightly asks why we never talk about the threat of &#8220;Booze Babies,&#8221; when alcohol is more harmful during pregnancy than crack.</p>
<p><iframe src="//player.vimeo.com/video/66409924?title=0&amp;byline=0&amp;portrait=0&amp;color=1db4c2" height="300" width="400" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0"></iframe></p>
<p>These videos remind us forcefully that the real meaning of stories about science takes time to unfold. That is very hard to remember, because there&#8217;s something intoxicating about a new science story. Suddenly some great truth about the world seems to be unveiled. That truth can be terrifying, or elating. I can&#8217;t count all the emails I&#8217;ve gotten when I&#8217;ve written a story about some very preliminary research on a disease, from people who suffer from the disease and want to know where they can go to get cured.</p>
<p>In reality, a lot of science-related conclusions fall apart or have to be revised in later years. Science itself is starting to grapple with its flaws, with papers like <a href="http://www.plosmedicine.org/article/metrics/info%3Adoi%2F10.1371%2Fjournal.pmed.0020124">&#8220;Most Published Research Findings Are False.&#8221;</a> On the other hand, some findings gain strength over the years, as more and more evidence supports them. But those studies pile up like sand grains, and so it&#8217;s easy for journalists to overlook them, even after they&#8217;ve grown into a mountain.</p>
<p>I hope Retro Report does more investigations into science. They&#8217;re wonderful history lessons, and they also help people think more realistically about today&#8217;s news.</p>
<p>Other science stories include:</p>
<p>&#8211;<a href="http://retroreport.org/summer-of-fire/">Summer of Fire</a>: How this year&#8217;s massive forest fires are part of a 25-year trend, due in part to human activity.</p>
<p>&#8211;<a href="http://retroreport.org/biosphere-2-an-american-odyssey/">Biosphere 2</a>, the sealed building that was supposed to become self-sufficient and instead went wrong in a fascinating way.</p>
<p>&#8211;<a href="http://retroreport.org/y2k-much-ado-about-nothing/">Y2K</a>, the computer bug that terrified the world in 1999 with the prospect of computers shutting down on New Years Day.</p>
<p>&#8211;<a href="http://retroreport.org/voyage-of-the-mobro-4000/">Voyage of the Mobro 4000</a>: an ill-fated voyage of a garbage barge that gave rise to the recycling movement.</p>
<p><em>(P.S.: Retro Report is a <a href="http://retroreport.org/about/">non-profit project</a>. The New York Times, where I&#8217;m a columnist, distributes Retro Report, but I&#8217;ve not had any dealings with them aside from as a viewer.)</em></p>

<p>From my studies of the history of science, I think the main problem is that &#8220;science&#8221; was expanded beyond the careful, limited, solid procedure described by Sir Francis Bacon. Indeed, science is often still described in similar terms, in which data gathering and inductive reasoning inspire imaginative deductive conclusions (I don&#8217;t know how people can say Bacon&#8217;s method was simply inductive logic) which are then tested by attempts at further repeatable observations, with controlled experiments if possible or necessary to distinguish between possibilities and clearly demonstrate what is actually happening in nature.<br />
    About 200 years later, when &#8220;natural philosophers&#8221; started calling themselves &#8220;scientists,&#8221; they had already started accepting a different process, which started with induction and then by simple extrapolation extended the inductive conclusions into the past and to global and celestial scales, accepting the results as facts despite the obvious inability to repeatedly observe or experimentally reproduce such events. Flipping the normal practice of science on its head, the new conclusions were then used to interpret all new data, giving a false appearance of harmonious independent support that was actually the result of circular reasoning. This is most clearly seen in the extreme form of uniformitarian geology which eventually resisted and then was overthrown by the evidence for things such as plate tectonics and J. Harlan Bretz&#8217; Missoula flood.<br />

So is the physiology of reproduction in species from microbes to man, and reproduction occurs via conserved molecular mechanisms that I have detailed with across-species examples in my model. <a href="http://www.socioaffectiveneuroscipsychol.net/index.php/snp/article/view/20553/27989" rel="nofollow">http://www.socioaffectiveneuroscipsychol.net/index.php/snp/article/view/20553/27989</a></p>
<p>If fixation had ever been linked to mutations, but not to nutrient-dependent pheromone-controlled reproduction, the authors might have been able to continue to tout theory. Instead, they refuted mutation-driven evolution but say  &#8220;&#8230;our results show that further empirical work and more theoretical models are required to accurately predict the fate of that allele over long time spans.&#8221; They indicate they are simply waiting to begin touting another theoretical model.</p>
<p>Why do we need more theoretical models to predict the fate of any allele over long time spans? It is perfectly clear that epigenetic effects of the sensory environment arrive via olfactory/pheromonal input and control the nutrient-dependent physiology of reproduction, which controls the fate of alleles across the evolutionary continuum?  </p>
<p>What we need is to simply acknowledge the biological facts. The authors of this article do that, but only in passing. They say: &#8220;Our study confirms&#8230; and highlights [the fact] that the nature of adaptation can be complex.&#8221; That complexity is obviously found in the systems biology of epigenetically effected alternative splicings and fixation of alleles, which is nutrient-dependent and pheromone-controlled, which I began modeling in the early 1990&#8242;s. </p>
<p>Perhaps I should not have assumed that most people would know that new alleles are nutrient-dependent. That assumption led me to think it didn&#8217;t need to be proved, and that most people would accept the fact that fixation was pheromone-controlled, not mutation-driven and mutation-controlled. I was wrong to expect others would accept the facts instead of continuing to accept the theory of mutation-driven evolution.</p>

	</div>
	
</div>	</div>
</li>
