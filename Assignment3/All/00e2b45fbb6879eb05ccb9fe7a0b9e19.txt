			<h1 id="section_0">Stigmergy</h1><ul id="page-actions" class="hlist"><li id="ca-edit"></li><li id="ca-upload"></li><li id="ca-talk"><a href="/wiki/Talk:Stigmergy" title="Discussion about the content page [t]" accesskey="t">Talk</a></li><li id="ca-watch" class="watch-this-article"><a href="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:UserLogin&amp;returnto=Stigmergy" title="Add this page to your watchlist [w]" accesskey="w"></a></li></ul>			<div id="content" class="content">
				<div><p><b>Stigmergy</b> is a mechanism of indirect <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coordination" class="extiw" title="wikt:coordination">coordination</a> between agents or actions.<sup id="cite_ref-mpra.ub.uni-muenchen.de_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpra.ub.uni-muenchen.de-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The principle is that the trace left in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> by an action stimulates the performance of a next action, by the same or a different agent. In that way, subsequent actions tend to reinforce and build on each other, leading to the spontaneous emergence of coherent, apparently systematic activity.</p>
<p>Stigmergy is a form of <a href="/wiki/Self-organization" title="Self-organization">self-organization</a>. It produces complex, seemingly intelligent structures, without need for any planning, control, or even direct communication between the agents. As such it supports efficient collaboration between extremely simple agents, who lack any memory, intelligence or even individual awareness of each other.<sup id="cite_ref-mpra.ub.uni-muenchen.de_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mpra.ub.uni-muenchen.de-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><a href="#editor/1" data-section="1" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>The term "stigmergy" was introduced by French biologist <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Paul_Grass%C3%A9" title="Pierre-Paul Grassé">Pierre-Paul Grassé</a> in 1959 to refer to termite behavior. He defined it as: "Stimulation of workers by the performance they have achieved." It is derived from the Greek words <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CF%84%CE%AF%CE%B3%CE%BC%CE%B1" class="extiw" title="wikt:στίγμα">στίγμα</a> <i>stigma</i> "mark, sign" and <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%94%CF%81%CE%B3%CE%BF%CE%BD" class="extiw" title="wikt:ἔργον">ἔργον</a> <i>ergon</i> "work, action", and captures the notion that an agent’s actions leave signs in the environment, signs that it and other agents sense and that determine and incite their subsequent actions.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Later on, a distinction was made between the stigmergic phenomenon, which is specific to the guidance of additional work, and the more general, non-work specific incitation, for which the term <i>sematectonic</i> communication was coined<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a>, from the Greek words <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%E1%BF%86%CE%BC%CE%B1" class="extiw" title="wikt:σῆμα">σῆμα</a> <i>sema</i> "sign, token", and <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%CE%AD%CE%BA%CF%84%CF%89%CE%BD" class="extiw" title="wikt:τέκτων">τέκτων</a> <i>tecton</i> "craftsman, builder": "There is a need for a more general, somewhat less clumsy expression to denote the evocation of any form of behavior or physiological change by the evidences of work performed by other animals, including the special case of the guidance of additional work."</p>
<p>Stigmergy is now one of the key<sup id="cite_ref-parunak_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parunak-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> concepts in the field of <a href="/wiki/Swarm_intelligence" title="Swarm intelligence">swarm intelligence</a>.</p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stigmergic_behavior_in_lower_organisms">Stigmergic behavior in lower organisms</span><a href="#editor/2" data-section="2" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>Stigmergy was first observed in <a href="/wiki/Social_insects" title="Social insects" class="mw-redirect">social insects</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a> exchange information by laying down <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a> (the trace) on their way back to the nest when they have found food. In that way, they collectively develop a <a href="/wiki/Complex_network" title="Complex network">complex network</a> of trails, connecting the nest in the most efficient way to the different food sources. When ants come out of the nest searching for food, they are stimulated by the pheromone to follow the trail towards the food source. The network of trails functions as a shared external memory for the ant colony.</p>
<p>In computer science, this general method has been applied in a variety of techniques called <a href="/wiki/Ant_colony_optimization" title="Ant colony optimization" class="mw-redirect">ant colony optimization</a>, which search for solutions to complex problems by depositing "virtual pheromones" along paths that appear promising.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Other <a href="/wiki/Eusocial" title="Eusocial" class="mw-redirect">eusocial</a> creatures, such as <a href="/wiki/Termites" title="Termites" class="mw-redirect">termites</a>, use pheromones to build their complex nests by following a simple <a href="/wiki/Decentralized" title="Decentralized" class="mw-redirect">decentralized</a> <a href="/wiki/Rule_set" title="Rule set" class="mw-redirect">rule set</a>. Each insect scoops up a 'mudball' or similar material from its environment, invests the ball with pheromones, and deposits it on the ground, initially in a random spot. However, termites are attracted to their nestmates' pheromones and are therefore more likely to drop their own mudballs on top of their neighbors'. The larger the heap of mud becomes, the more attractive it is, and therefore the more mud will be added to it (positive feedback). Over time this leads to the construction of pillars, arches, tunnels and chambers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stigmergy has even been observed in bacteria, various species of which differentiate into distinct cell types and which participate in group behaviors that are guided by sophisticated temporal and spatial control systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Shapiro1988_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shapiro1988-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Spectacular examples of multicellular behavior can be found among the <a href="/wiki/Myxobacteria" title="Myxobacteria">myxobacteria</a>. Myxobacteria travel in <i><a href="/wiki/Swarm" title="Swarm" class="mw-redirect">swarms</a></i> containing many <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> kept together by intercellular molecular <a href="/wiki/Signal_transduction" title="Signal transduction">signals</a>. Most myxobacteria are predatory: individuals benefit from aggregation as it allows accumulation of extracellular <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> which are used to digest prey microorganisms. When nutrients are scarce, myxobacterial cells aggregate into <i>fruiting bodies</i>, within which the swarming cells transform themselves into dormant myxospores with thick cell walls. The fruiting process is thought to benefit myxobacteria by ensuring that <a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">cell growth</a> is resumed with a group (swarm) of myxobacteria, rather than isolated cells. Similar life cycles have developed among the cellular <a href="/wiki/Slime_mold" title="Slime mold">slime molds</a>. The best known of the myxobacteria, <i><a href="/wiki/Myxococcus_xanthus" title="Myxococcus xanthus">Myxococcus xanthus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Stigmatella_aurantiaca" title="Stigmatella aurantiaca">Stigmatella aurantiaca</a></i>, are studied in various laboratories as prokaryotic models of development.<sup id="cite_ref-Dworkin2007_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dworkin2007-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stigmergic_behavior_in_social_movements">Stigmergic behavior in social movements</span><a href="#editor/3" data-section="3" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>Stigmergy also occurs with social movements, such as the arc from <a href="/wiki/Wikileaks" title="Wikileaks" class="mw-redirect">Wikileaks</a>’ cable release in Summer 2010 to the developments in global <a href="/wiki/Occupy_movement" title="Occupy movement">Occupy movement</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The steady rise of <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Open_Source_software" title="Open Source software" class="mw-redirect">Open Source software</a> movement has been one of the big surprises of the 21st century, threatening stalwarts such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Britannica" title="Britannica" class="mw-redirect">Britannica</a>, while simultaneously offering insights into the emergence of large-scale peer production and the growth of <a href="/wiki/Gift_economy" title="Gift economy">gift economy</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>New <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> shall be based on <a href="/wiki/Systems" title="Systems" class="mw-redirect">systems</a>, not land mass, and that governance be by user groups, not <a href="/wiki/Elected_officials" title="Elected officials" class="mw-redirect">elected officials</a>. Stigmergy is the most effective way for those user groups to govern systems. Systems are currently primarily run by competitive <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organizations</a>. <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">Competition</a> creates redundancy, is slow and wastes resources on idea <a href="/wiki/Protection" title="Protection">protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Advertisement" title="Advertisement" class="mw-redirect">advertisement</a>, and more. The alternative to competition has traditionally been <a href="/wiki/Collaboration" title="Collaboration">collaboration</a>. This is most effective only in groups of two to eight people. With stigmergy, an initial idea is freely given, and the project is driven by the idea, not by a personality or group of personalities. No individual needs permission (competitive) or consensus (collaborative) to propose an idea or initiate a project.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, after <a href="/wiki/David_Graeber" title="David Graeber">David Graeber</a> studying Betafo, a rural community in Madagascar made up of the descendants of nobles and their slaves. Because of spending cuts mandated by the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>, the central government had abandoned the area, leaving the inhabitants to fend for themselves. They did, creating an egalitarian society where 10,000 people made decisions by stimergic <a href="/wiki/Consensus" title="Consensus" class="mw-redirect">consensus</a>. When necessary, criminal justice was carried out by a mob, but even there a particular sort of consensus pertained: a lynching required permission from the accused’s parents. Professor Graeber launch a worldwide revolution - <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Movement" title="Occupy Movement" class="mw-redirect">Occupy Movement</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><a href="#editor/4" data-section="4" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>Stigmergy is not restricted to eusocial creatures, or even to physical systems. On the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> there are many collective projects where users interact only by modifying local parts of their shared virtual environment. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> is an example of this.<sup id="cite_ref-Rheingold_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rheingold-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> The massive structure of information available in a <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-burbeck_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burbeck-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> or an <a href="/wiki/Open_source_software" title="Open source software" class="mw-redirect">open source software</a> project such as the <a href="/wiki/FreeBSD_kernel" title="FreeBSD kernel" class="mw-redirect">FreeBSD kernel</a><sup id="cite_ref-burbeck_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burbeck-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> could be compared to a <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termite</a> nest; one initial user leaves a seed of an idea (a mudball) which attracts other users who then build upon and modify this initial concept, eventually constructing an elaborate structure of connected thoughts.<sup id="cite_ref-heylighen_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heylighen-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rodriguez_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rodriguez-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The term is also employed in experimental research in <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> multi-<a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">agent</a> systems and communication in <a href="/wiki/Computer_networks" title="Computer networks" class="mw-redirect">computer networks</a>. In these fields there exist two types of stigmergy: active and passive. The first kind occurs when a robotic or otherwise "intelligent" "agent" alters its environment so as to affect the sensory input of another agent. The second occurs when an agent's action alters its environment such that the environmental changes made by a different agent are also modified. A typical example of active stigmergy is leaving behind artifacts for others to pick up or follow. An example of passive stigmergy is when one agent tries to remove all artifacts from a container, while another agent tries to fill the container completely.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In addition the concept of stigmergy has also been used to describe how cooperative work such as building design may be integrated. Designing a large contemporary building involves a large and diverse network of actors (e.g. architects, building engineers, static engineers, building services engineers and etc.). Their distributed activities may be partly integrated through practices of stigmergy.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
