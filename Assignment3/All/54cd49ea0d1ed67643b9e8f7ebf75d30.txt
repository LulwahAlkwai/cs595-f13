			<span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://blogs.scientificamerican.com/guest-blog/2013/08/21/understanding-why-science-research-is-translated-into-news-a-survey-for-journalists-bloggers/&media=http://blogs.scientificamerican.com/guest-blog/files/2013/08/IMG_5440.jpg&description=Understanding Why Science Research Is Translated into News: A Survey for Journalists, Bloggers')">
			</span>
		</span></a>When it comes to science in the news, many scientists  lament poor quality of news coverage of scientific studies. Over-claiming  headlines. Lack of understanding of the scientific method. Scientific  findings placed outside of their context.</p>
<p>But perhaps we cant fully understand the sources  of hype or misinformation in science news coverage until we better understand  the rules journalists use for selection and production of science news  studies. Why does one scientific study make it into the news, and another  not? Why does one scientific press release catch a journalist&#8217;s eye,  and another not?</p>
<p>This is precisely the question I am trying to answer  with a new science communication project for my PhD research at Louisiana  State University. For this project, <a href="http://www.scilogs.com/from_the_lab_bench/what-makes-science-research-get-translated-into-news-a-survey/" target="_blank">I have  created a  survey</a> that aims to answer questions related to how science research and  press releases are translated into science news.</p>
<p>If you are a journalist, blogger, freelance writer,  magazine writer, TV producer, radio announcer, podcast producer, or  anything in between, I&#8217;m asking you to participate in this online survey.  By participating in this survey, which only takes 15 minutes to complete,  journalists, bloggers and other communicators can help me understand  when and why science makes its way from research publication to news  story.</p>
<p>By participating in this survey, you also have the  chance to win a $20 Starbucks gift card as the 100th, 200th, e<a name="0.1__GoBack"></a>tc.  participant.</p>
<p>To participate, simply copy and paste the following URL into a new  browser window:</p>
<p><a href="https://lsucommunications.qualtrics.com/SE/?SID=SV_0PTVlA7OUCLqkyV" target="_blank">https://lsucommunications.qualtrics.com/SE/?SID=SV_0PTVlA7OUCLqkyV</a></p>
<p>This survey will hopefully be translated itself into  a peer-reviewed research paper that will help other communication scholars  understand the whys of science news story selection. But in order to make  that happen, I need your help! Please spread the word about this survey,  link to it on Facebook and Twitter, and send it to your journalism colleagues.</p>
<p>Thank you for your participation!</p>
