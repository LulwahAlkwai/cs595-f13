<section class='article-content'>

<p><a href="http://mashable.com/category/apple/">Apple</a> <a href="http://mashable.com/category/ipad/">iPads</a>, <a href="http://mashable.com/category/iphone/">iPhones</a> and <a href="http://mashable.com/category/ipod/">iPods</a> have been around long enough that some of the newer versions of iOS no longer work on them. Case in point: A first-generation iPad works with iOS 5.1.1, but nothing above. This also means that some apps, which developers are constantly updating, no longer work on legacy iOS versions and the hardware that supports them. Until now.</p>
<p>On Tuesday, Apple quietly introduced a new way for consumers to access previous App editions for their aging hardware. It works quite simply: You select the app you want in the <a href="http://mashable.com/category/app-store/">App Store</a> and, if it's incompatible with your iOS version, a prompt will appear offering to let you download a previous, compatible version of the app.</p>
<div class="see-also"><p>See also: <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/05/22/ipad-apps-free/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">The 25 Best Free iPad Apps</a></p></div>
<p>That version will lack certain features found in the newest build and it's unlikely Apple or app developers will let you download a version that includes bugs. Even so, it's quite likely that you'll have a fully functional version of your favorite app for your orphaned hardware. Apple, by the way, is not saying how many versions of apps the App Store will store, but the company did confirm that there will be multiple versions of the apps.</p>
<p>For this writer the new feature is a welcome bit of coincidence. Just days ago, I had to <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/09/16/ipad-tech-disaster/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Restore my iPad Gen 1</a>, losing much of my app data and finding, to my dismay, that apps like <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/07/14/doodlely-update/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Doodle.ly</a> and <a href="http://mashable.com/category/google-maps/">Google Maps</a> would not install on an <a href="http://mashable.com/category/ios-5/">iOS 5.1.1</a> device — they demanded <a href="http://mashable.com/category/ios-6/">iOS 6</a>. That all changes today. </p>
<p>This is an especially smart move for Apple and its app developer partners as the company prepares to roll out <a href="http://mashable.com/category/ios-7/">iOS 7</a> updates to millions of device users. Obviously, that also means app developers are all upgrading their apps. Now you don’t have to worry that these software companies will develop you out of relevance: No, there will almost always be a legacy version of the app, just waiting for your iOS 5 or iOS 6 device.</p>
