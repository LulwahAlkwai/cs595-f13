<section class='article-content'>

<p>Samsung’s new <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/09/04/hands-on-samsung-galaxy-gear-smart-watch/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Galaxy Gear smart watch</a> got a lot of press and attention a few weeks ago, and you probably think you know all there is to know about the technology giant’s first wearable gadget and its plans for on-your-wrist technology. We bet you don’t.</p>
<p>Here are some interesting facts and insights, shared with us by Samsung execs, about the smart watch and what Samsung is considering for its future in the potential $16 billion wearable technology market.</p>
<h3>1. Get Ready for the Apps</h3>
<p><a href="http://mashable.com/category/samsung/">Samsung</a> expects that there will be 70 third-party apps ready for the Galaxy Gear when it launches in the U.S. in October. When <em>Mashable</em> quizzed Samsung execs on whether apps are ported or built, they acknowledged that they're mostly built. Samsung Mobile’s Chief Product Officer Kevin Packingham, however, clarified: Since the apps are all Android-based, “it’s typically slimming it down, not a complete rewrite.”</p>
<p>As for where the rest of the social network apps are (at the unveiling the Gear only had Path), Ryan Bidan, Director of Product Marketing for Samsung Mobile, told us, “There are safe expectations that additional social networks are coming in short order.”</p>
<h3>2. The APIs Are Coming</h3>
<p>While the Galaxy Gear can already take pictures and sense its own movement and orientation, the initial set of apps and connected devices (namely just one, the <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/09/04/galaxy-note-3-hands-on/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Samsung Galaxy Note 3</a>) only scratch the surface of what’s possible with the device. Samsung plans to deliver a set of open APIs to developers so they can engage with all the sensors on the Galaxy Gear and leverage its persistent connection to the phone.  Packingham could not provide an API delivery date, though.</p>
<h3>3. Sensors Are the Same</h3>
<p>Even though the Samsung Galaxy Gear is part of a new generation of wearable tech, its guts are more similar to a traditional smartphone – at least when it comes to sensors. Packingham told <em>Mashable</em> that the Gear’s sensors are the same as what you’d find in one of their phones, though it lacks a compass.</p>
<p>We asked why the Gear doesn't include something like a heart-rate meter –- a perfect fit for wearable tech. Packingham explained that the regulations required for those types of sensors are more intense. They require “the next level of accuracy.” Plus, if consumers want to use them in some sort of medical-advisory role, that puts an additional burden on Samsung.</p>
<h3>4. Why This Smart Watch Can Succeed</h3>
<p>When we asked how the Gear can succeed where, for instance, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smart_Personal_Objects_Technology" target="_blank">Microsoft Spot</a> watches failed, Packingham first offered an admittedly trite answer, “The technology available to us makes a big difference,” adding, “These devices are genuinely intelligent.” He also noted that they run actual operating systems (Android) and have access to far more real-time data than their ill-fated predecessors. They’re also more adaptable and flexible, putting for instance, your social network on your wrist. <span class="microcontent" data-fragment="original-smart-watches-had" data-description="Original smart watches had limited technology and were, in essence, “one-trick ponies.”" data-micro="1">Original smart watches had limited technology and were, in essence, “one-trick ponies.”</span></p>
<p>According to Bidan, who was at Microsoft during the Spot era, “the technology is finally catching up to the expectations.” They also believe the smartphone era has helped train and prepare consumers for this new age of smart watches.</p>
<h3>5. The Band Is Full of Technology</h3>
<p>There’s no missing the 1.9-megapixel camera on the Galaxy Gear’s wrist band, but did you know that the band also includes a speaker on the clasp? </p>
<p>When we quizzed Samsung on the somewhat surprising decision to place the camera in the part of the watch that takes the most wear and tear, Bidan said, “There is mental sidestepping because it happens to be in the form factor of a watch. If it wasn’t a watch, we would not be talking about the band.”</p>
<p>In other words, Samsung would like of us to think more about the Gear as a piece of wearable tech that has technology in all the places necessary to be truly useful. So, for example, the speaker in the clasp works in concert with the noise-canceling microphones in the watch body, allowing you to hold the watch up near your face to conduct a phone call (routed through your smartphone).</p>
<p>Packingham also revealed that while the customer-care plan for the Gear should be similar to what Samsung offers for a phone, if your Galaxy Gear band is broken or damaged, “a simple change of the band in the store is unlikely. You do need tools for repair.”</p>
<h3>6. You Can Ignore a Call by Placing Your Hand Over the Phone</h3>
<p>Samsung designed the Galaxy Gear to be a glanceable device. If you get a phone call on your Galaxy Note 3, the alert will appear on the watch. You can glance at your wrist to see who is calling. If you don’t want to take the call, you just cover the phone with your hand. (Your palm does have to touch the screen.) When we asked how the phone knows the difference between, say, two fingers and a palm, Bidan credited the math behind touch technology, but couldn’t clarify if the device truly recognizes the difference.</p>
<h3>7. Samsung Is Targeting Customers, Not Women</h3>
<p>Samsung’s gotten <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/09/05/galaxy-gear-fashion-fail/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">some flack</a> over the size and design limitations of the Galaxy Gear, especially from some fashion-conscious women and those with particularly small wrists. But when we asked about the possibility of designing a “female first” smart watch, Bidan joked “‘Female first’ is a scary rat hole to go down.”</p>
<p>Bidan noted that the Galaxy Gear is just one device. “Samsung isn’t a company that releases <i>A</i> device,” explained Bidan acknowledging the company’s proclivity for delivering multiple products in a category.</p>
<h3>8. Samsung Will Produce More Wearables</h3>
<p>“Will there be additional Gear devices and wearables in our future? Sure,” Bidan told us. A bag is a possibility. Essentially “you could put it anywhere. It’s a screen with some tech behind it.”</p>
<h3>9. Start Charging With a Tap</h3>
<p>Samsung's Galaxy Gear is designed to survive at least a day on a single charge, though Packingham insists you can extend battery life "quite a bit based on how you configure your device." However, it will likely need to charge overnight, every, or every other night. To accomplish this task, you'll use the included charging device, which literally wraps around the gear body and snaps closed. It has a USB charging port in the base and copper connectors inside that match up with the exposed copper on the base of the Gear. The charger also has built-in NFC, so, according to Samsung, you simply tap to pair.</p>
<p>Do you still have questions about Samsung's Galaxy Gear? Expect more answers in our upcoming review. In the meantime, feel free to drop your questions and comments in the space below.</p>
