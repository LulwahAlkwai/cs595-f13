			<h1 id="section_0">Detection theory</h1><ul id="page-actions" class="hlist"><li id="ca-edit"></li><li id="ca-upload"></li><li id="ca-talk"><a href="/wiki/Talk:Detection_theory" title="Discussion about the content page [t]" accesskey="t">Talk</a></li><li id="ca-watch" class="watch-this-article"><a href="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:UserLogin&amp;returnto=Detection+theory" title="Add this page to your watchlist [w]" accesskey="w"></a></li></ul>			<div id="content" class="content">
				<div><p><b>Detection theory</b>, or <b>signal detection theory</b>, is a means to quantify the ability to discern between information-bearing patterns (called <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(psychology)" title="Stimulus (psychology)">stimulus</a> in humans, <a href="/wiki/Signal_(electronics)" title="Signal (electronics)" class="mw-redirect">signal</a> in machines) and random patterns that distract from the information (called <a href="/wiki/Noise_(electronics)" title="Noise (electronics)">noise</a>, consisting of background stimuli and random activity of the detection machine and of the nervous system of the operator). In the field of <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>, the separation of such patterns from a disguising background is referred to as <i><b>signal recovery</b></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilmshurst_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilmshurst-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to the theory, there are a number of determiners of how a detecting system will detect a signal, and where its threshold levels will be. The theory can explain how changing the threshold will affect the ability to discern, often exposing how adapted the system is to the task, purpose or goal at which it is aimed.</p>
<p>When the detecting system is a human being, experience, expectations, physiological state (e.g. fatigue) and other factors can affect the threshold applied. For instance, a sentry in wartime will likely detect fainter stimuli than the same sentry in peacetime due to a lower criterion, however they will also be more likely to treat innocuous stimuli as a threat.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Much of the early work in detection theory was done by <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1954 the theory was fully developed on the theoretical side as described by <a href="/wiki/W._Wesley_Peterson" title="W. Wesley Peterson">Peterson</a>, Birdsall and Fox<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the foundation for the psychological theory was made by Wilson P. Tanner, David M. Green, and <a href="/wiki/John_A._Swets" title="John A. Swets">John A. Swets</a>, also in 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Detection theory was used in 1966 by <a href="/wiki/John_A._Swets" title="John A. Swets">John A. Swets</a> and David M. Green for <a href="/wiki/Psychophysics" title="Psychophysics">psychophysics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Green and <a href="/wiki/John_A._Swets" title="John A. Swets">Swets</a> criticized the traditional methods of psychophysics for their inability to discriminate between the real sensitivity of subjects and their (potential) <a href="/wiki/Response_bias" title="Response bias">response biases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Green.26Swets_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green.26Swets-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Detection theory has applications in many fields such as <a href="/wiki/Diagnostics" title="Diagnostics" class="mw-redirect">diagnostics</a> of any kind, <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect">telecommunications</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>. The concept is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Signal_to_noise_ratio" title="Signal to noise ratio" class="mw-redirect">signal to noise ratio</a> used in the sciences and <a href="/wiki/Confusion_matrix" title="Confusion matrix">confusion matrices</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>. It is also usable in <a href="/wiki/Alarm_management" title="Alarm management">alarm management</a>, where it is important to separate important events from <a href="/wiki/Background_noise" title="Background noise">background</a> <a href="/wiki/Noise_(environmental)" title="Noise (environmental)" class="mw-redirect">noise</a>.</p>

</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology">Psychology</span><a href="#editor/1" data-section="1" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>Signal detection theory (SDT) is used when psychologists want to measure the way we make decisions under conditions of uncertainty, such as how we would perceive distances in foggy conditions. SDT assumes that the decision maker is not a passive receiver of information, but an active decision-maker who makes difficult perceptual judgements under conditions of uncertainty. In foggy circumstances, we are forced to decide how far away from us an object is, based solely upon visual stimulus which is impaired by the fog. Since the brightness of the object, such as a traffic light, is used by the brain to discriminate the distance of an object, and the fog reduces the brightness of objects, we perceive the object to be much farther away than it actually is (see also <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">decision theory</a>).</p>
<p>To apply signal detection theory to a data set where stimuli were either present or absent, and the observer categorized each trial as having the stimulus present or absent, the trials are sorted into one of four categories:</p>

<p>Signal detection theory can also be applied to memory experiments, where items are presented on a study list for later testing. A test list is created by combining these 'old' items with novel, 'new' items that did not appear on the study list. On each test trial the subject will respond 'yes, this was on the study list' or 'no, this was not on the study list'. Items presented on the study list are called Targets, and new items are called Distractors. Saying 'Yes' to a target constitutes a Hit, while saying 'Yes' to a distractor constitutes a False Alarm.</p>

</dl></div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><a href="#editor/2" data-section="2" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>Signal Detection Theory has wide application, both in humans and <a href="/wiki/Comparative_psychology" title="Comparative psychology">other animals</a>. Topics include <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>, stimulus characterists of schedules of reinforcement, etc.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensitivity_or_discriminability">Sensitivity or discriminability</span><a href="#editor/3" data-section="3" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h3>
<p>Conceptually, sensitivity refers to how hard or easy it is to detect that a target stimulus is present from background events. For example, in a recognition memory paradigm, having longer to study to-be-remembered words makes it easier to recognize previously seen or heard words. In contrast, having to remember 30 words rather than 5 makes the discrimination harder. One of the most commonly used statistics for computing sensitivity is the so-called <a href="/wiki/D%27" title="D'">sensitivity index</a>, or <i><a href="/wiki/D%27" title="D'">d'</a></i>. There are also <a href="/wiki/Non-parametric" title="Non-parametric" class="mw-redirect">non-parametric</a> measures, such as the area under the <a href="/wiki/Receiver_operating_characteristic" title="Receiver operating characteristic">ROC-curve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Green.26Swets_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green.26Swets-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bias">Bias</span><a href="#editor/4" data-section="4" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h3>
<p>Bias is the extent to which one response is more probable than another. That is, a receiver may be more likely to respond that a stimulus is present or more likely to respond that a stimulus is not present. Bias is independent of sensitivity. For example, if there is a penalty for either false alarms or misses, this may influence bias. If the stimulus is a bomber, then a miss (failing to detect the plane) may increase deaths, so a liberal bias is likely. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Crying_wolf" title="Crying wolf" class="mw-redirect">crying wolf</a> (a false alarm) too often may make people less likely to respond, grounds for a conservative bias.</p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">Mathematics</span><a href="#editor/5" data-section="5" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="P.28H1.7Cy.29_.3E_P.28H2.7Cy.29_.2F_MAP_Testing">P(H1|y) &gt; P(H2|y) / MAP Testing</span><a href="#editor/6" data-section="6" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h3>
<p>In the case of making a decision between two <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypotheses</a>, <i>H1</i>, absent, and <i>H2</i>, present, in the event of a particular <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a>, <i>y</i>, a classical approach is to choose <i>H1</i> when <i>p(H1|y) &gt; p(H2|y)</i> and <i>H2</i> in the reverse case.<sup id="cite_ref-Schonhoff_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schonhoff-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In the event that the two <i><a href="/wiki/A_posteriori" title="A posteriori" class="mw-redirect">a posteriori</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probabilities</a> are equal, one typically defaults to a single choice, say <i>H2</i>. One could also flip a coin although the expected number of errors would be the same.</p>
<p>When taking this approach, usually what one knows are the conditional probabilities, <i>p(y|H1)</i> and <i>p(y|H2)</i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> probabilities <img class="tex" alt="p(H1) = \pi_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/c/8/2c81f87d941130f628af86b14c13072f.png"> and <img class="tex" alt="p(H2) = \pi_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/f/7/cf717da47c6f07363e0180e931eef020.png">. In this case,</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="p(H1|y) = \frac{p(y|H1) \cdot \pi_1}{p(y)} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/3/6/5360d174e856b34226f8a6a81729bbba.png">,</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="p(H2|y) = \frac{p(y|H2) \cdot \pi_2}{p(y)} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/0/5/e05d6f0312ebb7cacbb152ffe0cf07e0.png"></p>
<p>where <i>p(y)</i> is the total probability of event <i>y</i>,</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" p(y|H1) \cdot \pi_1 + p(y|H2) \cdot \pi_2 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/b/7/0b7e15fef22469568ea57974bf579aeb.png">.</p>
<p><i>H2</i> is chosen in case</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" \frac{p(y|H2) \cdot \pi_2}{p(y|H1) \cdot \pi_1 + p(y|H2) \cdot \pi_2} \ge \frac{p(y|H1) \cdot \pi_1}{p(y|H1) \cdot \pi_1 + p(y|H2) \cdot \pi_2} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/7/2/172453a9c9eae1323dc087f124511692.png"></p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" \Rightarrow \frac{p(y|H2)}{p(y|H1)} \ge \frac{\pi_1}{\pi_2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/c/5/dc50782d3b196bc35f6664367faf115b.png"></p>
<p>and <i>H1</i> otherwise.</p>
<p>Often, the ratio <img class="tex" alt="\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/9/0/1903337c55b43a6e5d42ef3cb261ca28.png"> is called <img class="tex" alt="\tau_{MAP}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/6/e/c6e5ebf8121367203261ed97fa699277.png"> and <img class="tex" alt="\frac{p(y|H2)}{p(y|H1)}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/d/9/ed94a41a85d98152e8ba96e5c07123e9.png"> is called <img class="tex" alt="L(y)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/e/a/7ea1f8c67cfa9f5ba278d734022aed36.png">, the <i><a href="/wiki/Likelihood_ratio" title="Likelihood ratio">likelihood ratio</a></i>.</p>
<p>Using this terminology, <i>H2</i> is chosen in case <img class="tex" alt="L(y) \ge \tau_{MAP}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/c/7/cc7d389658d8b1ff2d25b7749a6e73c5.png">. This is called MAP testing, where MAP stands for "maximum <i>a posteriori</i>").</p>
<p>Taking this approach minimizes the expected number of errors one will make.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bayes_Criterion">Bayes Criterion</span><a href="#editor/7" data-section="7" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h3>
<p>In some cases, it is far more important to respond appropriately to <i>H1</i> than it is to respond appropriately to <i>H2</i>. For example, if one is trying to detect an incoming bomber known to be carrying a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapon</a>, it is much more important to shoot down the bomber if it is there, than it is not to send a fighter squadron to inspect a false alarm (assuming a large supply of fighter squadrons). The <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bayes" title="Thomas Bayes">Bayes</a> criterion is an approach suitable for such cases.<sup id="cite_ref-Schonhoff_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schonhoff-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Here a <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">utility</a> is associated with each of four situations:</p>
<ul><li><img class="tex" alt="U_{11}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/0/2709737db362314e4758bc51582696c6.png">: One responds with behavior appropriate to H1 and H1 is true: fighters destroy bomber, incurring fuel, maintenance, and weapons costs, take risk of some being shot down;</li>
<li><img class="tex" alt="U_{12}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/a/f/1aff0fe3737785c57703f8ca924a9efc.png">: One responds with behavior appropriate to H1 and H2 is true: fighters sent out, incurring fuel and maintenance costs, bomber location remains unknown;</li>
<li><img class="tex" alt="U_{21}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/7/0/4703d3c412e285cb47e9e2db5252b47c.png">: One responds with behavior appropriate to H2 and H1 is true: city destroyed;</li>
<li><img class="tex" alt="U_{22}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/2/0/120931159634824e104814469f3bf71f.png">: One responds with behavior appropriate to H2 and H2 is true: fighters stay home, bomber location remains unknown;</li>
</ul><p>As is shown below, what is important are the differences, <img class="tex" alt="U_{11} - U_{21}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/d/9/0d9bd872f9d615d00935f9775fd84db3.png"> and <img class="tex" alt="U_{22} - U_{12}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/7/c/57c45866f1ad8f61d14861c4cfdbee75.png">.</p>
<p>Similarly, there are four probabilities, <img class="tex" alt="P_{11}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/f/6/0f6cda8d30d77ab362ff54ac94fba3ca.png">, <img class="tex" alt="P_{12}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/d/d/edd41c20d7307002b981051515083ee0.png">, etc., for each of the cases (which are dependent on one's decision strategy).</p>
<p>The Bayes criterion approach is to maximize the expected utility:</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" U = P_{11} \cdot U_{11} + P_{21} \cdot U_{21} + P_{12} \cdot U_{12} + P_{22} \cdot U_{22} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/3/b/d3b637ffa38f8973acb6f12c47d3c9aa.png"></p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" U = P_{11} \cdot U_{11} + (1-P_{11}) \cdot U_{21} + P_{12} \cdot U_{21} + (1-P_{12}) \cdot U_{22} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/3/7/a3741c174489789b76721287d1411879.png"></p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" U = U_{12} + U_{21} + P_{11} \cdot (U_{11} - U_{21}) - P_{12} \cdot (U_{22} - U_{12}) " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/b/7ab29faade9e60a124c1e5521a1815b5.png"></p>
<p>Effectively, one may maximize the sum,</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="U' = P_{11} \cdot (U_{11} - U_{21}) - P_{12} \cdot (U_{22} - U_{12}) " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/6/8/96815a799ae5421791906f30cc12224b.png">,</p>
<p>and make the following substitutions:</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="P_{11} = \pi_1 \cdot \int_{R_1}p(y|H1)\, dy " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/6/c/26c36ee9e29997a0afb91eefb19598fe.png"></p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="P_{12} = \pi_2 \cdot \int_{R_1}p(y|H2)\, dy " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/e/c/dec53b7ec1467ee980bccffb83443abc.png"></p>
<p>where <img class="tex" alt="\pi_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/b/23bbd698ad7518cd800cc57921436e96.png"> and <img class="tex" alt="\pi_2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/8/6/38680b6a1bf1f27d2587dc33df489cde.png"> are the <i>a priori</i> probabilities, <img class="tex" alt="P(H1)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/b/a/0ba844424e7784e97fa22106b5a77621.png"> and <img class="tex" alt="P(H2)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/6/a/26a6a58d80b7cd2aca16d95bbdf75f04.png">, and <img class="tex" alt="R_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/4/be473692ca1cbc48985e5e93af6755bf.png"> is the region of observation events, <i>y</i>, that are responded to as though <i>H1</i> is true.</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" \Rightarrow U' = \int_{R_1} \left \{ \pi_1 \cdot (U_{11} - U_{21}) \cdot p(y|H1) - \pi_2 \cdot (U_{22} - U_{12}) \cdot p(y|H2) \right \} \, dy " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/b/b/bbb937f552518510a5893200e0e070e5.png"></p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="U'" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/7/0/2701bdca83dc42ed0ced6e5975b65f43.png"> and thus <img class="tex" alt="U" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/c/6/4c614360da93c0a041b22e537de151eb.png"> are maximized by extending <img class="tex" alt="R_1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/e/4/be473692ca1cbc48985e5e93af6755bf.png"> over the region where</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="\pi_1 \cdot (U_{11} - U_{21}) \cdot p(y|H1) - \pi_2 \cdot (U_{22} - U_{12}) \cdot p(y|H2) &gt; 0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/0/3/403342b003c5fd978a2f8e0434495686.png"></p>
<p>This is accomplished by deciding H2 in case</p>
<p><img class="tex" alt="\pi_2 \cdot (U_{22} - U_{12}) \cdot p(y|H2) \ge \pi_1 \cdot (U_{11} - U_{21}) \cdot p(y|H1) " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/2/c/f2c80f033be192fb1009c439206a5403.png"></p>
<p><img class="tex" alt=" \Rightarrow L(y) \equiv \frac{p(y|H2)}{p(y|H1)} \ge \frac{\pi_1 \cdot (U_{11} - U_{21})}{\pi_2 \cdot (U_{22} - U_{12})} \equiv \tau_B " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/a/5/7a52a6fff8141d9fe089ba55f5d68395.png"></p>
<p>and H1 otherwise, where <i>L(y)</i> is the so-defined <i><a href="/wiki/Likelihood_ratio" title="Likelihood ratio">likelihood ratio</a></i>.</p>
