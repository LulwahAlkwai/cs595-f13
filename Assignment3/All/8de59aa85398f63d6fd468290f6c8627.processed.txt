Cryptography and the NSA

   Back at Northwestern I occasionally taught an undergraduate
   cryptography class since I was the local expert in the field (a
   statement not even remotely true at Georgia Tech). I would cover the
   [5]Advanced Encryption Standard, an implementation of a one-way
   key-based permutation. AES had many components included an
   [6]S-Box that seems like a random shuffle but is actually based on the
   inverse of a polynomial. One sentence in the textbook of [7]Trappe and
   Washington made the following point (page 161).

     The S-box was constructed in an explicit and simple algebraic way so
     as to avoid any suspicions of trapdoors built into the algorithm.

   Really? Can't we trust the government not to put back doors into our
   standardized cryptographic algorithms?
   After reading last week's New York Times [8]article on the NSA, I
   realize my naivety. The NYT article doesn't go into how and which
   protocols the NSA has their hand in but I now understand the concern.
   It doesn't look like the NSA has actually broken cryptographic
   protocols, have a [9]secret quantum-like computer in their basement or
   polynomial-time algorithms for SAT. I could go on for pages but Scott
   has done an [10]excellent job talking about the complexity issues
   involved. They've more likely found ways to access your information
   before it has been encrypted or after its been decrypted.
   Matthew Green wrote a [11]nice post speculating on what the NSA might
   be able to do, so nice that it caused some [12]controversy at Johns
   Hopkins.
   The whole Snowden affair gives us a glimpse into the NSA but they
   [13]hide their capabilities well and we'll never know the full extent
   of their knowledge.
