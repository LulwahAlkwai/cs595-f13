			<h1 id="section_0">Dissipative system</h1><ul id="page-actions" class="hlist"><li id="ca-edit"></li><li id="ca-upload"></li><li id="ca-talk"><a href="/wiki/Talk:Dissipative_system" title="Discussion about the content page [t]" accesskey="t">Talk</a></li><li id="ca-watch" class="watch-this-article"><a href="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:UserLogin&amp;returnto=Dissipative+system" title="Add this page to your watchlist [w]" accesskey="w"></a></li></ul>			<div id="content" class="content">

</tr></table><p>A <b>dissipative system</b> is a thermodynamically <a href="/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)" title="Open system (systems theory)">open system</a> which is operating out of, and often far from, <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a> in an environment with which it exchanges <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>.</p>
<p>A <b>dissipative structure</b> is a dissipative system that has a dynamical r√©gime that is in some sense in a reproducible steady state. This reproducible steady state may be reached by natural evolution of the system, by artifice, or by a combination of these two.</p>

</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><a href="#editor/1" data-section="1" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipative</a> structure is characterized by the spontaneous appearance of symmetry breaking (<a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">anisotropy</a>) and the formation of complex, sometimes <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a>, structures where interacting particles exhibit long range correlations. The term <i>dissipative structure</i> was coined by Russian-Belgian physical chemist <a href="/wiki/Ilya_Prigogine" title="Ilya Prigogine">Ilya Prigogine</a>, who was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 1977 for his pioneering work on these structures. The dissipative structures considered by Prigogine have dynamical r√©gimes that can be regarded as thermodynamically steady states, and sometimes at least can be described by suitable <a href="/wiki/Extremal_principles_in_non-equilibrium_thermodynamics" title="Extremal principles in non-equilibrium thermodynamics">extremal principles in non-equilibrium thermodynamics</a>.</p>
<p>Examples in every day life include <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living organisms</a>. Less common examples include <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9nard_cells" title="B√©nard cells" class="mw-redirect">B√©nard cells</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Belousov%E2%80%93Zhabotinsky_reaction" title="Belousov‚ÄìZhabotinsky reaction">Belousov‚ÄìZhabotinsky reaction</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>One way of mathematically modeling a dissipative system is given in the article on <i><a href="/wiki/Wandering_set" title="Wandering set">wandering sets</a></i>: it involves the action of a <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measurable set</a>.</p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_control_theory">In control theory</span><a href="#editor/2" data-section="2" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>In systems and <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a>, dissipative systems are dynamical systems with a state <img class="tex" alt="x(t)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/d/5/fd5f9e2ee180a439aaec015692916a1c.png">, inputs <img class="tex" alt="u(t)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/0/d/a0d664fdd9965ace52f10dd8d03aea2d.png"> and outputs <img class="tex" alt="y(t)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/7/0/0707669836d19443cf6c5cc89ca963e6.png">, which satisfy the so-called "dissipation inequality".</p>
<p>Given a function <img class="tex" alt="w" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/2/f1290186a5d0b1ceab27f4e77c0c5d68.png"> on <img class="tex" alt="U \times Y" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/8/3/783f5fc04b73cc26d0e60cf59ee94957.png">, with finite integral of its modulus for any input function <img class="tex" alt="u" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/7/7b774effe4a349c6dd82ad4f4f21d34c.png"> and initial state <img class="tex" alt="x(0)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/6/3/0636e6ee3577c00d82887a3ac9b7d159.png"> over any finite time <img class="tex" alt="t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png">, called the "supply rate", a system is said to be dissipative if there exist a continuous nonnegative function <img class="tex" alt="V(x)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/d/a/7da3f9417e349bc472da3b128413cd70.png">, with <img class="tex" alt="x(0) = 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/d/a/8da03aa5f2ca4d36d812595c5f45c31c.png">, called the storage function, such that for any input <img class="tex" alt="u" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/b/7/7b774effe4a349c6dd82ad4f4f21d34c.png"> and initial state <img class="tex" alt="x(0)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/6/3/0636e6ee3577c00d82887a3ac9b7d159.png">, the following inequality, known as dissipation inequality, always holds:</p>
<dl><dd><img class="tex" alt="V(x(t)) - V(x(0)) \le \int_{0}^{t} u(\tau) \cdot y(\tau) d \tau" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/2/f/d2f965b40beafaa58e1dab84904fd8cf.png">,</dd>
</dl><p>Dissipative systems with supply rate</p>

</dl><p>where <img class="tex" alt=" \cdot " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/6/f/36f8ae4c86b69d52d037a6802d91cc4a.png"> denotes the scalar product,</p>
<p>Dissipative systems satisfy the inequality:</p>
<dl><dd><img class="tex" alt="\frac{dV(x(t))}{dt} \le u(t) \cdot y(t)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/f/7/af74403c955e7a5fc8697384240f6b86.png"></dd>
</dl><p>The physical interpretation is that <img class="tex" alt="V(x)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/d/a/7da3f9417e349bc472da3b128413cd70.png"> is the energy in the system, whereas <img class="tex" alt="u \cdot y" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/6/4/264c2fd1b8813c47483bc6a2c75010fa.png"> is the energy that is supplied to the system.</p>
<p>This notion has a strong connection with <a href="/wiki/Lyapunov_stability" title="Lyapunov stability">Lyapunov stability</a>, where the storage functions may play, under certain conditions of controllability and observability of the dynamical system, the role of Lyapunov functions.</p>
<p>Roughly speaking, dissipativity theory is useful for the design of feedback control laws for linear and nonlinear systems. Dissipative systems theory has been discussed by V.M. Popov, J.C. Willems, D.J. Hill and P. Moylan. In the case of linear invariant systems, this is known as positive real transfer functions, and a fundamental tool is the so-called <a href="/wiki/Kalman%E2%80%93Yakubovich%E2%80%93Popov_lemma" title="Kalman‚ÄìYakubovich‚ÄìPopov lemma">Kalman‚ÄìYakubovich‚ÄìPopov lemma</a> which relates the state space and the frequency domain properties of positive real systems. Dissipative systems are still an active field of research in systems and control, due to their important applications.</p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_dissipative_systems">Quantum dissipative systems</span><a href="#editor/3" data-section="3" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Quantum_dissipation" title="Quantum dissipation">Quantum dissipation</a></div>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, and any classical <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical system</a>, relies heavily on <a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics" title="Hamiltonian mechanics">Hamiltonian mechanics</a> for which <a href="/wiki/Time_reversibility" title="Time reversibility">time is reversible</a>, these approximations are not intrinsically able to describe dissipative systems. It has been proposed that in principle, one can couple weakly the system ‚Äì say, an oscillator ‚Äì to a bath, i.e., an assembly of many oscillators in thermal equilibrium with a broad band spectrum, and trace (average) over the bath. This yields a <a href="/wiki/Master_equation" title="Master equation">master equation</a> which is a special case of a more general setting called the <a href="/wiki/Lindblad_equation" title="Lindblad equation">Lindblad equation</a> that is the quantum equivalent of the classical <a href="/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(Hamiltonian)" title="Liouville's theorem (Hamiltonian)">Liouville equation</a>. The well known form of this equation and its quantum counterpart takes time as a reversible variable over which to integrate, but the very foundations of dissipative structures imposes an <a href="/wiki/H-theorem" title="H-theorem">irreversible</a> and constructive role for time.</p>
