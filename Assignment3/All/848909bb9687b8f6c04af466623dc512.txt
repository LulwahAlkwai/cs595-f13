			<p>Australia&rsquo;s top domain-names company, Melbourne IT, was thrust into the latest development of the Syrian civil war when pro-Damascus hackers, aided by people in India, penetrated its security systems and &shy;disrupted access to <i>The New York Times</i> website and Twitter.</p>
<p>As one analyst warned the ASX-listed company faced a &ldquo;global feeding frenzy&rdquo; over the security breach, &shy;Melbourne IT chief executive Theo Hnarakis denied it was responsible.</p>

<p>&ldquo;This is something that could &shy;happen to any company and any staff member,&rdquo; said Mr Hnarakis, who announced on Tuesday he would step down after 11&nbsp;years as CEO.</p>
<p>&ldquo;This is not a breakdown of security, this is not a weakness of our systems where someone&rsquo;s been able to hack in and grab customer information or credit card information.&rdquo;</p>
<p>In an attack The New York Times Co equated to &ldquo;breaking into Fort Knox&rdquo;, hackers changed the records of nine separate websites from about 5am AEST on Wednesday.</p>
<p>The records ensure visitors who type &ldquo;nyt.com&rdquo; end up at <i>The New York Times </i>website, and the hacks meant &shy;visitors were redirected to a different website, or were not able to reach those sites at all for several hours.</p>
<p>The incursion was traced to India-based perpetrators who fooled staff at a US-based reseller of Melbourne IT &shy;services into handing over personal details and, as a result, the login and passwords which allowed someone to access and change key details for the websites, Mr Hnarakis said.</p>
<p>The reseller is likely to be Corporation Services Company, which purchased Melbourne IT&rsquo;s digital marketing division and, along with it, contracts for <i>The New York Times</i>  and Twitter website records for $152.5&nbsp;million in March.<p><b>Dangerous for companies</b></p></p>
<p>Melbourne IT&nbsp;was made aware of the attacks at 6am AEST on Wednesday. &ldquo;This could happen to anyone, it just happens to be our turn,&rdquo; Mr &shy;Hnarakis said.</p>
<p>James Turner, an analyst at technology research firm IBRS, said being at the centre of a hacking scandal was dangerous for companies and he pointed to the liquidation of previously successful web-hosting company &shy;Distribute.IT after it was hit by a malicious hacker in 2011.</p>
<p>&ldquo;Melbourne IT is now in danger from a global feeding frenzy that could start with security experts coming out of the woodwork to decry them for not putting enough defences in place,&rdquo; he said. &ldquo;But I really think that misses the mark; they were attacked by malicious entity and we should focus on that.&rdquo;</p>
<p>The attack left visitors unable to visit <i>The New York Times </i>website for several hours. It also affected several country-specific Twitter websites and Twitter&rsquo;s online image-hosting service, Twimg.</p>
<p>Other websites targeted are believed to include the <i>Huffington Post</i>&rsquo;s UK site, which had additional security measures in place to prevent similar changes.</p>
<p>The affected email addresses and login details have since been blocked from accessing those records.</p>
<p>The Syrian Electronic Army, a collective of hackers which openly supports the Syrian government, claimed credit but did not make clear its motives. </p>
<p>The Syrian government is under threat of a military strike from the US and others after allegedly launching chemical weapons killing hundreds last week.</p>
<p>Any immediate damage from the hacking attack to Melbourne IT was not apparent on Wednesday, when its shares fell 1&cent; to $1.63. Stockbroking &shy;analysts contacted declined to comment publicly until more details about the gravity of the hacks became clear.<p><b>Second hacking event</b></p></p>
<p>One said any adverse investor reaction would be led by whether a significant outcry erupted and dealt a heavy blow to the company&rsquo;s reputation.</p>
<p>It is the second time in as many years the company has been implicated in a security failure. It was similarly at the centre of hacking attempts last year,  which saw the details of thousands of AAPT customers stolen.</p>
<p>Mr Turner, the IBRS analyst, said web companies such as Melbourne IT could be expected to have the highest levels of security in place, but there was no such thing as a 100&nbsp;per cent secure defence against attacks that were &shy;perpetrated by hackers tricking people.</p>
<p>&ldquo;We know that the Australian market has a high degree of intolerance about these things, but in many ways this isn&rsquo;t actually Melbourne IT&rsquo;s fault&nbsp;. . .&nbsp;they have been attacked by a group of hackers and they shouldn&rsquo;t be punished by retribution coming from the marketplace,&rdquo; he said.<span channel="!AFR">&ldquo;It appears these types of attacks will continue, so long as the &lsquo;supply chain&rsquo; security remains weak for these firms,&rdquo; he said.</span></p>
<p>&ldquo;There will be a lot of people out there saying they should have done more and that this is unforgivable, but this is also the reality of doing business now and you can&rsquo;t protect yourself from everything,&rdquo; he said.</p>
<p>Security experts warned that similar, future attacks were likely against mainstream news publications.</p>
<p>The New York Times Co&rsquo;s chief information officer, Marc Frons, told the newspaper that the outage was &ldquo;the result of a malicious external attack&rdquo; equivalent to &ldquo;breaking into Fort Knox&rdquo;.</p>
<p>&ldquo;A domain registrar should have extremely tight security because they are holding the security to hundreds if not thousands of websites,&rdquo; he said.<p><b>CEO had announced departure</b></p></p>
<p>It is the latest in a line of attacks for the pro-Syrian Government group. It has previously attacked the websites <i>Financial Times </i>and <i>The Washington Post</i>, causing outages or website redirections for some articles.</p>
<p>In April, <a target="_blank" href="http://www.afr.com/p/technology/watchdogs_issue_warning_on_twitter_ohxbUH1r9g2YzW2B8k78OI">it famously took control of the Twitter account of Associated Press</a>, falsely reporting a bomb explosion at the White House that wiped $US136.5 billion off US equity markets in three minutes.</p>
<p>The Syrian Electronic Army reported on Twitter shortly after the attacks that its own website was taken down by its website domain host due to a breach of agreement.</p>
<p>Mr Hnarakis, who <a target="_blank" href="http://www.afr.com/p/technology/melbourne_it_chief_theo_hnarakis_p9qVS8klWTM0zIMjQmMNLL">announced his departure from company a day before the attacks</a> after 11 years as CEO, said it was one of the most serious and high-profile attacks he had seen.</p>
<p>&ldquo;Obviously it&rsquo;s a concern and as these sites become more and more critical to organisations, the higher profile these attacks,&rdquo; he said. &ldquo;But this is not isolated.&rdquo;<div id="U619167306390JD" class="left_image" style="width:646px;"><img src="/r/2009-2014/AFR/2013/08/28/Photos/7d380f54-0fd9-11e3-ab1d-b9f686903bd2_29p03hack.png" width="646" height="425"></div></p>
<p>A former security reporter with <i>The Washington Post</i>, Brian Krebs, said that although the attack was simple, it was not common to gain access to the records of multiple websites at once.</p>
<p>&ldquo;It&rsquo;s the simplest way of defacing a site to which you cannot write,&rdquo; he said. &ldquo;If you can&rsquo;t arbitrarily post content on <i>The New York Times</i>&rsquo; website, that&rsquo;s not a big deal if you control their DNS records; you can simply redirect visitors to nytimes.com to a site that you control, and then you can serve them with whatever message or content you like.</p>
<p>&ldquo;It only takes one [website host] to have a security weakness for persistent attackers like the SEA to find a way in.&rdquo;</p>
<p><a target="_blank" href="http://mashable.com/2013/08/27/sea-hacks-twitter/">Security researcher HD Moore told technology blog Mashable</a> that other domains including those belonging to AOL, Yahoo!, Google and Microsoft were also hosted with Melbourne IT and could also be under threat if the attackers had discovered a weakness in the company&rsquo;s systems.</p>
<p>FireEye manager of threat intelligence Darien Kindlund said the group had specifically focused on attacking the weakest in a chain of suppliers behind mainstream news websites. </p>
<p>&ldquo;Rather than attacking a large firm directly, SEA is opting to identify weaker links between the firm and other partnering organisations that they use for business operations,&rdquo; he said. &ldquo;This is because the victim firm may not have as much control over the operational security employed by the partners, so the partners are an easier target to focus on.&rdquo;</p>
<p>He said blame likely laid with all three parties involved &ndash; Melbourne IT, the US reseller and the website owner &ndash; due to a failure of security at different levels.<p><b>READ NEXT:</b></p><ul class="story_bullets" ffxid="readnext"><li><b><a href="http://www.afr.com/p/technology/ny_times_says_outage_most_likely_jWiEHh2ApKLm5PtoEk33OK" title="www.afr.com">NY Times says outage &lsquo;most likely&rsquo; malicious</a></b></li><li><b><a href="http://www.afr.com/p/technology/new_york_times_and_other_news_sites_rc8FKl1kK5fxGLBeWzCLXP" title="www.afr.com">New York Times and other news sites attacked by hackers</a></b></li><li><b><a href="http://www.afr.com/p/technology/melbourne_it_chief_theo_hnarakis_p9qVS8klWTM0zIMjQmMNLL" title="www.afr.com">Melbourne IT chief Theo Hnarakis steps down, revenue drops</a></b></li><li><b><a href="http://www.afr.com/p/technology/cyber_experts_fear_escalation_of_rsOINb57tPtPqsn0CENGFO" title="www.afr.com">Cyber experts fear escalation of attacks</a></b></li></ul></p><p class="story-byline">
