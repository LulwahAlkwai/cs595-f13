<p>One possible contributing factor is that the median age of Italy is the highest in Europe (except for Monaco), about 3 years higher than the overall average.</p>

<p>&#8216;would mean Italy’s pace of technology adoption is only 70% as fast as the EU as a whole&#8217;</p>
<p>This is odd &#8211; a lot of older Italians refuse to use credit cards (at least according to several Italians I know, talking about family members), and it is not a sign that they are not aware of modern banking (which was essentially an Italian invention, after all). Instead, they have no desire to participate in a system that records all their transactions.</p>
<p>Instead, they seem fully aware just how credit cards provide information to people that those older Italians have no intention sharing information with.</p>
<p>And the strange thing? Snowden&#8217;s ongoing revelations of just how thoroughly the Internet is monitored means that the same sort of Italian utterly uninterested in the benefits of the modern credit card based economy were correct in their disdain in regards to the interconnected database financial system.</p>
<p>Some people, especially older ones with a quite different set of memories and experiences, prefer keeping their affairs private &#8211; Facebook, Gmail, etc are not a sign of technology adoption. They are a sign of just how effective automated privacy invasion has become.</p>

<p>I am just not following you with this policing/crime issue. Why would it be harder to police a group of smaller states (I never said dozens, as that would almost surely be too many)? We do most law enforcement locally and regionally in the US, with the FBI only occasionally getting involved, and that system most assuredly works better than if there were simply one national police force. And do you mean to suggest that Italy doesn&#8217;t have a problem with organized crime right now? I would think that a number of smaller states would be less prone to organized crime than a unified one, as they would have less of a captive audience of people and capital, and exit is easier (Tiebout and all that).</p>
<p class="reply"></p>

<p>I don&#8217;t think Italy or the US or anything will last till the end of time, but Italy, unlike say Yugoslavia or even Spain, doesn&#8217;t serm to be brimming with genuine seccessionist groups.  I haven&#8217;t noticed the the Northern League having ever had a paramilitary arm, honestly outside of the Veneto, I have never met an Italian seperatist having the enthusiasm of even a Texas or Alaskan seccessionist let alone the PQ.  The only Italians with that sort of National conciousness seem to be on Sicily and Sardinia, and they would have way too much to lose at the moment.  Spain has had how many civil wars in the last two centuries?  How many has Italy had?  And how many didn&#8217;t involve an occuppying foreign army?</p>

Even with Eurogeddon I just don&#8217;t see it happening.  This is just masturbatory talk.  One might as well talk about the recreation of the Republic of the Rio Grande.</p>
