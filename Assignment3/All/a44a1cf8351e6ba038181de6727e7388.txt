<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
There are protons in Europe, too
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-5874729187788666503' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on">
Speaking of <a href="http://runningahospital.blogspot.com/2013/09/still-nothing-from-cms-but-blue-shield.html">proton beams</a>, while they populate like rabbits in the US based on a financial model that includes overpayment for prostate irradiation, there are real debates going on in Europe about their efficacy.&nbsp; Here are excerpts from an article from the <a href="http://www.bmj.com/content/345/bmj.e4917?ijkey=uaGOc2cp3IPRzI3&amp;keytype=ref"><i>British Medical Journal</i></a>:<br />
<br />
<i>Critics say the NHS should not be spending so much money on a treatment 
that has not been subjected to randomised controlled trials and for 
which there is little evidence of long term efficacy or safety. In 
April, an article in the BMJ questioned whether the government&#8217;s &#163;250m investment was premature.
 &#8220;For most indications,&#8221; reported a review of the evidence in February, 
no firm conclusions could be drawn about the superiority of protons over
 photons and it was &#8220;sobering to observe that no phase III trials have 
been performed.&#8221; </i><br />
<br />
<div id="p-11">
<i>The debate has been clouded in the UK by a focus on the 
widespread use of proton therapy in America for the treatment of 
prostate cancer.
 This is a red herring, says Adrian Crellin, a consultant clinical 
oncologist at St James&#8217;s University Hospital, Leeds and the Department 
of Health&#8217;s national lead for proton beam therapy: &#8220;We have quite 
specifically excluded prostate cancer as a standard indication for 
treatment because there&#8217;s no evidence.&#8221;</i></div>
<div id="p-11">
<br /></div>
<div id="p-12">
<i>In fact, the 
application of proton therapy in the UK is limited to just 15 rare 
cancers&#8212; three adult and 12 paediatric&#8212;that have the clearest evidence, 
including base of skull chordomas and chondrosarcomas and primary 
paraspinal tumours. Referrals to overseas treatment centres, which will 
continue until the NHS units are up and running in 2017, are subject to 
approval by a national clinical panel that takes account of a range of 
other factors, including the timing of radiotherapy in relation to other
 treatment and the stage and pathology of the cancer.</i></div>
<div id="p-12">
<br /></div>
<div id="p-12">
Meanwhile, we see some caution <a href="http://medicalphysicsweb.org/cws/article/research/54462">in the Netherlands</a>:</div>
<div id="p-12">
<br /></div>
<div id="p-12">
<i>Roelofs explained that the Netherlands takes a somewhat cautious 
approach when it comes to introducing new treatment regimes, as 
evidenced by its health insurance board, which will only reimburse 
proven technologies. For many years, there was concern about the lack of
 evidence for proton therapy, a situation that led to the instigation of
 <a href="http://www.mistir.info/?q=ROCOCO">ROCOCO</a> - an ongoing international multicentric in silico trial comparing photons, protons and carbon ions.&nbsp;</i></div>
<div id="p-12">
<br /></div>
<i>The idea is that before a patient is referred for proton therapy,
 simulated plan comparisons must reveal a significant dosimetric benefit
 of protons. This advantageous dose distribution then needs to be 
translated into clinical benefits, such as reduction of side effects, 
using proven complication prediction models.  "This general in silico
 concept was picked up by the health insurance board to be enough 
evidence to reimburse," Roelofs said. The board has stated four 
model-based indications: head-and-neck, lung, prostate and breast 
cancer, where patients are eligible for reimbursement, as well as the 
standard indications: intraocular tumours, chordoma/chondrosarcoma and 
paediatric tumours.&nbsp;</i><br />
<br />
But they recognize that the jury is still out and so study is needed.<br />
<br />
<i>Another government requirement is that all patients treated with protons
 in the new centres are included in standardized clinical trials with 
uniform outcome measurement, requiring close collaboration between 
sites. "All four initiatives have been working on this proposal for 
years, so there's already good collaboration between them," Roelofs 
explained. "We're working together to set up one registry for protocols.
 When you have four centres treating in the same way, collecting the 
data in the same way, this will enable you to perform comparisons more 
easily. I think it will help create the evidence for proton therapy that
 is still needed."&nbsp;



</i><br />
<div id="p-12">
<br /></div>
<div id="p-12">
In the US, there is not a requirement that efficacy be demonstrated.&nbsp; Only safety.&nbsp; So the capital markets have leaped on the technology as a way to draw in the dollars.&nbsp; Hospitals sign the mortgage to create a competitive advantage.&nbsp; The medical arms race continues, enhanced by <a href="http://www.proton-therapy.org/">a national association</a>!</div>
</div>
