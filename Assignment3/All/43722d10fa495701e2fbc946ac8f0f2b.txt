<section class='article-content'>

<p>Looking in the mirror every day, it's unlikely that we notice our faces aging. It isn't until we look back at <a href="http://mashable.com/category/photography/">photos</a> that we see how much faces can change in just a few years.</p>
<div class="see-also"><p>See also: <a href="http://mashable.com/2012/02/19/travel-the-world-time-lapse/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Travel the World With These 10 Time Lapse Videos</a></p></div>
<p>Filmmaker <a href="http://anthonycerniello.com/#/page/work" target="_blank">Anthony Cerniello</a> explores this idea in his latest short film, <em>Danielle</em>. The simple clip focuses on a woman's face before a white backdrop. In under five minutes, the character ages decades, from a very young child to an older woman. </p>
<p><iframe src="//player.vimeo.com/video/74033442" width="500" height="281" frameborder="0" webkitallowfullscreen mozallowfullscreen allowfullscreen></iframe></p>
<p>Cerniello told <em>Mashable</em> that the video is based around a friend, Danielle, and photographs of her cousins and relatives. After scanning the individual photographs, he searched for similarities in bone structure among the women. </p>
<p>Cerniello created a slow morph of these still in Adobe After Effects, and hired animators to add in blinks, mouth and neck gestures and camera movements. After a nuke artist smoothed out discrepancies in the hair and eyes and color corrected between skin tones, Cerniello finished the piece with eerie abstract music, composed by a friend, Mark Reveley.</p>
<p>"I was thinking a lot about death, having panic attacks for a while and I wanted to explore that, I guess," says Cerniello. "It was my way of working through that time of my life."</p>
