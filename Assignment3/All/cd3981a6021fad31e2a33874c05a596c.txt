style="float:none;" alt="comment"></a></li></ul></div><p><img class="aligncenter" alt="" src="/img/photos/biz/2-year-track-ribbon.jpg" />In a <a href="http://chronicle.com/article/Who-Is-Driving-the-Online/140505/">recent column,</a> I asked the question, &#8220;Who is driving the online locomotive?&#8221;—in other words, who exactly is pushing the idea of offering more and more (not to mention bigger and bigger) online classes? Because that&#8217;s certainly where higher education seems to be headed.</p>
<p>I concluded that the people who hire college graduates are not among the culprits, citing a recent <em>Chronicle</em> survey in which prospective employers reported positive impressions of all types of higher-education institutions—except for online colleges. Of course, as I noted (and as several readers pointed out), there&#8217;s a big difference between getting an entire degree online and taking a few online courses en route to getting a degree. But employers&#8217; general lack of regard for computer college suggests that they find the online-learning environment a bit suspect.</p>
<p>Not long after that column was published, I had a conversation with a good friend of mine that reinforced my perception of employers&#8217; attitudes toward online classes.</p>
<p>My friend is a partner in a large, multinational professional-services firm. I wish I could say more, but even though he gave me permission to quote him in this post, he really doesn&#8217;t want to be identified. Suffice it to say that his firm is one of the best known in its industry, with a client list that includes many of the world&#8217;s most recognizable brands.</p>
<p>One of my friend&#8217;s duties, as a partner, is to serve as the primary recruiting and hiring manager for the regional office where he works. In that capacity he frequently visits college campuses, coordinates with human-resources folks at corporate headquarters, and interacts with his counterparts at other firms. So even though some might dismiss his thoughts as merely &#8220;one person&#8217;s opinion,&#8221; I&#8217;m convinced that this particular person knows what he&#8217;s talking about—that he speaks not just for himself, and not just for his firm, but for a large and lucrative profession.</p>
<p>What he said to me, after I told him about my column and some of the responses to it, was, &#8220;Oh, we would never hire somebody who took online classes.&#8221;</p>
<p>Never? Really?</p>
<p>&#8220;Well,&#8221; he admitted grudgingly, &#8220;I suppose a few online classes wouldn&#8217;t necessarily be a deal-breaker. But if someone has a degree from an online college, or we can tell from their transcript that they took a lot of their classes online, we wouldn&#8217;t consider them. I think that&#8217;s pretty much true for any of the big firms in our industry.&#8221;</p>
<p>Think about that statement, just for a moment, in light of Jeb Bush&#8217;s recent prediction that, by 2018, more than 80 percent of professional degrees will be earned online.</p>
<p>When I asked my friend why companies like his aren&#8217;t interested in people who took &#8220;a lot&#8221; of classes online, he explained, &#8220;The biggest problem with most of our new hires is that they already think everything can be done electronically. They&#8217;re always on their computers and handheld devices, e-mailing or texting clients and colleagues. As their supervisor, I&#8217;m constantly having to remind them that our business is based on personal relationships. I literally have to tell them to get out from behind their computers and go talk to people. That concept is largely foreign to them.&#8221;</p>
<p>He concluded, &#8220;I&#8217;m sure online classes are fine for teaching certain specific concepts. But that&#8217;s not really what we&#8217;re interested in. We can teach those concepts ourselves. What we really value in a college education is the interpersonal skills that students develop while they&#8217;re in school. Online classes just don&#8217;t teach those.&#8221;</p>

function openAccountWorkflow(href, mTitle, options, show_close) {
    /* merge defaults and options, without modifying defaults */
    var settings = $.extend({}, acmgt_dialog_options, options, {title: mTitle});    
    var ua = $.browser;
    $("#account-workflow").dialog(settings);        
    if ( ua.mozilla && (ua.version.slice(0,3) <= "1.9") && (document.readyState != "complete") ) {
        // Firefox 3.6 and under has a bug setting src of iframe so wait till page is done loading to set
        $(window).load(function() {
            $('#modal-iframe').attr('src', href);
        });
    } else {
        // all other browsers set right away.
        $('#modal-iframe').attr('src', href);
    }
    $("#account-workflow").dialog("open");
    if ( !show_close ) {
        $('a.ui-dialog-titlebar-close').hide();
    }
}
