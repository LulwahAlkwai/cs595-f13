</div> </div> <div class="post"> <!--[if lt IE 9]> <noscript><div class="header" section="title"></noscript> <![endif]--> <header section="title"> <h1>Apple's 64-bit A7 chip (FAQ)</h1> <p id="introP">The company's move to a 64-bit chip is necessary. And it's meaningful that Apple got there first.</p> <div class="postByline"> <a rel="author" href="http://www.cnet.com/profile/mbrookec/"><figure><img class="mugshot" alt="Brooke Crothers" height="43" width="60" src="http://asset3.cbsistatic.com/cnwk.1d/i/tim/2011/10/12/headshots_Brooke_Crothers_140x100_60x43.jpg" /></figure></a> <div id="nameAndTime"> <span class="author"> <div class="singleAuthor"> by <a rel="author" href="http://www.cnet.com/profile/mbrookec/">Brooke Crothers</a> </div> </span> <!--[if lt IE 9]> <noscript><div class="datestamp"></noscript> <![endif]--> <time class="datestamp"> September 14, 2013 4:53 PM PDT </time> <!--[if lt IE 9]> <noscript></div></noscript> <![endif]--> <menu class="inline-view fullwidth noMarginTop"> <ul> <li> <a href="http://www.twitter.com/mbrookec" class="twitter-follow-button" data-show-screen-name="false" data-show-count="false" data-width="70px">Follow @mbrookec</a> </li> </ul> </menu> <!--Bug 616807, this is to test if the Author is Chris Matyszczyk --> </div> </div> </header> <!--[if lt IE 9]> <noscript></div></noscript> <![endif]--> <div class="postBody txtWrap" section="txt" >
<div class="cnet-image-div image-REGULAR float-none" style="width: 600px">
<img class="cnet-image" src="http://asset2.cbsistatic.com/cnwk.1d/i/tim2/2013/09/14/a7-cnet.jpg" alt="Apple introduces the iPhone 5S and its A7 processor."
width="600" height="399" />
<p class="image-caption">Apple introduces the iPhone 5S and its A7 processor.</p>
<span class="image-credit">

CNET)
</span>
</div>
<p>Apple is the first to get a 64-bit processor into a smartphone. So, what's all this 64-bit business anyway? </p>
<p><b>Is this the first 64-bit ARM chip for smartphones?</b>
</p>
<p></p><blockquote>The Apple A7 is the first processor from any company to implement the
new 64-bit <a href="http://www.arm.com/products/processors/armv8-architecture.php">ARMv8</a> instruction set. </blockquote>
</p>
<p>Linley Gwennap, principal analyst at the <a href="http://www.linleygroup.com/analyst_detail.php?Linley-Gwennap-1">Linley Group</a>, a chip technology consultancy, supplied CNET with the quote above, answering that question. </p>
<p>The "ARM" in ARMv8 refers to the dominant chip technology used in smartphones and
<a href="http://reviews.cnet.com/tablets/" section="luke_topic" > tablets</a> worldwide. Apple, Qualcomm, Samsung, Nvidia, MediaTek, and others all license technology from ARM. That's how
<a href="http://www.cnet.com/android-atlas/" section="luke_topic" > Android</a> can run on a variety of smartphones with a variety of ARM chips. </p>
<p>The 'v8" part refers to ARM's 64-bit chip architecture. Current ARM chips from all of the above chip suppliers are 32-bit. </p>
<p>Apple is ahead of the pack. "To achieve this feat, Apple must have designed its own custom CPU," Gwenapp said, referring to the 64-bitness in the CPU or central processing unit. ARM's own 64-bit designs are not scheduled for commercial production until 2014 -- and that's probably later in 2014, not early in the year. </p>
<p><b>What does 64-bit get me?</b><br /> Ah, there's the rub. <a href="https://discussions.apple.com/thread/2248103?start=0&tstart=0">This same question was asked</a> when Apple <a href="http://www.apple.com/pr/library/2003/06/23Apple-Unleashes-the-Worlds-Fastest-Personal-Computer-the-Power-Mac-G5.html">introduced the 64-bit PowerPC G5</a> from IBM. </p>
<p>The short answer is a future
<a href="http://reviews.cnet.com/ipad/" section="luke_topic" > iPad</a> -- or whatever future newfangled iOS device Apple dreams up -- can address more memory. Let's just say more than the 4GB limitation in many cases for 32-bit processors. </p> <p>The longer answer is that 64-bit allows data-intensive applications to handle large chunks of data more efficiently than 32-bit. Right now, ARM is aiming 64-bit chips at servers, not smartphones. Why? Because servers can benefit immediately in a big way -- for the reasons stated above. </p> <div class="floatList" section="related">
<h3>Related stories</h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/8301-1035_3-57605807-94/qualcomm-gambit-apple-64-bit-a7-is-a-gimmick/">Qualcomm gambit: Apple 64-bit A7 is a 'gimmick' </a></li>
<li><a href="/8301-13579_3-57605093-37/iphone-5s-gets-quad-core-graphics-analysis-shows/">iPhone 5S gets quad-core graphics, analysis shows</a></li>
</ul>
</div> <p>In fact, the only other ARMv8 chip that has even reached the sample production stage is <a href="http://www.apm.com/products/x-gene">AppliedMicro's X-Gene</a>, a server processor expected to reach production late this year, according to Gwennap. </p> <p>So, Apple will have to convince its own developers that it makes sense to develop 64-bit apps for consumers, like the Epic Games Infinity Blade 3 demoed at the Apple iPhone event on Tuesday. </p> <p>Apple's Phil Schiller, speaking at the event, said Apple's iOS 7 has been "completely re-engineered for 64-bit...64-bit kernels, libraries, and drivers. And the apps that come with your iPhone 5S, they've been re-engineered to 64 bit as well." </p> <p>"This will be an easy transition for developers, we've updated our tools...to support 64-bit," he added. </p> <p>If you listen to Epic Games <a href="http://www.apple.com/apple-events/september-2013/">executives talk about the improvements that the 64-bit A7 made possible</a> (the 38:40 mark) for their game, it's impressive. But at least some of that performance boost is coming from the <a title="The real reasons Apple's 64-bit A7 chip makes sense -- Wednesday, Sep 11, 2013" href="/8301-1035_3-57602372-94/the-real-reasons-apples-64-bit-a7-chip-makes-sense/">richer OpenGL ES 3.0 graphics-acceleration interface</a> and an improved graphics chip, not from the 64-bit design. </p> <p>So, what does 64-bit get you? The upshot is the potential for faster apps that can juggle large amounts of data more efficiently. </p> <p><b>When will we see more 64-bit apps?</b><br /> Don't hold your breath. It's a wait-and-see scenario to see which apps jump to 64-bit quickly because of the obvious benefits -- and which don't. </p> <p><b>Do all apps benefit from a 64-bit processor?</b><br /> No. Many, if not most, apps won't see any meaningful benefit. </p> <p><b>Will the iPad go 64-bit A7 too?</b><br /> You bet. Possibly with a faster version of the A7 for the iPad. And there's probably equal or more potential for future iOS devices that Apple is undoubtedly planning. </p> </div><!-- /div section --> <footer> <!--[if lt IE 9]> <noscript><div></noscript> <![endif]--> <aside> </aside> <!--[if lt IE 9]> <noscript></div></noscript> <![endif]--> <div section="tags" class="tagsWrap"> <div class="postLinks"> <dl> <dt class="pipe"><b>Tags:</b></dt> <dd> <a href="/8300-5_3-0.html?keyword=64-bit">64-bit</a>, </dd> <dd> <a href="/8300-5_3-0.html?keyword=iphone+5s">iPhone 5S</a>, </dd> <dd> <a href="/8300-5_3-0.html?keyword=a7">A7</a>, </dd>  <dd> <a href="/8300-5_3-0.html?keyword=apple">Apple</a> </dd> <!-- Added DVAR tag per bug 422649. liaHeader value=64-bit;iPhone 5S;A7;Apple --> </dl> </div> </div> <div class="editorBio"> <a rel="author" href="http://www.cnet.com/profile/mbrookec/"><figure><img class="mugshot" src="http://asset3.cbsistatic.com/cnwk.1d/html/news/clear.gif" height="100" width="140" lazySrc="http://i.i.cbsi.com/cnwk.1d/i/tim/2011/10/12/headshots_Brooke_Crothers_140x100_140x100.jpg" /></figure></a> <div id="bioLinks"> <a rel="author" href="http://www.cnet.com/profile/mbrookec/">Brooke Crothers</a> <p class="shortBio">Brooke Crothers writes about small devices and the hardware inside.
