<section class='article-content'>

<div class="shift-to-hero"><p><iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="360" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/2rjbtsX7twc" width="640"></iframe></p></div>
<p>Timothy Blais decided that completing his master's thesis — titled "<a href="https://docs.google.com/a/mashable.com/file/d/0B5MlzMZS6K4fbU1vYTlPUWNFVTA/view?sle=true" target="_blank">A New Quantization Condition for Parity-Violating Three-Dimensional Gravity</a>" — wasn't enough of a challenge. So the physics whiz stepped it up with a musical version set to Queen's classic anthem "Bohemian Rhapsody." </p>
<div class="see-also"><p>See also: <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/07/25/incredible-lego-creations/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">27 Brickin' Incredible Lego Creations</a></p></div>
<p>The cleverly titled "Bohemian Gravity" features brain-flexing science vocabulary set over a background of smooth harmonies. This isn't Blais' first scientific tune either — last year, he sang about the <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VtItBX1l1VY" target="_blank">Higgs particle</a> to the tune of Adele's "Rolling in the Deep."</p>
<p>"Bohemian Gravity" delves deep into complicated physics references, but even if you don't understand the science, you can still enjoy the Albert Einstein sock puppet. </p>

<p>Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman gave a thorough interview about his life and his science that appeared on TV in 1981 with the title "The Pleasure of Finding Things Out."
