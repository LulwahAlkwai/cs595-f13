						<h3>Women who don&#8217;t self-promote are letting us down.</h3>
						<p><small>Maggie Fox, June 26 2013</small></p>
	
							
				
						<p>I don&#8217;t care how distasteful you find it. Or, perhaps it&#8217;s not even modesty, but rather a feeling that giving that interview or speaking at that conference is not a good use of your time. You&#8217;re too busy. Whatever the reason, I&#8217;ll make this very clear: women who want to <a href="http://www.blogher.com/rachel-sklar-changing-ratio" target="_blank">&#8220;change the ratio&#8221;</a> but don&#8217;t self promote are letting all of us down.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m publishing this post (in which no names shall be named) in response to my recent experience on a writing project. I&#8217;m interviewing amazing founders and CEOs, talking to them about their businesses and how they got there. I am committed to ensuring that the voices that make up this story are diverse &#8211; I&#8217;d like to have a decent ratio of women to men (50/50 is probably ambitious, but I&#8217;ll try). Thanks to introductions from well-connected and helpful friends, I&#8217;ve interviewed some of the best-known names in Silicon Valley and elsewhere, and their stories have been amazing, compelling, and strikingly humble.</p>
<p>The problem? Most of them are men. Why? Because less than a third of the women I&#8217;ve approached have responded or agreed to be interviewed. <strong>All of the men have.</strong></p>
<p>At this rate, I&#8217;ll have to approach three female tech CEOs for every single interview I&#8217;m able to book. I invite you to pity me in my attempts to &#8220;change the ratio&#8221; &#8211; something that now appears to be a mathematical impossibility.</p>
<p>So I&#8217;ll just say it: women who don&#8217;t self promote are letting us down. This isn&#8217;t going to happen by magic &#8211; this is your <strong>responsibility.</strong></p>
