					<h1><a href="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/2013/09/11/therefore-his-shipmates-called-him-mad-the-science-of-moby-dick/">“Therefore His Shipmates Called Him Mad”: The Science of Moby Dick</a></h1>
					<div class="post-author"><span class="by">by</span> Carl Zimmer </div>
				</header>
				<div class="article-text">
					<p>One of the best things about a blog is that it can function as a public sketch pad, where I can try out ideas that aren&#8217;t quite right for a full-blown magazine feature, book, or newspaper article. Sometimes those verbal sketches can mature into something more.</p>
<p>In November, for example, I was inspired by an online reading of <em>Moby Dick</em> <a href="http://phenomena.nationalgeographic.com/2012/11/11/herman-melville-science-writer/">to praise Melville as a science writer</a>. Soon afterwards, I was contacted by the Los Angeles Public Library, which was planning <a href="http://lfla.org/mobydick/?page_id=695">a month-long celebration of the book</a> that has just kicked off. They asked if I would write an essay about the science behind the novel, which they could include in the program for <a href="http://thebroadstage.com/show_info.php?show_id=241">the final event</a> on October 5.</p>
<p>It was a great pleasure to dig deeper into Melville&#8217;s life and times, and reflect on how his scatter-shot education in pre-Darwinian biology shaped his book.</p>
<p>Here&#8217;s how the piece starts:</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>“To have one’s hands among the unspeakable foundations, ribs, and very pelvis of the world; this is a fearful thing. What am I that I should essay to hook the nose of this leviathan!”</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Ishmael asks himself this question at the beginning of “Cetology,” the thirty-second chapter of <em>Moby Dick</em>. Up till this point, the narrative of <em>Moby Dick</em>, as Ishmael recounts his experiences joining the crew of the Pequod, feels fairly straightforward. Readers who bought the novel when it first came out in 1851 probably found it similar to Melville’s previous novels of the sea, like <em>Mardi</em> and <em>White-Jacket</em>. But then Ishmael abruptly turns into a peculiar sort a naturalist. He dedicates an entire chapter to whale taxonomy in absurdly exhaustive detail. Later in the novel, he writes chapters dedicated to the anatomy of whales, their fossils, and their ecology.</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Those chapters put off many readers and critics in Melville’s day. All that science felt like a massive distraction from the central story of Ahab’s mad pursuit of the White Whale. And even today, I’d wager that a lot of readers page quickly through the long passages about whale flukes and whale brains. But the science of <em>Moby Dick</em> is as superfluous to the novel as lungs are to the body. Melville used science to elevate the hunt for a single sperm whale into a metaphysical tragicomedy.</strong></p>
<p><strong></strong>The entire essay is available at the event&#8217;s web site. <a href="http://lfla.org/mobydick/?page_id=920">Check it out.</a></p>
