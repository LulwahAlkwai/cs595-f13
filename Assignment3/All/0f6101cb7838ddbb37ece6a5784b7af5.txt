									<h1 class="entry-title">How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%</h1>
										<div class="comments-link">
					<a href="http://blog.play-i.com/carnegie-mellon-study-on-gender-and-computer-science/#comments" title="Comment on How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%">25 Replies</a>				</div><!-- .comments-link -->
					</header><!-- .entry-header -->

				<div class="entry-content">
			<p>In 1995 Allan Fisher, the Associate Dean of Computer Science at Carnegie Mellon University, realized there was a gender ratio problem in the department. <strong>Only 7%</strong> of freshman computer science majors were women. So he and Jane Margolis, a social scientist, tried to figure out what they could do to change the ratio. By 2000, <strong>42% of the freshman class was made up of women</strong>.</p>
<p>These changes not only attracted more women to computer science but also made the culture of the department more inclusive for everyone. In 2004, 30% of undergraduate computer science degrees awarded at Carnegie Mellon were to women.</p>
<div id="attachment_1348" class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 590px"><a href="http://www.hackingplay.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/123.png"><img class="lazy lazy-hidden size-full wp-image-1348" alt="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" src="http://blog.play-i.com/wp-content/plugins/bj-lazy-load/img/placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-lazy-src="http://www.hackingplay.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/123.png" width="580" height="325" title="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" /><noscript><img class="size-full wp-image-1348" alt="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" src="http://www.hackingplay.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/123.png" width="580" height="325" title="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" /></noscript></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Image courtesy of Tech President</p></div>
<p>Here are 4 things they implemented to recruit and retain more women in the field:</p>
<h2>1. Outreach to high schools</h2>
<p>During 1996 through 1998, CMU set up summer institutes for computer science high school teachers where 25% of the curriculum focused on how to provide gender equity instruction. 16% of all high school AP CS teachers in the nation participated in this program. They also directly reached out to high school girls who did well in math and science through activities like <a href="http://women.cs.cmu.edu/technights/">Technights</a>.</p>
<h2>2. A more inclusive admissions process</h2>
<p>In the past, admissions gave preference to people with lots of previous programming experience. The new criteria focused on <em>potential</em> by targeting students who showed science and math aptitude as well as non-academic strengths like leadership. The department made it explicit that no prior experience was required.</p>
<h2>3. Broaden the scope of early coursework</h2>
<p>Early in the curriculum freshmen learned about various areas of research, which introduced them to the broad potential impact of the field. New classes focused on how computer science could impact society. &#8220;Technology Consulting in the Community,&#8221; for example, provided opportunities for students to help non-profits solve real-world technical problems.</p>
<h2>4. Programs to change peer culture</h2>
<p><a href="http://women.cs.cmu.edu/" target="_blank">Women @ SCS</a> was started to provide support and mentorship for women in computer science. A Big Sister / Little Sister program paired upper-class and graduate students with freshmen. The organization also hosted panels and events that addressed women&#8217;s issues.</p>
<p><a href="http://women.cs.cmu.edu/"><img class="lazy lazy-hidden" alt="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" src="http://blog.play-i.com/wp-content/plugins/bj-lazy-load/img/placeholder.gif" data-lazy-type="image" data-lazy-src="http://www.hackingplay.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/Screen-Shot-2013-08-29-at-11.42.36-AM.png" width="531" height="129" title="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" /><noscript><img alt="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" src="http://www.hackingplay.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/Screen-Shot-2013-08-29-at-11.42.36-AM.png" width="531" height="129" title="How Carnegie Mellon Increased the Proportion of Women in Computer Science From 7% to 42%" /></noscript></a></p>
<blockquote><p>&#8220;The goal should not be to fit women into computer science but rather to change computer science.&#8221; &#8211; Jane Margolis and Allan Fisher</p></blockquote>
<p>This year 35% of the freshman class is women. For more details on the Carnegie Mellon study on gender and computer science, check out Margolis&#8217; paper <a href="http://www.cs.cmu.edu/afs/cs/project/gendergap/www/papers/sigcse97/sigcse97.html" target="_blank">here</a> as well as more recent studies <a href="http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~cfrieze/" target="_blank">here</a>.<a href="http://www.cs.cmu.edu/afs/cs/project/gendergap/www/papers/sigcse97/sigcse97.html" target="_blank"><br />
</a></p>
<p>We would love to hear from you. What do you think the implications of these changes are?</p>

			<section class="comment-content comment">
				<p>Don&#8217;t get me wrong &#8212; I think CMU is doing a lot of really good things.</p>
<p>But the fact is that after the initial boost back in 2000, CMU has lost a lot of ground (42% freshmen in 2000, 35% freshmen today).  Those freshmen also necessarily don&#8217;t turn into majors &#8212; this recent (February 2013) article:</p>
<p>    <a href="http://news.cs.cmu.edu/article.php?a=3570" rel="nofollow">http://news.cs.cmu.edu/article.php?a=3570</a></p>
<p>says,  &#8220;Women currently comprise 26 percent of undergraduate student majors in CMU&#8217;s School of Computer Science, while national graduation rates for women in CS are below 12 percent, according to last year&#8217;s Computer Research Association Taulbee Survey.&#8221;  (I won&#8217;t comment on the misleading nature of comparing percentage of majors to national percentages of graduates&#8230;)</p>
<p>So first of all, those 35% of freshmen majors are going to turn into only 26% of majors overall (which, of course, means that significantly fewer than 26% of <i>upperclassmen</i> are computing majors).</p>
<p>Second of all, there is an even larger disparity between freshmen major rates and graduation rates, at least if you look at the 2000-to-2004 comparison.  42% of freshmen in 2000 were women; only 30% of graduates in 2004 were women.  That means that the male retention rate is 1.7 times that of women.   And depending on what the overall retention (graduation) rate is, that means women were dropping <i>out</i> of the major at a rate anywhere from 1.5 times as often as the men (if the overall graduation rate was 50%) up to <i>14 times</i> as often as the men (if the overall graduation rate was 80%).</p>
<p>If you extend this trend, and assume that the male retention rate continues to be 1.7 times that of women, for a 35% freshman class, the projected graduating class would be down to 25% women.</p>
<p>Like I said, don&#8217;t get me wrong; CMU is obviously doing some really good stuff.  That&#8217;s twice the national average, and if we could get numbers like that at UMBC, I&#8217;d be elated.  But I&#8217;d be equally distressed to see that huge retention disparity for women, and at the significant overall downward trend within the program.  I don&#8217;t think I&#8217;d be posting about the great success of the program based on something that was accomplished 10 years ago but that has eroded significantly.</p>
<p>(June also says in one of her comments:  &#8220;Remember that 2000 was the peak of the tech bubble, so there was a lot more interest in general in the field.&#8221;  Actually, I think this isn&#8217;t true &#8212; while enrollments dropped after 2000, by now they are back up nationally to where they were in the late 90s, if not higher. )</p>
