			<h1 id="section_0">Earthquake light</h1><ul id="page-actions" class="hlist"><li id="ca-edit"></li><li id="ca-upload"></li><li id="ca-talk"><a href="/wiki/Talk:Earthquake_light" title="Discussion about the content page [t]" accesskey="t">Talk</a></li><li id="ca-watch" class="watch-this-article"><a href="https://en.m.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:UserLogin&amp;returnto=Earthquake+light" title="Add this page to your watchlist [w]" accesskey="w"></a></li></ul>			<div id="content" class="content">
				<div><p>An <b>earthquake light</b> is an unusual <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminous</a> aerial <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> that reportedly appears in the sky at or near areas of <a href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic" class="mw-redirect">tectonic</a> stress, <a href="/wiki/Seismic" title="Seismic" class="mw-redirect">seismic</a> activity, or <a href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic" class="mw-redirect">volcanic</a> eruptions. Once commonly challenged, it was not until photographs were taken during the Matsushiro <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_swarm" title="Earthquake swarm">earthquake swarm</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nagano" title="Nagano" class="mw-redirect">Nagano</a>, Japan (which occurred from 1965 through 1967) that the seismology community acknowledged their occurrence.</p>

</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><a href="#editor/1" data-section="1" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>The lights are reported to appear while an earthquake is occurring, although there are reports of lights before or after earthquakes, such as reports concerning the 1975 <a href="/wiki/Kalapana,_Hawaii" title="Kalapana, Hawaii">Kalapana</a> earthquake.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> They are reported to have shapes similar to those of the <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroras</a>, with a white to bluish hue, but occasionally they have been reported having a wider color spectrum. The luminosity is reported to be visible for several seconds, but has also been reported to last for tens of minutes. Accounts of viewable distance from the epicenter varies: in the 1930 Idu earthquake, lights were reported up to 70 miles (110 km) from the epicenter.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Earthquake lights were reportedly spotted in <a href="/wiki/Tianshui" title="Tianshui">Tianshui, Gansu</a>, approximately 400 kilometres (250 mi) north-northeast of the earthquake's epicenter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/2007_Peru_earthquake" title="2007 Peru earthquake">2007 Peru earthquake</a> lights were seen in the skies above the sea and filmed by many people. The phenomenon was also observed and caught on film during the <a href="/wiki/2009_L%27Aquila_earthquake" title="2009 L'Aquila earthquake">2009 L'Aquila</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/2010_Chile_earthquake" title="2010 Chile earthquake">2010 Chile earthquakes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Video footage has also recorded this happening during the 9 April 2011 eruption of <a href="/wiki/Sakura_Island" title="Sakura Island" class="mw-redirect">Sakurajima Volcano, Japan</a>. The phenomenon was also reported around the Amuri Earthquake in New Zealand, that occurred 1 September 1888. The lights were visible in the morning of 1 September in Reefton, and again on 8 September.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</div><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><a href="#editor/2" data-section="2" class="edit-page">Edit</a></h2><div>
<p>Earthquake lights are caused by an unknown mechanism. There are numerous theories as to how and why they occur.</p>
<p>One explanation involves intense electric fields created <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectrically</a> by tectonic movements of rocks containing <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Another possible explanation is local disruption of the Earth's magnetic field and/or <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> in the region of tectonic stress, resulting in the observed glow effects either from ionospheric radiative recombination at lower altitudes and greater atmospheric pressure or as <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurora</a>. However, the effect is clearly not pronounced or notably observed at all earthquake events and is yet to be directly experimentally verified.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
