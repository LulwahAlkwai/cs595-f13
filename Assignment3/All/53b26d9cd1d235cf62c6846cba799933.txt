<img alt='' src='http://1.gravatar.com/avatar/11de74e59314d314a2133a54bb062e93?s=32&amp;d=http%3A%2F%2F1.gravatar.com%2Favatar%2Fad516503a11cd5ca435acc9bb6523536%3Fs%3D32&amp;r=G' class='avatar avatar-32 photo' height='32' width='32' /><div class="post-info"><span class="date published time" title="2013-08-15T14:52:37+00:00">August 15, 2013</span>  by <span class="author vcard"><span class="fn"><a href="http://archivesocial.com/blog/author/robin-withall/" title="Robin Withall" rel="author">Robin Withall</a></span></span> <span class="post-comments"><a href="http://archivesocial.com/blog/government-social-media-policy-part-2/#respond"><span class="dsq-postid" rel="2738 http://archivesocial.com/blog/?p=2738">Leave a Comment</span></a></span> </div><div class="entry-content"><a id="dd_start"></a><div id="attachment_2754" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 310px"><a href="http://archivesocial.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/4847679257_9c502307eb.jpg" ><img class="size-full wp-image-2754 " alt="government social media policy" src="http://archivesocial.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/4847679257_9c502307eb.jpg" width="300" height="250" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text"><span style="font-size: xx-small;">flickr. ivanpw</span></p></div>
<p>In addition to the <a href="http://archivesocial.com/blog/government-social-media-policy-part-1/" >external government social media policy</a> that we highlighted earlier this week, it is equally important for public sector organizations to establish an internal employee-use policy for social media.</p>
<p dir="ltr">The policy should include 3 elements:</p>
<ol>
<li>Guidelines for personal versus professional use,</li>
<li>A mechanism for an agency to approve and register social media sites, and</li>
<li>The ability for oversite and enforcement.</li>
</ol>
<h2 dir="ltr">Personal vs. Professional Use</h2>
<p dir="ltr">Any employee-use policy must differentiate between personal and professional social media usage, and explain how the rules apply to each.</p>
<p>GSA&#8217;s <a href="http://www.gsa.gov/portal/content/250045" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.gsa.gov']);">Social Media Navigator</a> does a great job of outlining what &#8220;official use&#8221; means:</p>
<blockquote><p>When you join Facebook, Twitter and other social sites, you do so with different responsibilities. When representing the agency, you&#8217;re speaking in an &#8220;official&#8221; voice and have been authorized to speak for the agency. If you have your own Facebook and Twitter sites, you&#8217;re speaking in a &#8220;personal&#8221; voice&#8221; with your own views and opinions.</p>
<p>The difference between official use and personal use of social media is this:</p>
<p>Personal use: You can do anything that&#8217;s not illegal.</p>
<p>Official Use: You can do what you&#8217;re authorized to do.&#8221;</p></blockquote>
<h2>Approval and Registration of Social Media Sites</h2>
<p>A fundamental component of the employee-use policy is the section describing approval and registration of social media sites.</p>
<p>Remember: this does not mean that a public affairs office should approve every post. But the person in charge of social media should approve the actual social media profile. In other words, an agency&#8217;s facebook page should be approved, but not every single post written by the agency.</p>
<p>Why would an agency need to do this, you ask? It&#8217;s actually a way for public affairs to accomplish several goals at once: to ensure brand consistency, to provide training, and to ensure that each social networking profile serves a distinct and meaningful purpose.</p>
<p dir="ltr">But wait! There&#8217;s a bonus. The approval process provides a natural entry point for populating and maintaining a <a href="http://www.usa.gov/Contact/verify-social-media.shtml" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.usa.gov']);" rel="nofollow">registry</a> of the organization’s social media profiles.</p>
<h2>Oversight and Enforcement</h2>
<p>Finally, the employee-use policy should establish rules regarding oversight and enforcement. A social media profile created for professional purposes ultimately belongs to the organization, and not the individual employee.</p>
<p>Responsible parties in the organization, ranging from public affairs to legal, must have the right to monitor and enforce actions on a profile when necessary.</p>
