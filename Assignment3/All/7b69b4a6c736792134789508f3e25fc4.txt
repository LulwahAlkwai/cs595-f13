<figure><img alt="Summize" src="http://rack.3.mshcdn.com/media/ZgkyMDEzLzA5LzE3L2YzL3N1bW1pemUuMzNmM2MuanBnCnAJdGh1bWIJODUweDU5MD4KZQlqcGc/a84f505d/2d3/summize.jpg"></figure><div class="meta">
<h2 class="title">1. Summize</h2>
<div class="caption">

<p><a href="http://mashable.com/category/twitter/">Twitter</a>'s looming <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/09/12/twitter-ipo-analysis/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">IPO</a> brings up a lot of questions, the biggest of which being what going public will mean for the company's future. </p>
<p>As any good history teacher will tell you, the best way to understand the future is to consider the past. An acquisition, whether it be for patents or talent or anything between, can speak volumes about a company's future plans.</p>
<div class="see-also"><p>See also: <a href="http://mashable.com/2012/07/27/smart-twitter-accounts/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">25 Twitter Accounts That Will Make You Smarter</a></p></div>
<p>Twitter has made a whopping 31 acquisitions since its inception in 2006, and it's easy to notice trends in the types of acquisitions the microblogging site has made. Namely, there is a focus on mobile and advertising, with a few notable acquisitions in other types of social media, such as <a href="http://mashable.com/category/vine/">Vine</a> and We Are Hunted, which went on to become <a href="http://mashable.com/category/twitter-music/">Twitter Music</a>. </p>
<p>What part of Twitter's acquisition history stands out to you? What direction do you think the company is moving toward? Let us know in the comments section.</p>
