		<h2 class="title"><a href="http://reason.com/blog/2013/09/16/time-magazines-americas-weak-and-wafflin"><em>Time</em> Magazine's "America's Weak and Waffling" Cover Not Featured in America</a><span class="editor" pid="212490"></span></h2>
		<p class="byline"><a href="http://reason.com/people/zenon-evans/all" rel="author">Zenon Evans</a><span class="pipe">|</span><time datetime="2013-09-16T19:59:00+00:00">Sep. 16, 2013 3:59 pm</time></p>
	</header>
							
		    

	

<div class="p402_premium">
<div class="entry postcontent">
					<p><em>Time</em> magazine featured a critique of the United States'
foreign policy and an image of Russian President Vladimir Putin
<a href="http://content.time.com/time/magazine/0,9263,7601130916,00.html">on
the cove</a>r of its September 16<sup>th</sup> edition. American
readers did not see the critique, which ran throughout the rest of
the world. The U.S. edition focused instead on college
football.</p>
<p><span class="addcaption pic" style="width:480px"><img alt="Time" height="240" src="http://cloudfront-media.reason.com/mc/zevans@reason.com/time-magazine-putin.jpg?h=240&amp;w=480" title="" width="480" /><span class="caption">Credit: Screencap, Time.com</span></span></p>
<p>The international edition, which is divided into three regions
(Europe, the Middle East, and Africa; Asia; and the South Pacific)
all <a href="http://content.time.com/time/covers/europe/0,16641,20130916,00.html">
declare</a> “America's weak and waffling, Russia's rich and
resurgent.” In the U.S, <em>Time</em>'s cover <a href="http://content.time.com/time/magazine/0,9263,7601130916,00.html">states</a>,
“It's Time To Pay College Athletes.” The Putin story is tucked in
the corner and neutrally retitled, “What Putin Wants.”</p>
<p>Neil Munro of the Daily Caller <a href="http://dailycaller.com/2013/09/16/time-mag-hides-putins-success-from-u-s-voters/">
insists</a> that the magazine is deliberately “shielding Americans
from the demoralizing picture... safely overlook[ing] a widely
perceived fumble by President Barack Obama that left Russia to
carry the ball in the Syrian war.”</p>
<p><em>The Atlantic</em>'s Philip Bump sarcastically <a href="http://www.theatlanticwire.com/politics/2013/09/time-magazine-hiding-lot-stuff-lot-countries-apparently/69450/">
questions</a>, “Or maybe it was trying to shield the rest of the
world from the divisive issue of whether or not we should pay
college athletes (which we presume is also Obama's fault)?” The
article goes on to defend the practice: “<em><em>Time</em></em>, an
American magazine, has America-specific features all the time...
This isn't a conspiracy; it's marketing.”</p>
<p>Yet, the article about Putin is America-specific in its
subtitle, and mentions the U.S. <a href="http://content.time.com/time/magazine/article/0,9171,2151148,00.html">
another four times</a> in the first three paragraphs.</p>
<p><em>Bump</em><em> </em>is correct in that the international
editions pay no heed to American football. However, they do feature
an <a href="http://content.time.com/time/magazine/article/0,9171,2151174,00.html">
article</a> about China's space program that is not even in the
American magazine. The article, “Under a Chinese Moon,” is quick to
reference the U.S. The subtitle states, “Beijing's commitment to
manned space flight is a reminder of what the U.S. could once do.”
The piece begins with a harsh take on the U.S.:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Want to hear a piece of utter non-news? The U.S. is sending an
unmanned spacecraft to the moon this month. Want to hear a piece of
very big news? China is sending an unmanned spacecraft to the moon
this year. That expedition is huge, even cosmically game changing.
More to the point, it's perfectly legit to respond to such similar
missions in such dissimilar ways.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>As BuzzFeed <a href="http://www.buzzfeed.com/hunterschwarz/time-magazine-covers-in-the-us-vs-the-rest-of-the-world">
acknowledges</a>, covers are typically the same across the board.
However, the site presents comparisons of various editions that
inexplicably leave American readers out of the discussion of
hot-button issues, even ones that directly involve the U.S. These
include covers that <a href="http://content.time.com/time/magazine/europe/0,9263,901111024,00.html">
read</a>, “Why the U.S. Will Never Save Afghanistan,” and describe
the “squandered hope” of America's military efforts. Others ranging
in coverage from China's industrial power, to Europe's economic
stability, and civil unrest in Pakistan are replaced with
lighthearted images like school children and house pets for the
U.S. audience. </p>	
	</div>
</div>
