  <h2 class="post-title">
    Salt Lake City: How a Remote Red-State City Became a Transit Leader  </h2>
  <p class="post-author">
  by <a href="http://dc.streetsblog.org/author/angie-schmitt/" title="Posts by Angie Schmitt">Angie Schmitt</a>  </p>
  <div class="post-entry">

 
            <p><div id="attachment_140112" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 591px"><a href="http://dc.streetsblog.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/salt-lake-city-light-rail-flickr.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-140112   " title="salt-lake-city-light-rail-flickr" src="http://dc.streetsblog.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/salt-lake-city-light-rail-flickr.jpg" alt="" width="581" height="385" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Salt Lake City&#39;s transit system is the envy of cities five times its size, and it&#39;s all because planners listened to what the public wanted. Image: <a href="http://www.smartgrowthamerica.org/2011/10/21/coordinating-housing-and-transit-plans-in-salt-lake-city/">Smart Growth America</a></p></div></p>
<p>It&#8217;s number one in the nation in per-capita transit spending. The only city in the country building light rail, bus rapid transit, streetcars and commuter rail at the same time. And that city &#8212; Salt Lake City &#8212; is a town of just over 180,000 in a remote setting in a red state.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s a remarkable story that began in the 1990s, when an organization called Envision Utah facilitated a regional visioning process and created a plan that has been recognized as one of the most promising smart growth models in the nation.</p>
<p>There&#8217;s a lesson here for other cities. In 1997, leaders in a 10-county region centered on Salt Lake County set out to see what people valued about where they lived. They designed a plan around those values, with a communications campaign to support it. At that time, the state was expected to grow by a million people by 2020. Rather than cede that growth to meandering sprawl, the region chose something more orderly and compact.</p>
<p>&#8220;At that point, to many Utahns, &#8216;smart growth&#8217; was not a popular word,&#8221; said Robert Grow, Envision Utah&#8217;s president and CEO. &#8220;We made people some promises. We’d save a lot of time, money, lower emissions, improve air quality, develop more housing choices, and build a transportation system with greater efficiency.&#8221;</p>
<p>The organization interviewed 150 key stakeholders &#8212; elected officials, activists, heads of major institutions. And they surveyed some 20,000 Utahns about their hopes and wishes. Leaders even engaged in an effort at &#8220;value mapping,&#8221; to get a sense for local priorities and deeply held beliefs as they related to land use and transportation.</p>
<p>Project leaders discovered Utahns liked the idea of transit more than they expected. The Envision Utah effort began shortly after the first light rail track was laid in the city, which had been controversial. But polling showed 88 percent of residents favored expanding the system.</p>
<p><span id="more-140081"></span></p>
<p>&#8220;There wasn’t much energy to go beyond that [first line] until this public effort about how we could build a real system,&#8221; Grow said at a session at the Congress for New Urbanism conference, held last week in Salt Lake City.</p>
<p>As a result of the feedback, planners added 300 miles of light rail to regional plans and began assembling the right-of-way. Now 70 percent of the region&#8217;s population lives within three miles of a light rail stop. A recent study found the Salt Lake City transit system offers <a href="http://www.sltrib.com/sltrib/news/54470965-78/transit-percent-area-jobs.html.csp">better job access</a> than that of any other city in the country. Regional leaders also point to studies that show local economic growth has been more equitable than elsewhere in the U.S., with gains across many income levels. The region even exceeded federal air quality standards. (Although stricter standards have since knocked the area back out of attainment, leaders like Grow say it will soon meet them.)</p>
<p>Once Salt Lake City leaders knew what people wanted, they embarked on a public relations campaign. Dee Allsop of the communications firm Heart and Mind Strategies led the effort.</p>
<p>&#8220;How is it that the most conservative state&#8230; how is it they’re one of the most progressive in the country on transit?&#8221; said Allsop. &#8220;It’s because the case was made in a way that fit with people’s values.&#8221;</p>
<p>The values they settled on were to have a city that was &#8220;beautiful, prosperous and neighborly.&#8221; This spot is an example of Envision Utah messaging.</p>
<p><center><iframe src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/WSNPfQOuCkQ" frameborder="0" width="420" height="315"></iframe></center>The land use plan developed by Envision Utah was voluntary, with no way to enforce recommendations &#8212; but it seems to have done the trick. The group was able to show that density had a $15 billion public infrastructure benefit over sprawling growth. That spoke loudly to state and local leaders, Grow and Allsop say. Smart growth principles were also the most fiscally responsible.</p>
<p>“To be sustainable,&#8221; Allsop said, &#8220;a region must satisfy the values, hope and dreams of present and future residents.”</p>

            <div id="dsq-comment-message-433263" class="dsq-comment-message"><p>I lived in Salt Lake City for nearly 20 years, and I can tell you first hand that the public transit system is a spectacular model of success and urban planning. Even when I lived nearly 19 miles outside of downtown, I could drive to the end of the light rail line (3 miles) and commute inexpensively and very conveniently. In the winter, my travel time was often shorter than if I&#8217;d driven myself on I-15. </p>
<p>There are a couple details that need to be clarified in this story however. First, as mentioned by other posters, the metro Salt Lake area is roughly 1.2 million people, with a staggering 16% growth rate. This casts a much different picture than &#8220;a remote setting in a red state&#8221; with 180,000 people. </p>
<p>Second, much of the unrelenting effort to bring in light rail, expanded bus service and the other public transportation upgrades came from the mayors of Salt Lake City and Salt Lake County. Salt Lake City has had a Democrat for mayor since 1976. Their mayor from 2000-2008 (Ross &#8220;Rocky&#8221; Anderson) went on to mount a third-party presidential run and is recognized as a global authority on both environmental issues and human rights. Salt Lake County has been slightly more balanced, having had a Democrat as mayor for the last 9 years, and a Republican for the first 4 years after they converted from a county council model to a mayoral model. </p>
<p>Without doing an exhaustive deconstruction of the region&#8217;s history with regard to public transportation, I can agree that it is a national leader, and a role model, for many other communities to follow, both small and large, in part due to Envision Utah. Beyond that, though, the premises of the article are far off base.</p>
