                <h1 class="blogtitle">Apollo legend on NASA: &#8216;It’s a tragedy. It really is.&#8217;</h1>
				
				<div class="post-info">
								<span class="post-date">Tuesday, September 3, 2013</span>
							
			</div>
				
                <div class="entry-content blogentrytext">

</script></li><li class="share-twitter"><div class="twitter_button"><iframe allowtransparency="true" frameborder="0" scrolling="no" src="http://platform.twitter.com/widgets/tweet_button.html?url=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.chron.com%2Fsciguy%2F2013%2F09%2Fapollo-legend-on-nasa-its-a-tragedy-it-really-is%2F&#038;counturl=http%3A%2F%2Fblog.chron.com%2Fsciguy%2F2013%2F09%2Fapollo-legend-on-nasa-its-a-tragedy-it-really-is%2F&#038;count=horizontal&#038;text=Apollo%20legend%20on%20NASA%3A%20%27It%E2%80%99s%20a%20tragedy.%20It%20really%20is.%27%3A" style="width:86px; height:20px;"></iframe></div></li><li class="share-google-plus-1"><div class="googleplus1_button"><div class="g-plusone" data-size="medium" data-callback="sharing_plusone" data-href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/2013/09/apollo-legend-on-nasa-its-a-tragedy-it-really-is/"></div></div></li><li class="share-end"></li></ul></div></div></div><p dir="ltr"><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft,_Jr."><strong>Chris Kraft</strong></a> did it all for NASA. He was the space agency&#8217;s first flight director, overseeing the nation&#8217;s first human spaceflight, first human orbital flight and first spacewalk. He was a senior manager and planner during the Apollo program. After the Apollo program he directed Johnson Space Center for a decade and oversaw development of the space shuttle. Mission Control at Johnson Space Center is named after him.</p>
<p dir="ltr">In short, Kraft is a legend, and therefore not afraid to speak his mind.</p>
<p dir="ltr">Last week I visited Kraft at his home for a long talk about the state of NASA, the agency for which he has devoted his life. He&#8217;s deeply troubled about NASA&#8217;s future. Among the topics we discuss:</p>
<ul>
<li>Expense and sustainability of the Space Launch System</li>
<li>The popularity of NASA&#8217;s proposed asteroid mission</li>
<li>Whether astronauts like flying to the International Space Station</li>
<li>Where humans should go in the solar system</li>
</ul>
<p dir="ltr">I think it&#8217;s safe to say these are viewpoints NASA officials aren&#8217;t sharing with President Obama&#8217;s administration nor the members of Congress. But having spoken to a number of current NASA officials and even astronauts, many of them will privately express these views as well. Here, then, is the interview:</p>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>I know you don’t like NASA’s approach with the Space Launch System. Why is this?</strong></p>
<p>The necessity of having a big rocket is not justifiable. It’s not justifiable from a cost point of view, and it’s not justifiable from a mission plan point of view. It just doesn’t make good sense.</p>
<p><strong>Let&#8217;s start with cost. I know <a href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/2013/08/the-congressional-debate-over-nasas-asteroid-capture-mission-ignores-the-agenys-real-spaceflight-problem/">it&#8217;s expensive</a>.</strong></p>
<p>The problem with the SLS is that it’s so big that makes it very expensive. It’s very expensive to design, it’s very expensive to develop. When they actually start to develop it, the budget is going to go haywire. They’re going to have all kinds of technical and development issues crop up, which will drive the development costs up. Then there are the operating costs of that beast, which will eat NASA alive if they get there. They’re not going to be able to fly it more than once a year, if that. Because they don’t have the budget to do it. So what you’ve got is a beast of a rocket, that would give you all of this capability, which you can’t build because you don’t have the money to build it in the first place, and you can’t operate it if you had it.</p>
<div id="attachment_19461" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 610px"><a href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/wp-content/blogs.dir/1898/files/2013/08/SaturnVceremony_SU.01.jpg"><img class="size-large wp-image-19461" alt="Kraft, at Johnson Space Center, in 2007 in front of a Saturn V rocket. (Steve Ueckert/Chronicle)" src="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/wp-content/blogs.dir/1898/files/2013/08/SaturnVceremony_SU.01-600x470.jpg" width="600" height="470" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Kraft, at Johnson Space Center, in 2007 in front of a Saturn V rocket. (Steve Ueckert/Chronicle)</p></div>
<p><strong>What is the alternative?</strong></p>
<p>In the private sector we’ve got an Atlas and a Delta rocket, and the Europeans have an Ariane rocket. The Russians have lots of rockets which are very reliable, and they get reliable by using them. And that’s something the SLS will never have. Never. Because you can’t afford to launch it that many times.</p>
<p><strong>This is the approach by which NASA would save a lot of money by using existing rockets, rather than developing its own?</strong></p>
<p>What’s so magic about this being able to lift 120 tons? Why can’t you use what you’ve got and put your vehicles into space in pieces, like you did with the space station? That’s the right way to do it. Eventually you’ll get to the point where, even with a really big rocket, you get to the point where you can’t put everything on there you need to go where you want to go. Whether you want to go to the moon or Mars, you’re going to have to do something in Earth orbit, or maybe lunar orbit, with an assembly capability, a fuel depot capability and the capability to have people operating there sort of as a Cape Canaveral in the sky. Everybody that looks into this comes to the same conclusion. JSC, if you ask them how to do it, is going to tell you the way to do it is with an assembly point in orbit, with fuel depots, where you come together and then go.</p>
<p><strong>In the glory days of NASA you had this huge rocket, the Saturn V. It flew astronauts to the surface of the moon seven times. And after that NASA could no longer afford it. Are we repeating history?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">Yes. We’re heading back to that model, only worse. That’s the thing. We’ve got those smarts, we’ve learned that, we have that institutional knowledge, and yet we’re ignoring it. It doesn’t make sense. We’ve got this tremendous capability to put things into orbit, it already exists, and we’re not going to use it. Studies all over the country say this is the right thing to do. It’s a tragedy. It really is.</p>
<p><strong>You&#8217;re also not a fan of <a href="http://www.nasa.gov/press/2013/july/nasa-completes-first-internal-review-of-concepts-for-asteroid-redirect-mission/#.UflIDaI3vSd">NASA&#8217;s plan</a> to lasso an asteroid, bring it near the moon, and then send humans to stand upon it. Why?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">Congress is already saying what NASA is doing is wrong. They’re saying they don’t like the asteroid mission. Most in Congress want to see NASA go back to the moon. So do nearly all of the scientific and technical organizations in the world. China said last week they’re going to go to the moon. They aren’t going just because it’s there, it’s because it’s a place where you want to be from all kinds of points of view, from science, from a resource point of view. There’s no reason why you couldn’t set up a factory on the moon to build solar panels. You could provide enough electrical power on the moon from solar cells, and eventually you could supply enough power for half the people on Earth with a solar cell farm on the moon. I just think the world is going to use the moon for practical purposes. And it makes sense that it should be a worldwide project.</p>
<p><strong>You mean we should involve the Europeans in this?</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">Exactly. And I know for a fact that the Europeans have told NASA this, that they want to go to the moon, but they say they can’t because NASA won’t do it. We are captured by what NASA wants to do, they say. There are many in top NASA management who would prefer to make a return to the moon as the next step for the US and the rest of the nations involved in the ISS, but are reluctant to say so because of restrictions from the present administration.</p>
<div id="attachment_19460" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 522px"><a href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/wp-content/blogs.dir/1898/files/2013/08/chris_kraft_mission_control.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-19460" alt="Kraft, directing the flight of Gemini 7 in Houston in 1965. (AP Photo)" src="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/wp-content/blogs.dir/1898/files/2013/08/chris_kraft_mission_control.jpg" width="512" height="392" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Kraft, directing the flight of Gemini 7 in Houston in 1965. (AP Photo)</p></div>
<p><strong>It seems like a lot of former astronauts, and some current ones who talk to me off the record, feel the same way.</strong></p>
<p>I talked to Neil Armstrong an awful lot near the end of this life. Too bad he’s gone, because he was an important spokesman for being adverse to what the political part of NASA says we’re going to do. Bolden, let’s face it, he doesn’t know what it takes to do a major project. He doesn’t have experience with that. He’s a flier, a Marine general. He’s never known what it takes to do a massive program. He keeps talking about going to Mars in the 2030s, but that’s pure, unadulterated, BS. And what have you got if you get there? Who wants to operate something that’s 40 minutes, by voice, from the Earth. Why would you want to do that? As an operator, damned if I like that. If I’m on the moon, I&#8217;ve got a 3 second turnaround. Everything I go to do on Mars I’ve got to prepare to do in an automatic mode. That’s not very smart. Pretty much everything we need to do on Mars can be done robotically. We’ve already got robots there. By the time we get the capability to send humans to Mars, it might be that robots are smarter than humans anyway. I’m serious.</p>
<p><strong>We&#8217;re already seeing the impressive capabilities with robots like Curiosity.</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">Yeah. Exactly. If you think about the practical aspects of going into space, there’s no practical reason for going to Mars. But there is a practical reason for going to the moon. And furthermore, if you really want to go somewhere, get out of this solar system. Eventually that’s what you’re going to have to do. I don’t know how to do that, but we’ll figure out how to do it one day.</p>
<p><strong>A couple of weeks ago I had a pleasant morning at Johnson Space Center where they introduced eight bright, talented and accomplished <a href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/2013/08/and-the-most-awesomest-new-astronaut-is/">new astronaut candidates</a>. I couldn&#8217;t help but wonder where these men and women were going to fly to.</strong></p>
<p dir="ltr">For the last 10 years they’ve had trouble getting astronauts to go to the space station. I don’t know if you know that, but a lot of people don’t want to go to the space station. They don’t want to spend six months there. Three people have to take care of the station while three people do science. That’s three people 24 hours a day to keep the station operating. It’s not a very good job for an astronaut. Astronauts want to do something that has some excitement to it. The engineers that come to JSC want to do something. You go talk to the guys who were doing Constellation, and the reason they came to NASA was to go back to the moon. They’re all leaving now. The leaders are leaving for a lot of other reasons also, but they’re leaving because there’s no future that they want to be involved in. And that’s unfortunate. You’ve got to have a reason for people to give you their lives. Which is what I did. I gave NASA my life not because they asked me to, but because I wanted to. I had a reason. But I just don’t think that’s there now. The livelihood of the organization is not very good.</p>
<p><strong>I heard that you recently spoke with the Johnson Space Center co-op students who made the wildly popular <a href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/2013/01/students-create-nasas-most-popular-web-video-ever-chat-with-them-later-today/">NASA Johnson Style-video</a>. What did you say to them?</strong></p>
<p>I gave &#8216;em hell. When the group did the NASA Johnson Style video, when I saw that, I said I want to go and speak to those guys. And so I did. I said look, “You just spent all of this effort to make a movie, how about spending all of that effort in making a space program go? Give me your body and mind and work on those kinds of things. I thought the video was beautiful, but that’s not why you’re here.” I was very honest with them. They are the future of the program. If you can’t keep them interested, you’ve lost it.</p>
<div id="attachment_19463" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 522px"><a href="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/wp-content/blogs.dir/1898/files/2013/08/chris_kraft_space_center.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-19463" alt="Kraft (left), George Low and Robert Gilruth at mission control in 1969. (Houston Chronicle)" src="http://blog.chron.com/sciguy/wp-content/blogs.dir/1898/files/2013/08/chris_kraft_space_center.jpg" width="512" height="337" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Kraft (left), George Low and Robert Gilruth at mission control in 1969. (Houston Chronicle)</p></div>
<p dir="ltr"><strong>Speaking of NASA&#8217;s future, it seems like politically it&#8217;s expedient to have centers all over the country. But is that the most efficient way to conduct a space program?</strong></p>
<p><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Low"><strong>George Low</strong></a> (manager of the Apollo program and a NASA administrator) and myself got together in 1975, and he asked me how we could rebuild and reshape NASA to have a vibrant group of people. We knew we had five or six too many centers. We didn’t need that many. Let the Jet Propulsion Laboratory do the unmanned stuff, and have two or three other centers do manned spaceflight. That’s all NASA needed then, and needs now. But politics wouldn’t let us do that. The centers are still going today, and some are getting bigger.</p>

Soon Americans will be flying in space on American rockets and in American spacecraft. The irony is that these rockets and spacecraft will not be designed or controlled by NASA. </p>
<p>SLS will be the death of NASA if it is not cancelled. If SLS survives to completion NASA will have become a one-trick SLS pony as all its resources will have had to be re-directed to building the SLS. And then if the SLS is completed there will be no money left to fly it or to build any payloads to lunch from it. </p>
<p>The longer SLS continues, and it looks to be missing its milestones pretty regularly these days, the more marginalized NASA will become. Commercial space will be doing all the cool stuff while everyone sees NASA spinning its wheels never finishing its expensive shiny new rocket based on four-decade-old technology.</p>
<p>The SLS may never fly, but that is hardly a problem, as it&#8217;s supporters in Congress care not a whit about launching anything on the SLS. They just want to keep the pork flowing as long as possible to their constituents. They don&#8217;t care that NASA is being starved by the hungry SLS.</p>
<p>The only possible way out of this mess could come after Falcon Heavy flies next year and SpaceX announces upgrades to it&#8217;s second stage engine allowing the Falcon Heavy to lift over 70mt. When this happens there will be no need for the SLS. Support for the SLS would be untenable and it might then be cancelled. Only this could save NASA and the American space program. But I&#8217;m not holding my breath.</p>
</div>
