	<h1 class="title entry-title"><a rel="bookmark" href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/2013/09/12/how-did-odd-lobster-get-six-claws/">How Did Odd Lobster Get Six Claws?</a></h1>
	<div class="byline vcard author">
		<img alt='' src="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/08/lizphoto-58x58.jpg" class='avatar avatar-58 photo' height='58' width='58' />Posted by <span class="fn"><a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/author/llangley/" title="Posts by Liz Langley">Liz Langley</a></span> in <a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/blog/weird-wild/">Weird &amp; Wild</a> on <abbr class="published" title="2013-09-12T17:08:14+00:00">September 12, 2013</abbr> 	</div><!--/byline-->

	<div class="addthis_toolbox addthis_default_style" addthis:url="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/2013/09/12/how-did-odd-lobster-get-six-claws/" addthis:title="How Did Odd Lobster Get Six Claws?">

</div>	<div class="main">
		<div class="content entry-content">
			<p><strong>We dare you to high five this <a href="http://animals.nationalgeographic.com/animals/invertebrates/lobster/">lobster</a>.</strong></p>
<p>Amazingly, you could. Lola, a 4-pound (1.8-kilogram) lobster with five claws on her left side, was caught off Hyannis, Massachusetts, last week by the F/V<a href="http://www.rleah.com/rleah.com/R.L..html"><em> Rachel Leah</em></a> (from the TV show <a href="http://dsc.discovery.com/tv-shows/other-shows/videos/lobster-wars-battle-begins-clip-1.htm"><em>Lobster Wars</em></a>) and donated to <a href="http://www.maine.gov/dmr/rm/aquarium/">Maine State Aquarium</a> in Boothbay Harbor.</p>
<div id="attachment_108093" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 610px"><a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/09/lola-lobster-2.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-108093" alt="lobster picture" src="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/09/lola-lobster-2-600x480.jpg" width="600" height="480" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Lola was caught last week off Massachusetts. Photograph courtesy Maine State Aquarium</p></div>
<p><a href="http://www.lobsters.org/ldoc/ldocpage.php?did=435">Claw abnormalities</a> occur when a set of regulatory genes that dictate growth give the wrong signal. They also sometimes occur when a lobster regenerates a claw after losing one, experts say.</p>
<p>The multiclawed crustacean got to meet the public briefly before being pulled off display due to health concerns, aquarium manager Aimee Hayden-Roderiques told National Geographic.</p>
<p>Aquarium staff believe that Lola was in the molting process at the time she was caught and was acclimating to the aquarium system, she said.</p>
<p>&#8220;It&#8217;s standard protocol for us to pull any potentially molting lobster off exhibit for their protection.&#8221;</p>
<p>After they shed their exoskeleton, lobsters &#8220;become this soft, water balloon-version of a lobster,&#8221; and then hide until they regrow their shell, she said. A lot of them eat their old shell to get the calcium necessary to regrow a new one. &#8220;We definitely have to keep them separated until they harden enough to be exhibit-worthy again.&#8221;</p>
<div id="attachment_108092" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 610px"><a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/09/lola-lobster.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-108092" alt="lobster picture" src="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/files/2013/09/lola-lobster-600x450.jpg" width="600" height="450" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Lola&#8217;s claws are likely due to a genetic glitch. Photograph courtesy Maine State Aquarium</p></div>
<p>Hayden-Roderiques added there&#8217;s no guarantee that Lola will regrow her unique claw after the molting process. (Also see <a href="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2006/07/060720-lobster-photo.html">photo: &#8220;Lobster Caught ‘Half-Cooked’ in Maine</a>.&#8221;)</p>
<p><strong>An Oddity Among Oddities</strong></p>
<p>Though a six-clawed lobster is an oddity, Lola will be among her own kind there.</p>
<p>That&#8217;s because Maine State Aquarium seems to be <i>the</i> place for <a href="http://maine.gov/dmr/rm/aquarium/exhibits.htm">unique lobsters</a>. The aquarium houses lobsters with a variety of color variations like orange, blue, and calico, as well as a 20-pounder (we assume that one gets lots of dinner invites) and several other lobsters with unique claw mutations. (<a href="http://newswatch.nationalgeographic.com/2012/07/10/weird-wild-odd-colored-lobsters-decoded/">Read about a bright-blue lobster caught in 2012</a>.)</p>
<p>But Lola is a catch.  Even the aquarium’s seasoned marine scientist, David Libby from the Maine Department of Marine Resources, was so impressed by the six claws that he told the <a href="http://bangordailynews.com/2013/09/06/news/state/lola-the-six-clawed-lobster-finds-new-home-at-maine-state-aquarium/">Bangor Daily News</a> that his 40 years of experience with ocean creatures was “apparently not enough.”</p>
<p>What&#8217;s more, Lola has to be the friendliest lobster in world… Well, at least she always looks like she’s waving.</p>
