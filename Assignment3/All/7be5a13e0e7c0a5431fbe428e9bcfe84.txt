<div id="article_story_body" class="article story"><div class="articlePage"><ul class="socialByline">By <li>BEN ZIMMER</li>
            </ul><p>This week, <a href="/public/quotes/main.html?type=djn&amp;symbol=AAPL" class="companyRollover link11unvisited">Apple</a>  
<span data-widget="dj.ticker" data-ticker-name="AAPL"></span>
 rolled out its new iPhone 5C, a cheaper and more colorful alternative to the top-of-the-line 5S model. In the product video, Apple's head of design  <a href="http://topics.wsj.com/person/I/Jonathan-Ive/6755" class="topicLink">Jonathan Ive</a> explained that despite having the guts of the old iPhone 5, the 5C combines a steel skeleton and a seamless plastic exterior, "creating a bespoke assembly."</p><p>Having the dulcet-toned, British-born Mr. Ive introduce the 5C with phrases like "bespoke assembly" went a long way to assuage those who might see the model as nothing more than a repackaging of old goods in a colored plastic shell. "Bespoke," in particular, might encourage consumers to think that the "C" in "5C" stands for "classy" rather than "cheap." (More likely the "C" is intended to suggest "color" or "choice.")</p>
<a name="U100311169604IKE"></a><p>"Bespoke" means "custom-made," but it sounds much posher. Originally referring to men's clothing, particularly tailored suits, "bespoke" has been expanding its purview in recent years. Nowadays, you're just as likely to hear "bespoke" as a label for a luxury car, an audio system, a piece of jewelry or even high-end baked goods. In tech circles, "bespoke" describes a custom-designed approach to software development.</p>
<p>All of this semantic dilution no doubt irks the purveyors of bespoke tailoring on London's Savile Row—especially since "bespoke" no longer means what it used to. Traditionally, bespoke tailoring required clothes to be cut and sewn manually. But in 2008, the U.K.'s Advertising Standards Authority decreed that even machine-made suits could be called "bespoke" if  they were patterned for individual customers and not "off-the-rack."</p>
<a name="U10031116960429E"></a><p>The niceties of handmade couture have not concerned those who use "bespoke" for an array of fancy consumer goods. Especially in the U.S., "bespoke" simply conveys prestige, in no small part because it sounds so British. Ben Yagoda, an English professor at the University of Delaware, has included "bespoke" on his website "Not One-Off Britishisms," which catalogs creeping British influences on the American tongue. Jesse Sheidlower, president of the American Dialect Society, told the BBC last year that Americans who conspicuously use "bespoke" are just "showing off."</p>
<p>Ironically enough, "bespoke" has a less-than-posh etymological pedigree. It was formed from the verb "bespeak," which evolved from its original meaning of "speak up" and came to mean "engage someone to do a job" or "give an order to make goods." But to fashion an adjective out of it, the past participle "bespoken" would have been more proper than the simple past-tense "bespoke." Still, by the 18th century, "bespoke" had won out—to convey, today, an air of elegance to a plastic smartphone.</p>
