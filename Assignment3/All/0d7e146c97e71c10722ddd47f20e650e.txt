			<h2>Finding&nbsp;Collisions</h2>

			<div class="entry">
				<p>Some time ago, a friend was trying to find an efficient way (storage and time complexity) to find collisions for a (secure) hash function. Hashing random keys until you get a collision is reminiscent of von Mises&#8217; <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birthday_problem" target="_blank">birthday paradox</a>.</p>
<p><a href="http://hbfs.files.wordpress.com/2010/08/cake-2.png"><img src="http://hbfs.files.wordpress.com/2010/08/cake-2.png?w=450" alt="" title="cake-2"   class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-2745" /></a></p>
<p>In is simplest form, the birthday paradox states that, amongst (randomly) gathered people, the probability that (at least) two share a birthday increases counter-intuitively fast with the number of gathered people. This mean that even if your hash function is random (i.e., strong), you may not have to try a very large number of random keys before finding collisions. Or does it?</p>
<p><span id="more-4061"></span></p>
<p>Let us forget about people. Let us consider hash functions. Suppose we have <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d' title='d' class='latex' />, the number of possible values the hash will take. Typically, we have a large number, say <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d%3D2%5E%7B128%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d=2^{128}' title='d=2^{128}' class='latex' />. Let <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' /> be the number of keys to hash. The probability of <em>not</em> having a collision after <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' /> hashes is:</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+Q%28n%2Cd%29%3D%5Cfrac%7Bd%7D%7Bd%7D+%5Cfrac%7Bd-1%7D%7Bd%7D%5Cfrac%7Bd-2%7D%7Bd%7D+%5Ccdots+%5Cfrac%7Bd-%28n-2%29%7D%7Bd%7D+%5Cfrac%7Bd-%28n-1%29%7D%7Bd%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7Bd%21%7D%7B%28d-n%29%21d%5En%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;displaystyle Q(n,d)=&#92;frac{d}{d} &#92;frac{d-1}{d}&#92;frac{d-2}{d} &#92;cdots &#92;frac{d-(n-2)}{d} &#92;frac{d-(n-1)}{d} = &#92;frac{d!}{(d-n)!d^n}' title='&#92;displaystyle Q(n,d)=&#92;frac{d}{d} &#92;frac{d-1}{d}&#92;frac{d-2}{d} &#92;cdots &#92;frac{d-(n-2)}{d} &#92;frac{d-(n-1)}{d} = &#92;frac{d!}{(d-n)!d^n}' class='latex' /></p>
<p>(Because at first, you have zero items drawn so far, and there are <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d' title='d' class='latex' /> hashes still free, thus the probability is <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d%2Fd%3D1&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d/d=1' title='d/d=1' class='latex' /> of not having a collision. Then you draw one, and have <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28d-1%29%2Fd&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='(d-1)/d' title='(d-1)/d' class='latex' /> chances of not having a collision, since <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d-1&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d-1' title='d-1' class='latex' /> are still available, and so on until you draw the <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' />th hash, and you have <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d-%28n-1%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d-(n-1)' title='d-(n-1)' class='latex' /> keys already drawn from a possible set of <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d' title='d' class='latex' />, giving you a chance of <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28d-%28n-1%29%29%2Fd&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='(d-(n-1))/d' title='(d-(n-1))/d' class='latex' /> of not having a collision.) And therefore, the probability of <em>having</em> a collision is simply:</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=P%28n%2Cd%29%3D1-Q%28n%2Cd%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='P(n,d)=1-Q(n,d)' title='P(n,d)=1-Q(n,d)' class='latex' />.</p>
<p>Whenever <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d' title='d' class='latex' /> and <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' /> are largish, we can safely approximate using:</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+P%28n%2Cd%29%5Capprox%7B%7D1-e%5E%7B-%5Cfrac%7Bn%28n-1%29%7D%7B2d%7D%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;displaystyle P(n,d)&#92;approx{}1-e^{-&#92;frac{n(n-1)}{2d}}' title='&#92;displaystyle P(n,d)&#92;approx{}1-e^{-&#92;frac{n(n-1)}{2d}}' class='latex' />.</p>
<p>So, knowing <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d' title='d' class='latex' /> (for example <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d%3D2%5E%7B128%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d=2^{128}' title='d=2^{128}' class='latex' />), how large does <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' /> have to be to have a collision? OK, let us say only to have a probability <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;alpha' title='&#92;alpha' class='latex' /> of collision?</p>
<p>Well, we just solve</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=P%28n%2Cd%29%3D%5Calpha&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='P(n,d)=&#92;alpha' title='P(n,d)=&#92;alpha' class='latex' /></p>
<p>for <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' />.</p>
<p>After a bit of pain, we find</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+n%3D%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%5Csqrt%7B1-8d%5Cln%281-%5Calpha%29%7D%5Cright%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;displaystyle n=&#92;frac{1}{2}&#92;left(&#92;sqrt{1-8d&#92;ln(1-&#92;alpha)}&#92;right)' title='&#92;displaystyle n=&#92;frac{1}{2}&#92;left(&#92;sqrt{1-8d&#92;ln(1-&#92;alpha)}&#92;right)' class='latex' />.</p>
<p align="center">*<br />*&emsp;*</p>
<p>OK, let&#8217;s say now that we want a 50% chance of finding a collision, how large should <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' /> be? Well, now we have <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%3D0.5&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;alpha=0.5' title='&#92;alpha=0.5' class='latex' />, <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d%3D2%5E%7B128%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d=2^{128}' title='d=2^{128}' class='latex' />, and we solve for <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n' title='n' class='latex' />, finding that</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+n%3D%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%5Csqrt%7B1-8d%5Cln%281-%5Calpha%29%7D%5Cright%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;displaystyle n=&#92;frac{1}{2}&#92;left(&#92;sqrt{1-8d&#92;ln(1-&#92;alpha)}&#92;right)' title='&#92;displaystyle n=&#92;frac{1}{2}&#92;left(&#92;sqrt{1-8d&#92;ln(1-&#92;alpha)}&#92;right)' class='latex' /></p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+n%3D%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D%5Cleft%28%5Csqrt%7B1-8%282%5E%7B128%7D%29%5Cln%28%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D%29%7D%5Cright%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;displaystyle n=&#92;frac{1}{2}&#92;left(&#92;sqrt{1-8(2^{128})&#92;ln(&#92;frac{1}{2})}&#92;right)' title='&#92;displaystyle n=&#92;frac{1}{2}&#92;left(&#92;sqrt{1-8(2^{128})&#92;ln(&#92;frac{1}{2})}&#92;right)' class='latex' /></p>
<p>and finally</p>
<p align="center"><img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+n%5Capprox%7B%7D2.17%5Ctimes%7B%7D10%5E%7B19%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;displaystyle n&#92;approx{}2.17&#92;times{}10^{19}' title='&#92;displaystyle n&#92;approx{}2.17&#92;times{}10^{19}' class='latex' />.</p>
<p>We observe that <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n%5Capprox+1.17+%5Csqrt%7Bd%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='n&#92;approx 1.17 &#92;sqrt{d}' title='n&#92;approx 1.17 &#92;sqrt{d}' class='latex' /> (for <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%3D%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;alpha=&#92;frac{1}{2}' title='&#92;alpha=&#92;frac{1}{2}' class='latex' />, anyways).</p>
<p align="center">*<br />*&emsp;*</p>
<p>Therefore, before reasonably expecting a collision with probability <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%3D%5Cfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='&#92;alpha=&#92;frac{1}{2}' title='&#92;alpha=&#92;frac{1}{2}' class='latex' /> using <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d%3D2%5E%7B128%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d=2^{128}' title='d=2^{128}' class='latex' />, we would have to hash about <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=2.17%5Ctimes%7B%7D10%5E%7B19%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='2.17&#92;times{}10^{19}' title='2.17&#92;times{}10^{19}' class='latex' /> keys, which&#8230; may take a while.</p>

		<p>Interesting.</p>
<p>This assumes, of course, that hash values are truly random over 128-bit. That&#8217;s a strong requirement.</p>
<p>A slightly more realistic requirement would be to assume 32-bit hash values (as in, for example, Java). Your formula then gives me 77163. Not quite as good, right?</p>
<p>But what about truly  random hashing?</p>
<p>I would argue that there is no such thing in practice. For example, the best we ever do in actual software over strings is to have pairwise independence  (i.e., strong universality). For a mathematical justification, see&#8230;</p>
<p>Daniel Lemire, The universality of iterated hashing over variable-length strings, Discrete Applied Mathematics 160 (4-5), 2012.<br />
<a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1008.1715" rel="nofollow">http://arxiv.org/abs/1008.1715</a></p>
<p>and</p>
<p>Daniel Lemire and Owen Kaser, Recursive n-gram hashing is pairwise independent, at best, Computer Speech &amp; Language 24 (4), pages 698-710, 2010.<br />
<a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/0705.4676" rel="nofollow">http://arxiv.org/abs/0705.4676</a></p>
<p>Formally speaking, some people get 4-wise and 5-wise independence using tabulation, but I would argue that just getting &#8220;random hashing&#8221; (at all!) in software is quite something. I haven&#8217;t seen tabulation hashing ever used&#8230;</p>
<p>The Ruby and Perl languages do have random hashing&#8230; but that is about it. </p>
<p>Most software uses deterministic hashing and, in that case, the probability of having a collision is either 1 or 0. </p>
<p>Final plug: Strongly universal hashing over strings is quite fast&#8230; see  <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1202.4961" rel="nofollow">http://arxiv.org/abs/1202.4961</a> But it is inconvenient because&#8230; well, you need to read my paper to see the downside. ;-)</p>

But that&#8217;s intuitive in some sense: you would&#8217;nt be surprised to need very few trials to get collisions on, say, 4 bits, even though you throw a perfectly random dice to get your bits. If you have a very small number of bits, collisions are easy to understand. It&#8217;s when the number of bits grows that the result counter-intuitive. You kind of expect the number of trials needed to get a collision to be somewhat linear in <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=d&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='d' title='d' class='latex' /> and instead it&#8217;s <img src='http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=O%28%5Csqrt%28d%29%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=333333&amp;s=0' alt='O(&#92;sqrt(d))' title='O(&#92;sqrt(d))' class='latex' />.
