					<article class="post" id="post_43088488614">

						<div class="sharePopOver">
              <a class="facebook icon sprite" href="http://facebook.com/sharer.php?u=http%3A%2F%2Flilly.tumblr.com%2Fpost%2F43088488614%2Fa-few-folks-have-asked-me-what-i-think-of-the-news&amp;t=" target="_blank"><span>Share</span></a>
              <a class="twitter icon sprite" href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?text=A%20few%20folks%20have%20asked%20me%20what%20I%20think%20of%20the%20news%20yesterday%20that%20Opera%20is%20moving%20away%20from%20their%20own...%20http%3A%2F%2Ftmblr.co%2FZUAZbye8HYYc" target="_blank"><span>Tweet</span></a>
						</div>

						
							
							<p>A few folks have asked me what I think of the news yesterday that Opera is moving away from their own technology stack to build on top of WebKit going forward. </p>
<p>From Opera&#8217;s perspective as a business, I think they probably had to do this. They&#8217;ve got a much larger global footprint than most people know &#8212; hundreds of millions of users, mostly on mobile &#8212; but the lion&#8217;s share of that are on devices that I don&#8217;t think matter much in the future &#8212; they&#8217;re dumb phones, not smartphones like Android or iOS. Apple&#8217;s restrictions mean that as long as they&#8217;re using their own renderer, they couldn&#8217;t be on iOS. Android is possible, for sure, but they&#8217;d have to decide whether to only be on Android or support two different rendering technologies for Android &amp; iOS. Then you&#8217;re left with what is a very existential question for a browser maker: is your browser all about compatibility of site from platform to platform? Or just about the UX? (FWIW, everyone will answer this question somewhat differently &#8212; your mileage may vary.)</p>
<p>So from a tech stack perspective, given their history, I think Opera just decided they needed something more coherent, and more compatible with now-dominant WebKit (which is, obviously, an extremely good piece of technology and open source phenomenon overall).</p>
<p>From a broader web perspective, I don&#8217;t think it matters a lot that Opera is moving. It matters on the margins for sure &#8212; they&#8217;ve always had smart, dedicated people, and have been a distinctive and differentiated voice that&#8217;s made the overall web stronger as it&#8217;s evolved. But the consumer market has never really much cared about Opera overall despite their technical excellence. </p>
<p>So it doesn&#8217;t matter much for the overall web, in my view.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m seeing many arguments (from very smart people) that extrapolating from this, we should applaud a shrinking of the number of distinct rendering engines, we should all move to WebKit. </p>
<p>I don&#8217;t think that argument is a good one, though. Obviously I have a point of view that&#8217;s heavily influenced by my time at Mozilla, but now, in 2013, as a personal matter, I&#8217;m not particularly attached to any specific web engine &#8212; I think we have 2 or 3 very strong options, wrapped up with an increasing number of interesting user interfaces on mobile &amp; tablet.</p>
<p>But what I have learned being around the Mozilla folks is that technologies always, always, always have arcs. Seems obvious. But decisions that seem incredibly clear in the near term &#8212; say a period of 3 or 4 years &#8212; don&#8217;t always seem so clear several years later. The team at Mozilla has been around the web since pretty close to inception. They&#8217;ve seen the rise of the web and HTML &amp; JS. They&#8217;ve seen rises &amp; falls in market share of several different browsers. And they&#8217;ve seen the rises and falls of: Java, Flash, IE&#8217;s Trident. </p>
<p>What we know for sure is this: monocultures always make more &amp; faster progress in the near term when they&#8217;re stewarded by strong, vibrant leaders. But over time you get stuck. Companies change, sensibilities change. And then you&#8217;ve got all the technology, and all talent, and all of the best thinkers, all trapped on one technology stack.</p>
<p>The argument with WebKit is that since it&#8217;s open source, you can always fork it, take it over when the stewarding organization becomes indifferent or incapable, of moving it forward. </p>
<p>To that I would say &#8220;maybe.&#8221;</p>
<p>We don&#8217;t really know for sure. We don&#8217;t really know how this will play out over the next few years, especially as our mobile platforms are verticalized and locked down. We just don&#8217;t know what we don&#8217;t know about how business and technology pressures will change the strategies of the most involved companies, and how that will exert pressure on the web.</p>
<p>What we do know is that in technology, we&#8217;ve never been served well by monocultures &#8212; we know this for sure. I worry that in our desire for clearer definition, easier standards, faster progress, we&#8217;re forgetting that we know this. Same as it ever was, I suppose.</p>
