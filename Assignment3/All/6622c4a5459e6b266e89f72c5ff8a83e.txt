<section class='article-content'>

<p>Tina Brown will not be with <em><a href="http://mashable.com/category/the-daily-beast/">The Daily Beast</a></em> much longer.</p>
<p><em>The Daily Beast</em>'s editor-in-chief has decided to leave the publication this fall to start her own company, Tina Brown Live Media, which will expand on her three-year-old Women in the World conference series a spokesperson for Brown's new venture confirmed to <em>Mashable</em>. Brown's contract was not up until January, but Brown, apparently, has decided to leave early.</p>
<p>News that Brown was departing was first reported by <a href="http://www.buzzfeed.com/peterlauria/exclusive-iac-and-daily-beast-editor-tina-brown-to-part-ways" target="_blank"> <em>BuzzFeed</em></a>, and confirmed to <em>Mashable</em> by two sources familiar with the negotiations, one of whom added that the site is not shutting down.</p>
<div class="see-also"><p>See also: <a href="http://mashable.com/2013/04/29/barry-diller-newsweek-mistake/" data-crackerjax="#post-slider">Barry Diller Says Buying 'Newsweek' Was a Mistake</a></p></div>
<p>A spokesperson for <em>The Daily Beast</em> has not responded to a request for comment.</p>
<p><em>The Daily Beast</em> launched under Brown and IAC in fall 2008. It was, at first, like a free, web-only version of <em>Vanity Fair</em>, the Conde Nast title Brown used to edit. Brown discovered quickly that longform features weren't going to generate enough revenue to support <em>The Daily Beast</em> alone, and today it balances a mix of original reports with aggregated stories and slideshows, bringing in about 6 million unique visitors per month, per comScore. It is still not, however, profitable. According to <a href="http://www.adweek.com/news/press/following-newsbeast-mess-whats-tina-browns-next-act-152026" target="_blank"><em>Adweek</em></a>, the publication is slated to lose $12 million this year.</p>
<p>Brown's tenure at <em>The Daily Beast</em> — and later at <em>Newsweek</em>, which merged with <em>The Daily Beast</em> in late 2010 and was <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/08/04/business/media/newsweek-bought-by-digital-news-company-ibt.html" target="_blank">sold off</a> earlier this year — has been fraught with drama, both internal and external. She has famously clashed with editors and reporters, and been criticized for a series of <em>Newsweek</em> covers, like <a href="https://www.facebook.com/photo.php?fbid=10150287017801101&amp;set=a.99967331100.118431.18343191100&amp;type=1&amp;theater" target="_blank">this one</a> of a back-from-the-dead Princess Diana, for seeming "desperate."</p>
<p>The future of <em>The Daily Beast</em> is uncertain. <em>BuzzFeed</em> speculates that the publication could be sold, closed (unlikely, in my opinion, given the audience size and brand equity at stake — and our source says it won't be) or continue to run under a new editor — perhaps Executive Editor John Avlon.</p>
<p><strong>Update:</strong> A source claiming to be close to the negotiations called after we published this piece, emphasizing that Brown — not IAC — "ultimately made the decision to walk away from <em>The Daily Beast</em>" and that it's "more of a mutual separating of ways." The source added that Brown's relationship with IAC Chairman Barry Diller is "not strained" and she continues to have "a ton of respect" for Diller.</p>
